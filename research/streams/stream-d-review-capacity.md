# Stream D — Review Capacity as Part of Condition
## Cross-cutting research stream feeding Section 2 of *The Viable Coder Playbook* (Clay Turner, viablecoder.com)
 
> **What this stream is.** The Section 3 open questions flag review as possibly *the* binding constraint of the whole ADLC: if agents open pull requests faster than a human can truly review them, the **answerable back end** — not origination — is where the pipeline jams. Stream D's job is to establish what review capacity actually *is*, why it is the scarce resource now, and where it belongs in the playbook. Its load-bearing claim is the one the plan names: **review capacity is part of condition.** Section 1 already built the apparatus for that — *you are the instrument; your condition is the production input; self-report lies, so instrument the trailing signal* — and Stream F split the instrument into **climate** (stable configuration) and **weather** (day-to-day variance). Review capacity has exactly that two-part structure: a slow-built **climate** (the owned understanding you review *from*) sitting under a fast-depleting **weather** (the finite attention you review *with*). This stream cuts across the four audiences who must hold an answerable end over work they didn't produce — **SaaS Vendor Dependency, External Dev Dependency, Legacy Software Debt, and the AI Development Gap** — and it is the research backbone of the **Verification & Review Capacity** module, which the module grid already shows is default-or-borrowable in all six situations (a promotion candidate to the universal set).

## TL;DR
 
- **Review is not a quality checkpoint bolted onto authorship — it is comprehension work, and the field's own primary research says so.** Bacchelli & Bird's Microsoft study (ICSE 2013) and Sadowski et al.'s Google case study (ICSE-SEIP 2018) both found that although defect-finding is the *stated* motivation, reviews deliver less defect-catching than expected and far more knowledge transfer, shared awareness, and alternative-solution generation — and that **code-and-change understanding is the key activity of reviewing**, a need current tools mostly fail to meet. Microsoft's Czerwonka et al. went further: reviews largely *don't* catch deep logic defects; their real value is comprehension and maintainability. This reframes the whole stream. The answerable back end (Section 3, step 7) is not "scan the diff for bugs"; it is *rebuild enough of a mental model of someone else's change to stand behind it.* That is a cognitive act, and cognitive acts run on condition.
- **AWS's Werner Vogels named the precise mechanism, and it is pure Clay framing: when you author code, comprehension is free; when a machine authors it, you have to pay for that comprehension during review.** His coinage — *verification debt* — rests on the observation that comprehension comes with the act of creation, so when the machine writes the code you must *rebuild* that comprehension at review time, which takes real effort. The program-comprehension literature explains why this rebuild is expensive and slow: the thing an author has and a reviewer lacks is the **situation model** (the *why* — goals, intent, the mapping from code to purpose), which experts build over time and novices barely form (Pennington; Burkhardt, Détienne & Wiedenbeck; van Dijk–Kintsch lineage). The agent ships the *program model* (what the code does) for free; it cannot ship the *situation model* (why it's right), and that is exactly the half review depends on. Sonar's 2026 survey puts a number on the felt cost: **38% of developers say reviewing AI-generated code takes *more* effort than reviewing a human colleague's** — while 96% don't fully trust the output and only 48% always check it before committing. *(Point-in-time; cite the date.)*
- **Review capacity is a finite, depleting resource with a hard biological ceiling — which is why agent throughput now hits it like a wall.** The canonical SmartBear/Cisco inspection study (≈2,500 reviews over 3.2M lines) found defect detection peaks at roughly **200–400 lines per review and degrades sharply beyond it**, with quality falling after **60–90 minutes** of continuous review and inspection rates best kept under ~500 LOC/hour; detection rates fall from the high-80s% on small changes toward the high-20s% on 1,000+-line changes (exact percentages vary by dataset; the *direction* is robust and widely replicated). An agent has **no working-memory ceiling and never fatigues** (Augment Code's framing); a single prompt can return 600 lines that blow past the human limit in one shot. So the producible-middle acceleration loads directly onto the one input that *can't* scale — and "beyond the limit you're not reviewing, you're rubber-stamping" becomes the default failure mode. 2026 telemetry confirms the jam: Faros AI's *Acceleration Whiplash* report (22,000 developers, 4,000+ teams, two years of before/after data) found median PR review time **up ~441%**, incidents per PR **up ~243%**, code churn **up ~861%**, and roughly **31% of PRs merged with no review** under high AI adoption. *(Point-in-time; cite the date.)*
- **This is condition, with the same climate/weather structure Section 1 and Stream F established — and the same self-report failure.** The **weather of review** is finite attention and working memory: it depletes within the hour, degrades with fatigue and context-switching, and — crucially — *the depleted reviewer doesn't feel depleted*, which is the drunk-driver problem from the condition chapter pointed at the back end. The **climate of review** is the owned situation model: it can't be borrowed and it builds slowly (METR's brownfield result — experienced developers were *slower* with AI on mature repos they knew intimately, because their tacit, internalized understanding outran what the AI could supply, and reviewing the AI's "directionally correct but not exactly right" output cost more than writing it themselves). And automation complacency (Parasuraman & Manzey 2010, already a Section 1/B anchor) guarantees the human rubber-stamps unless *structurally* forced not to — a failure made worse here because AI code is **superficially convincing**, the single worst stimulus for a complacent reviewer. The gate is only real if the human has both the climate to review *from* and the weather to review *with*.

## Key Findings
 
**1. Robust / well-replicated (safe to build doctrine on):**
- *Code review is fundamentally comprehension work, not defect-scanning.* Bacchelli & Bird 2013 (Microsoft Research / ICSE; observed, interviewed, surveyed developers and hand-classified hundreds of comments): defects are the stated motive but reviews are "less about defects than expected"; the dominant value is knowledge transfer, team awareness, and alternative solutions, and **change-understanding is the key aspect of reviewing**, largely unmet by tools. Corroborated at Google (Sadowski et al. 2018: education, norm-maintenance, gatekeeping, accident-prevention) and sharpened by Czerwonka et al. (Microsoft): reviews don't reliably catch deep logic defects; value is comprehension/maintainability.
- *Review effectiveness has a finite-attention ceiling, and bigger/longer reviews detect less.* SmartBear/Cisco inspection study (≈2,500 reviews, 3.2M LOC, ~10 months): detection peaks ~200–400 LOC and ~60–90 minutes, inspection best under ~500 LOC/hour. The *direction* is replicated across many later datasets (large PRs → sharply lower detection, slower turnaround, fewer meaningful comments per added line); specific percentage curves vary by source and should be cited with their source, not as universal constants.
- *Owned understanding is non-negotiable for good review, and it's built slowly.* METR early-2025 RCT (16 experienced devs, mature repos averaging 5 years and ~1,500 commits): AI made them ~19% *slower*, and a primary mechanism was **implicit/tacit repository knowledge** the AI couldn't match, producing suggestions that were "directionally correct but not exactly what's needed" — the review-and-correct cost exceeded writing it themselves. The Feb-2026 follow-up softened the magnitude (~−4%) but not the mechanism. This is the empirical anchor for "the situation model can't be borrowed."
- *Automation complacency makes a human reviewer rubber-stamp unless structurally forced otherwise — and it hits experts too.* Parasuraman & Manzey 2010 (*Human Factors*), the canonical finding carried from Stream B / Section 1; the 2025–2026 "AI grades AI" and oversight literature shows the same deference pattern in AI-output review specifically (a human signing off often *ratifies* rather than checks).
**2. Strong but with a methodological caveat (cite with the caveat):**
- *The cognitive structure of program comprehension* — mental model built from chunks, plans, schemas, and beacons; the **program model** (what/how) vs. **situation model** (why/goals) distinction; experts hold a developed situation model that novices lack and that evolves over time while the program model stays comparatively static (Brooks; Soloway & Ehrlich; Pennington; von Mayrhauser & Vans integrated metamodel; Burkhardt, Détienne & Wiedenbeck). *Caveat:* this canon is mostly 1980s–90s, small-sample, procedural-era work. Use the **mechanism** — comprehension means constructing a situation model, which is slow and expertise-gated — not the specific experiments as proof. It explains Vogels' point at the cognitive level; it is not modern large-scale evidence.
- *Reviewing AI code reintroduces the "no shared author intent" problem that human review partly solved.* The reviewer is reconstructing intent with no author to ask, and AI output detaches the person from the work, so the usual trust heuristic ("this came from a careful colleague") fails (practitioner-strong, e.g. Swarmia; Atomic Robot's "you're reconstructing intent"). *Caveat:* practitioner/industry observation rather than controlled study — directionally consistent with the comprehension literature, cite as informed practice.
**3. Point-in-time (cite with the date; will go stale):**
- Sonar 2026 *State of Code* developer survey (~1,100 devs): 96% don't fully trust AI output / only 48% always verify / **38% say reviewing AI code takes more effort than human-written** / AI ≈ 42% of committed code, projected ~65% by 2027 / avg team uses 4 AI tools.
- Faros AI 2026 *Acceleration Whiplash* telemetry (22,000 devs, 4,000+ teams): review time +~441%, incidents/PR +~243%, churn +~861%, throughput +~34%, epics/dev +~66%, ~31% no-review merges, acceptance rates rising ~20%→~60%.
- The verification-debt vocabulary itself (Vogels, AWS re:Invent 2025; Sonar's 2026 framing; CACM Jan 2026), the vendor "review debt" coinage (Augment Code, Dec 2025), and the AI-reviewing-AI **correlated-failure / circularity** results (Zietsman 2026; Jin & Chen 2025 + 2026 follow-up).
**4. Design lesson, not measurement (flag as framing):**
- The spec-as-external-ground-truth argument: without an external reference, an AI reviewing AI-authored code reasons from the same artifact and shares the same failure distribution, so the review checks the code against itself, not against intent. Useful as the structural rationale for why spec-driven development (Section 3) is the answer to verification debt — not itself a measurement of any person's review capacity.

## Details (topic by topic)
 
### Topic 1 — The mechanics of output outrunning review (verification debt, review debt, acceleration whiplash)
 
**The term, traced to its real origin (a sourcing correction for the plan).** The Section 2 plan attributes "verification debt" to Sonar and "acceleration whiplash" to Augment Code. The cleaner attribution: **"verification debt" was coined by AWS CTO Werner Vogels** at re:Invent 2025; Sonar's 2026 survey *popularized and quantified* it. **"Acceleration whiplash" is Faros AI's** (their April 2026 telemetry report); **Augment Code's** own term is **"review debt."** They name one phenomenon from three vantage points — Vogels the cognitive cause, Sonar the survey symptom, Faros the telemetry symptom — and Section 2/3 should cite each for what it actually contributes rather than blur them.
 
**The mechanism, stated exactly (and why it's Clay's argument almost verbatim).** Vogels' framing is the keystone of the entire stream: you will write less code because generation is cheap, and review *more* code because understanding it takes time — and the reason it takes time is that comprehension comes with the act of creation. When you author, the mental model is a free byproduct of the work; when the machine authors, you must **rebuild that comprehension during review**, paying separately for what used to come bundled. That is the producible/answerable split looked at from the cost side: the agent took the producible middle *and the free comprehension that came with producing it*, leaving the human the answerable back end *without* the understanding that authorship used to supply. Verification debt is the gap between what's been shipped and what's been understood (CACM's framing: technical debt is a bet about future cost; **verification debt is unknown risk you are running right now**), and it "breeds false confidence — the codebase looks clean, the tests are green" (practitioner echo of the condition chapter's "green tests and ship it").
 
**The telemetry that shows the jam is real, not theoretical.** Faros AI's *Acceleration Whiplash* is the strongest current anchor because it's before/after telemetry, not a survey: throughput is genuinely up (epics/dev +~66%, tasks +~34%), and the downstream bill is larger — incidents per PR +~243%, code churn +~861%, **median review time +~441%**, ~31% of PRs merged with no human review at all. Faros' own framing is Clay-aligned: AI flooded a system built around human-paced development with output it was never designed to absorb, and the dangerous code isn't obviously bad — it's **superficially convincing**. Sonar adds the perception/behavior gap (96% distrust, 48% verify) and the effort cost (38% say AI code is *harder* to review than human code). Augment Code states the structural consequence plainly: code creation accelerated, review didn't, and that mismatch is now the bottleneck.
 
**The asymmetry that makes review *the* binding constraint.** The agent has no working-memory limit and does not fatigue; the human reviewer has both. So as the producible middle accelerates without bound, the entire pressure of the pipeline concentrates on the one resource that can't be scaled by adding compute — and you can't hire your way out of it either, because every added reviewer brings the same ceiling. This is *condition replaces time* from Section 3 made concrete at the back end: the binding constraint is no longer schedule, it's the human's capacity to review and answer. Review capacity is therefore not a sub-topic of the ADLC; it is the place the whole "human bears all the risk" thesis either holds or collapses.
 
### Topic 2 — What review capacity actually consists of (and whether it builds fast or slow)
 
**Review capacity is condition, and condition has two layers.** This is the stream's central synthesis, and it inherits Section 1 and Stream F directly. Review capacity decomposes into a **climate** layer and a **weather** layer, and they fail differently:
 
- **The climate of review — the owned situation model (slow to build, can't be borrowed).** Per the comprehension literature, understanding a program means holding a *mental model*: chunks and plans recognized in the text, beacons that cue familiar structures, and — the part that matters most — a **situation model** that maps the code to its *purpose* (the why), as distinct from the **program model** that captures *what it does* (the how). Experts hold a rich situation model; novices barely form one; and it develops slowly, with experience, while the program model is comparatively quick and static (Pennington; Burkhardt et al.). The METR brownfield result is this finding in the field: the experienced developers' edge *was* their situation model — internalized architecture, idioms, quality standards, tacit history — accumulated over years and ~1,500 commits, and precisely the thing the agent could not supply. **The agent ships you the program model for free and cannot ship you the situation model at all.** Review is the act of supplying the missing situation model yourself, fast, for code you didn't write. That is why owned understanding is non-negotiable, why it can't be rented, and why audiences whose work *is* unfamiliar code (Legacy, External-Dev handover) must **develop the faculty** rather than borrow a frame.
- **The weather of review — finite attention and working memory (fast to deplete, lies about its own state).** Even with a strong climate, the act of reviewing draws down a finite resource within a single sitting: detection degrades past ~200–400 LOC and after ~60–90 minutes; every open review thread is an open loop consuming the handful of working-memory slots a person has; context-switching between reviews imposes a heavy reorientation tax. This is the *weather* of the condition chapter, and it carries the same poison: the depleted reviewer's read on their own depletion fails with the depletion — the tired reviewer feels thorough while skimming. So "I reviewed it carefully" is a self-report, and self-report is least trustworthy exactly when the diff is largest and the hour is latest.
**Can it be built quickly or must it develop slowly? The answer splits along the develop-vs-borrow axis — which is why this stream feeds the diagnostic.** The two layers have different time-constants, and that difference *is* the develop-vs-borrow distinction applied to the back end:
- The **situation model (climate) must be developed slowly.** There is no shortcut; it is the expertise the comprehension literature and METR both describe as years-deep and tacit. Borrowing it is not on the menu.
- The **threat model / review frame (what to look for) can be partly externalized and taught faster** — a directed register of questions, a checklist of the failure modes AI code is prone to (the security data from the PPW profile: ~45% OWASP, ~2.7× issues, the polished-but-wrong pattern), a definition-of-done. Evidence supports a *partial* transfer: explicitly directing a reviewer to focus on security measurably increases security-defect detection, though a checklist *alone* doesn't (Braz et al., per the OpenStack/Qt review study). So the frame is borrowable enough to make a non-author *minimally answerable* — but only enough to catch the named failure modes, never enough to substitute for the missing situation model.
**The relocation-lens consequence (this is where Stream D meets Stream E).** Borrowing the frame without building the climate is a real, choosable trade — and it has a name in this stream: it trades the *cost* of comprehension for the *risk* of a blind spot. A reviewer running a borrowed checklist over code whose purpose they don't actually model will catch the listed failure modes and miss everything the list didn't anticipate — which, with superficially-convincing AI output, is the dangerous majority. The honest teaching move is the same as Stream E's: name the trade out loud. *You can stay answerable on a borrowed frame for a narrow, well-specified surface; the moment the surface is novel or the stakes are real, the unbuilt situation model is the dependency you chose, and it will surface as a blind spot you didn't see coming.*
 
### Topic 3 — Why the gate fails by default, and what makes it real (complacency, circularity, the spec)
 
**Automation complacency, pointed at the back end.** Section 1 and Stream B already establish Parasuraman & Manzey: complacency and automation bias afflict experts as well as novices, can't be trained away by simple practice, and worsen under load. Stream D's contribution is to show this is *worst* in AI-code review specifically, for three compounding reasons. First, AI output is **superficially convincing** — polished, symmetric, plausible — which is precisely the stimulus that lulls a reviewer (a messy human diff at least *looks* like it needs scrutiny). Second, the volume and the WIP load keep the reviewer perpetually depleted (weather), and complacency rises under exactly that load. Third, when an AI reviewer is added to "help," human deference to it tends to *ratify* its verdict rather than check it — a human-in-the-loop that amplifies rather than catches. The conclusion the practitioner literature converges on is the operational test for whether review is even happening: **if you can't articulate what "wrong" would look like for this change, you aren't reviewing — you're rubber-stamping** (the red-green-refactor / "seek out failure" discipline; Bahner et al. 2008 found deliberately exposing operators to automation failures during training is one of the few proven complacency countermeasures).
 
**The circularity trap when AI reviews AI.** A structurally important 2025–2026 result: when the generating agent and the reviewing agent share a training distribution, their failures correlate, so AI-review-of-AI-code checks the artifact against itself, not against intent (Zietsman 2026; the homogenization-trap and self-preference-bias findings). This is why "just add an AI reviewer" does not retire verification debt — it can launder it. The structural escape is an **external ground truth the review is checked against**, which is the spec.
 
**The spec is how you make the back end answerable without rebuilding the whole climate — the bridge to Section 3.** Spec-driven development (Section 3's most Clay-aligned flavor) is the structural answer this stream points to: a spec is origination made durable, an external reference that lets a reviewer check *does this do what we intended* rather than only *does this run*. It substitutes for part of the missing situation model by externalizing intent before the code exists — which is why the rubber-stamp threshold shifts when a developer verifies small increments against an executable spec rather than reviewing a 900-line diff at the end. Two cautions the stream must carry forward: the **self-spec substitution risk** (if the agent writes its own spec, it has taken the front end — Section 3 open question) and the reminder that a spec reduces but does not eliminate the comprehension burden; review capacity is still what's left to the human, spec or no spec.
 
### Topic 4 — Where "review capacity as condition" lives (resolving plan open question #4)
 
The plan asks whether this belongs in Section 2 (an audience trait) or Section 3 (an ADLC constraint). The research supports **both, with a clean division of labor** — and the climate/weather split is what makes the division clean:
 
- **Section 1 already owns the seed.** Review capacity *is* condition, so the one-line plant belongs in the condition arc: *the answerable half isn't just willingness to answer — it's the capacity to understand what you're answering for, and that capacity is condition, with the same climate and weather and the same self-report blindness.*
- **Section 2 owns it as an audience trait and a diagnostic reading.** For the four "answerable over work you didn't produce" audiences (SVD, EDD, LSD, AID), review capacity is the *thing being sorted* — which is why the Two Ends Diagnostic (Stream C) should read it behaviorally: not "do you review carefully?" (self-report, worst-case) but "show me the last diff you rejected and the reason," "what would 'wrong' look like for the last change you approved?" The diagnostic's job is to detect a *borrowed frame over an unbuilt climate* before it becomes a production incident.
- **Section 3 owns it as the ADLC constraint that makes the gates real.** The step-7 review gate and the deploy gate are only honorable if the human has the condition to honor them; "review capacity is part of condition" is the reason the maintenance trilogy (condition, observability, upkeep) bites hardest at the gates. This is where the stream's mechanics (batch size, WIP limits, spec-as-ground-truth, the rubber-stamp test) become process.
Net: **plant in Section 1, sort in Section 2, operationalize in Section 3, and measure in Section 4** (the Epistemic and Cognitive dimensions — can you still tell true from merely plausible as the volume grows, and is your review sharpening or dulling over time).

## Recommendations (module-build sequence)
 
Stream D is the research backbone of the **Verification & Review Capacity** module. The module grid marks it default for ITW, EDD, LSD, AID, and PPW and borrowable for SVD — the most portable row in the library alongside Spec-Driven Development, which makes it (per the plan's own note) a **promotion candidate to the universal set**. Recommendation: ship it as a near-universal module, built in four components by leverage, each pairing guidance + exercise + an agent skill, and each tied back to the climate/weather frame.
 
**Component 1 — The review-capacity read (build first; it's a diagnostic input).**
- *Guidance:* review capacity = climate (owned situation model, slow, unborrowable) + weather (finite attention, fast, self-report-blind). Name the rubber-stamp default and the superficially-convincing-output trap.
- *Exercise:* behavioral, not self-rated, per Stream C — "the last AI-authored change you approved: state what 'wrong' would have looked like, and how you'd have known." Inability to answer is the read.
- *Paired agent skill:* a review-load instrument that surfaces, from repo/PR history, diff sizes approved per sitting, review duration, and approve-without-comment rate — the observability dashboard pointed at the back end (a Faros-style read at one person's scale).
**Component 2 — Batch-size and WIP discipline (build second; the weather control).**
- *Guidance:* the 200–400 LOC / 60–90 min ceilings as engineering limits, not preferences; small batches are what make human oversight *feasible*, not just faster.
- *Exercise:* set a personal review-WIP limit and a max diff size that goes to `Ready`; split anything larger. Tie to Section 3's `Ready` gate.
- *Paired agent skill:* a PR-splitter / pre-review summarizer that flags logical breakpoints and refuses to present a single diff over the human's set ceiling without splitting it.
**Component 3 — The threat-model frame for AI-generated code (build third; the borrowable layer, with its limits stated).**
- *Guidance:* the named failure modes AI code is prone to (the PPW security set; polished-but-wrong; correlated AI-reviewer failures) as a *directed* review register — explicitly stating that this is the borrowable frame, and that it catches listed modes only.
- *Exercise:* run the register against a real AI-authored change; then deliberately seek the failure the register *didn't* name (the Bahner "seek out failure" drill) to expose the blind spot a borrowed frame leaves.
- *Paired agent skill:* an external-ground-truth reviewer that checks the change against the spec (not against itself), and explains *why* a flag matters in the org's terms — never auto-approving.
**Component 4 — Climate development for the develop-the-faculty audiences (build fourth; the slow, unborrowable layer).**
- *Guidance:* the situation-model finding — comprehension is the work; it builds slowly and can't be rented (METR brownfield). For Legacy/External-Dev, pair with the Code Comprehension & Characterization-Test-First modules.
- *Exercise:* before changing inherited/agent-authored code, reconstruct and write down its intent (an ADR / "why this exists" note) and a characterization test; the artifact *is* the situation model made external.
- *Paired agent skill:* an inquiry-mode comprehension partner (Explanatory/Socratic register, per Stream B) that helps build the model rather than handing over conclusions — calibrated by Stream F's wiring read.
**A library note for Clay.** Review capacity is the connective tissue of the back-end half of the playbook. It is the same construct as Section 3's step-7 gate at process scale, as Section 4's Epistemic/Cognitive dimensions at trailing-signal scale, and as the condition chapter's "weather" at the personal scale; it consumes Stream B's complacency and inquiry-vs-delegation findings, is *read* by Stream C's diagnostic, and is *traded* by Stream E's relocation lens (borrowed frame vs. blind-spot risk). The genuinely novel framing to present as the playbook's own — no off-the-shelf tool measures it — is **review capacity as condition with a climate/weather split**; the closest external anchors to credit are Vogels' verification debt (the cause), the Bacchelli–Bird / Sadowski review-is-comprehension finding (the nature of the work), and the SmartBear/Cisco ceiling (the limit).

## Clay-experience anchors to capture
 
Prompts for Clay to mine his 30-year engineering/founder history — the parts research can't supply:
 
- **The rubber-stamp you regret:** a change you approved that you hadn't truly understood — the diff that looked clean and broke something downstream. What made you skim it (size, fatigue, trust, deadline)? This is verification debt as a lived 2 a.m. story, and the argument for the weather control.
- **The situation model that saved you:** a review where your *owned understanding* of the system — something undocumented, tacit, in your head only — caught a defect no checklist or tool would have. This is the METR brownfield finding from the inside, and the argument that climate can't be borrowed.
- **A time you reviewed code you didn't write and couldn't see:** inheriting a legacy system, auditing an agency's or contractor's work, or taking over an agent's output — and the moment you realized you were answerable for something you didn't understand. This is the cross-audience thread (LSD/EDD) made concrete.
- **Your own review limit:** the diff size or the hour past which Clay knows his own review goes shallow — the personal version of the 400-LOC / 90-minute ceiling, and what discipline he built around it.
- **The first review question he asks:** when Clay reviews a change, what's the one question that tells him fastest whether the author (human or agent) actually understood the problem? Likely the flagship item for Component 1 and a candidate SJT for Stream C.
- **Borrowed-frame done right (and wrong):** a non-technical founder or operator he helped stay answerable for code they couldn't fully read — what minimal frame he gave them, and where it held vs. where the unbuilt model bit them. This is the develop-vs-borrow trade, lived.

## Caveats and reliability gradient
 
- **Build doctrine on (robust / well-replicated):** review is comprehension/knowledge-transfer work and change-understanding is its key activity (Bacchelli & Bird 2013; Sadowski et al. 2018; Czerwonka et al.) — peer-reviewed, multi-site, replicated; the finite-attention ceiling and the bigger/longer→worse-detection *direction* (SmartBear/Cisco lineage, corroborated across later datasets); owned/tacit understanding as the reviewer's decisive edge (METR early-2025 RCT + Feb-2026 follow-up; mechanism well-supported, sample small at 16 devs); automation complacency afflicting experts and resisting simple training (Parasuraman & Manzey 2010).
- **Cite with the caveat (strong but qualified):** the program-comprehension cognitive canon (program vs. situation model; chunks/plans/beacons; expert/novice gap; Brooks, Soloway & Ehrlich, Pennington, von Mayrhauser & Vans, Burkhardt et al.) — foundational and conceptually durable but mostly 1980s–90s, small-sample, procedural-era; use the *mechanism* (comprehension = building a slow, expertise-gated situation model), not the specific experiments as proof. The "no shared author intent makes AI code harder to review" point is practitioner-strong (Swarmia, Atomic Robot) and consistent with the comprehension literature, but is industry observation, not controlled study.
- **Point-in-time (will go stale; cite the date):** Sonar 2026 *State of Code* figures (96%/48%/38%/42%, ~65%-by-2027); Faros AI 2026 *Acceleration Whiplash* telemetry (+~441% review time, +~243% incidents/PR, +~861% churn, ~31% no-review merges, ~20%→~60% acceptance); the verification-debt/review-debt vocabulary and dates (Vogels at AWS re:Invent 2025; Augment Code Dec 2025; CACM Jan 2026); the AI-reviewing-AI correlated-failure results (2025–2026). Durable parts: the *direction* (output outruns review; review burden up; review is the binding constraint) and the *cause* (comprehension must be rebuilt at review). Perishable: every specific percentage and the relative ranking of vendors' review tools.
- **Sourcing correction for the plan:** "verification debt" originates with **Werner Vogels (AWS)**, popularized by **Sonar**; "acceleration whiplash" is **Faros AI's**, not Augment Code's (Augment's term is **"review debt"**). Cite each source for its actual contribution.
- **Treat vendor material as self-reported / commercially motivated:** Sonar, Augment Code, Faros AI, CodeRabbit, QA Wolf, and the various PR-analytics blogs all sell tools that the data implies you need. The *convergence* across independent vendors and academic studies is what makes the direction trustworthy; any single vendor's magnitude should be discounted. Faros is the strongest of these because it's before/after telemetry rather than a survey, but it is still **correlational**, not causal.
- **Methodological tension to keep visible, not paper over:** the review-effectiveness numbers (the 200–400 LOC ceiling, the detection-rate curves) were established for *team* code review with multiple human reviewers and historical defect data. The playbook applies the *principle* (finite attention has a hard ceiling; honor it with batch size and WIP limits) to a *single* originator-reviewer working with agents — a different setting. The honest framing: the ceiling is a robust human-attention finding; its exact numbers are a starting calibration to be re-read against the individual's own baseline (Stream F's climate), not a universal law.
