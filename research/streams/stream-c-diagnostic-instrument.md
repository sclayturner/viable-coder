# Stream C — The Diagnostic Instrument Itself
## Cross-cutting research stream feeding Unit 2 of *The Viable Coder Playbook* (Clay Turner, viablecoder.com)
 
> **What this stream is.** The producible/answerable split has to become a *usable sorting tool*, not just a philosophy. Unit 1 already established the principle this instrument must obey — *self-report lies; you don't ask a system how it feels, you instrument it* — but pointed it at the day-to-day condition (the **weather**). Stream C points the same principle at a standing question: **which of the two ends do you hold badly — origination at the front, answerability at the back?** It is the **content** half of the Unit 2 intake; **Stream F (the operating profile)** is the **wiring** half, and the two are administered together as one short read. Per the plan, the Two Ends Diagnostic ships as one of the five universal modules in every audience's default set, because every audience first has to be sorted before it can be served. This stream's job is to make that sort honest, short, and resistant to the flattering answer.
 
---
 
## TL;DR
 
- **The playbook's "self-report lies" principle is not a stance — it is the most replicated finding in the assessment literature, and it is *worst* exactly where this diagnostic operates.** Davis et al.'s 2006 *JAMA* systematic review found that of 20 comparisons between physician self-assessment and external measures of competence, 13 showed little, no, or an *inverse* relationship — and accuracy was worst among those who were the least skilled and the most confident, a pattern the authors note holds across professions. Self-perceived skill correlates with objectively measured skill at roughly r = .13–.24 in domains like evidence appraisal. The design consequence is direct: a diagnostic that asks *"how good is your judgment about what to build?"* measures confidence, not the faculty. The instrument has to read **behavior**, not self-rating — the observability move from Unit 1, turned from the weather to the climate.
- **The assessment field already built the three item-types that defeat the flattering answer, and they map cleanly onto the two ends.** (1) **Forced-choice** items match options on social desirability so the "best" answer is hard to spot, drastically reducing faking and acquiescence (Brown & Maydeu-Olivares; meta-analytic evidence) — use it where self-flattery is strongest, on origination and answerability themselves. (2) **Situational Judgment Tests** present a realistic scenario and read the *call you make in it*, with generalizable criterion validity around ρ = .34 (McDaniel et al. 2001 meta-analysis; 102 coefficients, 10,640 people) and less susceptibility to faking than self-report. (3) **Behavioral-event prompts** ("tell me about the last thing you built / shipped / inherited") rest on the behavioral-consistency principle — past behavior predicts future behavior — and have the property the playbook needs most: recounting a specific past event, you can't fabricate what actually happened. All three move the answer *outside the respondent's head*, which is the first of the three observability properties.
- **DORA's 2025 model is both the precedent and the warning: build a profile-router read off behavior, never a linear maturity ladder that types the person.** The 2025 DORA report *retired* its long-standing low/medium/high/elite tiers in favor of **seven multidimensional team archetypes** derived by cluster analysis (~5,000 respondents) — e.g., "Harmonious high-achievers" (~20%) and "Legacy bottleneck" (~11%) — plus a seven-capability "AI Capabilities Model," framing AI as an **amplifier / mirror** that "anoints" elite organizations and magnifies dysfunction. That is Clay's transfer-not-substitution arriving at team scale. The maturity-model literature explains *why* the tier-to-profile move matters: rigid staged ladders oversimplify, "neglect the potential existence of multiple equally advantageous paths" (Pöppelbuß & Röglinger 2011), and invite gaming the score — and CMM/CMMI was widely abandoned for Agile partly because of exactly that taxing rigidity. The six situations **are** the playbook's archetypes; the intake routes to one, it does not assign a grade. This keeps faith with Stream F's spine: **instrument the constraint, don't type the person.**
## Key Findings
 
**1. Robust / well-replicated (safe to build doctrine on):**
- *Self-assessment is an unreliable instrument, and unreliable in a direction that defeats triage.* Davis et al. 2006 (*JAMA* systematic review, 17 studies, 20 comparisons): 13/20 showed little/no/inverse self-vs-external correlation; worst accuracy in the least skilled and most confident. Corroborated in skill domains (EBM competence self-rating vs. Fresno test, r ≈ .13–.24). This is the Dunning-Kruger mechanism from Stream B, now stated as an *instrument-design* constraint rather than a learning one.
- *Structured, behaviorally-anchored assessment beats unstructured self-presentation on validity and reliability.* The U.S. OPM and the structured-interview literature: higher structure yields higher validity, rater reliability, and rater agreement, and less adverse impact. Hartwell (2019): past-behavioral and situational question types carry the highest predictive validity; background questions trail (≈ .16).
- *Situational Judgment Tests have generalizable criterion validity and resist faking better than self-report.* McDaniel et al. (2001) meta-analysis: ρ ≈ .34 for job performance, generalizable; ρ ≈ .46 with cognitive ability. Items are built from critical incidents; "construct-driven" SJTs (define the construct first, then write items keyed to it) predict the targeted construct better than generic ones.
- *Forced-choice formats reduce social-desirability, acquiescence, and faking.* Matching items on desirability makes the "best" answer hard to discern (Brown & Maydeu-Olivares 2014); meta-analytic work shows substantial faking reduction.
- *Brief instruments get used, and can match long ones when items are chosen for discrimination.* PHQ-9 development team: in busy settings, brevity drives adoption, and as few as 1–2 items can carry high screening sensitivity. IRT-based item reduction has produced short forms that match the full measure (e.g., a 4-of-17-item autism screener at AUC ≈ .95; a 43→15-item environment scan with domain correlations > .85).
**2. Strong but with a methodological caveat (cite with the caveat):**
- *Forced-choice has a cost: ipsativity.* Purely ipsative scores (rank within a block) are unsuitable for comparing one person to another or to a norm (Hicks 1970); partially-ipsative or Thurstonian-IRT scoring is needed to recover normative meaning, and forced-choice generally has *lower* reliability and demands *more* items than a Likert scale to reach the same precision.
- *SJTs measure a method, not a clean construct.* They correlate with cognitive ability and personality, so a high score isn't a pure read of the targeted faculty; their validity is real but partly "borrowed" from general ability.
**3. Point-in-time (cite with the mid-2025/2026 date; will go stale):**
- The DORA 2025 archetypes, percentages, and the seven-capability model (Google/DORA, Sept 2025; ~5,000 respondents). The *names and shares* will move in future reports; the durable parts are the **tier→profile shift** and the **amplifier/mirror** framing.
**4. Design lesson, not measurement (flag as framing):**
- The maturity-model critique tradition (CMM/CMMI lineage; Pöppelbuß & Röglinger 2011; Microsoft's "a model, not a prescriptive process"). Useful as a guardrail against building a ladder; not itself evidence about any person.
## Details (topic by topic)
 
### Topic 1 — Instruments that resist the flattering answer (the observability principle, pointed at the climate)
 
**The inheritance, stated exactly.** Unit 1's observability lesson gives a good self-instrument three properties: its answer **lives outside your head** (a log, a count, a record you could show someone), you **ask it off the decision** (on a schedule, not in the moment you most want a flattering answer), and it **targets your specific failure**, not the generic dimension. Stream C's entire task is to build the Unit 2 intake to those three properties — because the question *"which end do you hold badly?"* is the single question a respondent is most motivated to answer flatteringly, and least able to answer honestly by introspection.
 
**Why introspection fails here specifically — the generalization of "self-report lies."** Unit 1 made the case for the *weather* (a tired mind says it's sharp). The assessment literature makes the same case for the *standing faculty*, and it is the most load-bearing finding in this stream. Davis et al.'s 2006 *JAMA* review compared physicians' self-rated competence against external observation across 17 studies: most comparisons showed little, no, or an inverse relationship, and — the part that matters for a diagnostic — the people *least* able to self-assess were the least skilled and the most confident. The same poor correspondence shows up when you measure a concrete skill: senior medical students' self-perceived competence in searching and appraising evidence correlated with their objectively measured competence at only r ≈ .13 and .24. The lesson is not "self-report is worthless"; it is that **self-report is worst exactly at the low-skill / high-confidence corner** the diagnostic most needs to catch — the founder who is sure he originates but was handed his frame, the operator certain she can answer for code she's never read. An intake built on self-rating will wave those people through. One built to read behavior will not.
 
**The salvage condition.** Self-report is rehabilitated *only* when it is anchored to an external standard and to behavioral data — incorporating known standards of performance with internal and external evidence measurably improves the accuracy of a person's self-assessment (anatomical-pathology education review, citing the Davis line). This is the design rule for the whole intake: **never accept a self-claim as the sole input; pair every claim with an artifact.** "You review everything the agent writes" is a self-rating; "show me the last three diffs you rejected and why" is a reading that lives outside the head.
 
**The three item-types the field already validated, mapped to the two ends.**
 
- **Forced-choice (for the two ends themselves, where self-flattery is strongest).** Instead of "rate your judgment about what to build (1–5)" — which invites the flattering answer — forced-choice presents options matched on desirability and makes the respondent *choose*: e.g., between "I most enjoy finding the right problem to solve" and "I most enjoy making a solution robust enough to ship." Matching on social desirability makes the "best" answer hard to discern, which is precisely why forced-choice reduces faking and acquiescence (Brown & Maydeu-Olivares 2014; corroborated meta-analytically). The cost is real and must be designed around: pure ipsative scoring can't compare people or norms (Hicks 1970), reliability is lower, and more items are needed — so forced-choice is the right tool for the *origination-vs-answerability tilt* (an intra-person question, where ipsativity is a feature, not a bug) but the wrong tool for "how much capability does this person have in absolute terms."
- **Situational Judgment Tests (to read the call, not the claim).** Present a realistic agentic-era scenario and ask what the respondent would do, scoring the *judgment displayed* rather than the *competence asserted*: e.g., *"You hand a feature to an agent and it returns 600 lines that pass the tests. What do you do next?"* The answer that routes straight to merge, the one that reads every line, and the one that asks the agent to explain its own design are different readings of the answerable end. SJTs carry generalizable criterion validity (ρ ≈ .34; McDaniel et al. 2001) and are less fakeable than self-report because the respondent has to *act in the situation*, not describe themselves. Build them construct-driven: define origination and answerability first, then write scenarios keyed to each, so the score reads the intended end rather than general cleverness (the known SJT caveat — they correlate with cognitive ability, so they are not a construct-pure read).
- **Behavioral-event prompts (to read what actually happened).** The behavioral-interview tradition (developed by I/O psychologists in the 1970s; McClelland's competency lineage; the STAR structure) rests on one principle the playbook already believes: past behavior is the best predictor of future behavior. Its decisive property for this intake is that when a person recounts a *specific past event* — the last thing they built, shipped, or inherited — they cannot fabricate the facts of what actually happened the way they can polish a self-rating. *"Tell me about the last thing you built. Did you choose it, or did it choose you?"* is the can-vs-should gate (Stream A) administered as a behavioral-event prompt: it reads origination off a real episode, off the decision, from outside the head — all three observability properties at once. Structured and rubric-scored, these questions out-validate unstructured self-presentation (OPM; Hartwell 2019).
**The synthesis.** The intake should use all three, because they cover the two ends from three angles: forced-choice exposes the *tilt* between the ends, SJT scenarios read the *call* at each end, and behavioral-event prompts read the *track record* at each end. Each one is engineered to defeat the flattering answer in a different way; together they make "which end do you hold badly?" answerable without routing through the broken gauge.
 
### Topic 2 — Operationalizing "which end do you hold badly?" into a short intake
 
**The construct, made explicit.** The two ends are orthogonal, which yields a 2×2 the intake exists to locate a person within: **holds both** (the rare, fully-answerable originator), **holds the front, not the back** (originates well but can't stand behind the result — the prototype-wall and AI-gap shape), **holds the back, not the front** (answerable and conscientious but building from capability rather than conviction, or directing work they didn't originate), and **holds neither** (borrowing both ends from a vendor, an agency, or an agent). The plan's per-situation "end held badly" tags are read positions on this grid. This is the playbook's own construct; what the psychometric literature supplies is not proof of the construct but the *method* for measuring it honestly — and the method is construct-driven design: pin the construct down first, then build items keyed to it (the discipline that separates valid construct-driven SJTs from generic ones).
 
**Short is not a compromise — it's a requirement for use, and it's achievable.** An intake that doesn't get taken measures nothing, and length is the first thing that kills administration; brief instruments get used precisely where longer ones don't, and a screener can run on remarkably few items when those items are chosen for *discrimination* rather than coverage (PHQ-9 development; IRT item-reduction precedents producing short forms that match full measures). The engineering principle: don't write the intake to *cover* origination and answerability exhaustively; write it to *discriminate* the four quadrants and the six situations with the fewest, most-separating items — keep the items that change the routing, drop the ones that merely confirm it.
 
**Triage, not diagnosis — route, don't label.** A screener's job is sensitivity and routing, not a fine-grained score (the triage-tool tradition: a handful of items that *sort* people toward the right next step). The intake's output is a **module set**, not a grade: it sorts the respondent into one of the six situations (which picks the default modules), reads the persona overlay (which sets develop-the-faculty vs. borrow-the-frame), and hands off to Stream F's operating-profile read (which tunes delivery). The composition the plan names — *situation picks the module set; persona sets develop-vs-borrow; wiring tunes the delivery* — is exactly what a routing intake should output, and nothing more. Treating the result as a label ("you are a Level 2") is the maturity-ladder failure mode Topic 3 warns against.
 
**The error to optimize against.** Because self-report is worst at the low-skill/high-confidence corner (Topic 1), the costly error for this intake is a **false "holds the end fine"** — the flattering answer slipping through and routing someone away from the faculty they actually lack. So the intake should be tuned for **sensitivity to the unheld end**, and the tie-breaker rule is fixed in advance: **when the forced-choice/SJT reading and the behavioral artifact disagree, trust the artifact.** That rule is the observability principle ("the reading has to be something you could show another person") written into the scoring.
 
### Topic 3 — Maturity / readiness models as precedent (and the ladder trap to avoid)
 
**The precedent worth copying: DORA's move from tiers to profiles.** For more than a decade DORA sorted teams on a single linear ladder — low / medium / high / elite. The **2025 DORA report (*State of AI-assisted Software Development*; ~5,000 respondents) retired that ladder** and replaced it with **seven multidimensional team archetypes** derived by cluster analysis across performance, stability, and well-being dimensions — e.g., "Harmonious high-achievers" (~20% of respondents) and "Legacy bottleneck" (~11%) — alongside an inaugural **AI Capabilities Model** of seven foundational capabilities (a clear and communicated AI stance, healthy data ecosystems, AI-accessible internal data, a strong user-centric focus, and others). The report's framing is squarely Clay-aligned: AI is an **amplifier** and a **mirror** — it "anoints" elite organizations rather than creating them, and magnifies existing dysfunction as readily as existing capability, so a team's *archetype* determines what benefits and risks AI will bring it. This is transfer-not-substitution and "tooling without owned capability is hollow," arriving at team scale from an independent source. The structural lesson for the playbook: **sort into profiles, each with its own starting strategy — don't rank on a single axis.** The six situations are precisely that kind of profile set; DORA's archetypes are the working precedent that a profile-router is more honest and more actionable than a maturity grade.
 
**The lineage, briefly.** Maturity models descend from the Capability Maturity Model (SEI, Humphrey, late 1980s) and its successor CMMI — five staged levels (Initial → Managed → Defined → Quantitatively Managed → Optimizing). They are built two ways: **top-down** (fix the stages first, then describe each — Becker et al.) or **bottom-up** (determine characteristics first, then cluster them into levels — Lahrmann et al.). DORA's archetypes are a bottom-up, cluster-derived design, which is the relevant template here: let the profiles emerge from how the two ends actually co-occur, rather than decreeing a ladder.
 
**The trap to avoid, and why it matters more for *this* playbook than most.** The maturity-model critique tradition is consistent: staged ladders oversimplify reality, assume every user is alike, and "neglect the potential existence of multiple equally advantageous paths" (Pöppelbuß & Röglinger 2011); rigid frameworks are "inept at accounting for the differences between organizations" and, followed to the letter, push people to implement the wrong capabilities in the wrong order; CMM/CMMI was criticized for a strict process-orientation that "ignores the human factor," and risked organizations *gaming the level* rather than improving; much of the software industry abandoned CMM/CMMI for Agile partly because of that taxing rigidity, and Microsoft's own guidance is to treat CMMI as a model, not a prescriptive process to follow verbatim. For *The Viable Coder Playbook* the trap is sharper than usual, because the playbook's spine (Stream F) is **functional, not categorical — instrument the constraint, don't type the person.** A linear maturity score *is* a category; it types the person and invites them to grind for the next level. The diagnostic must therefore produce a **routing**, not a **rank**: which faculty to develop next, read off behavior and re-read over time — never a tier the person wears.
 
## Recommendations (module-build sequence)
 
Stream C feeds **one universal module — the Two Ends Diagnostic** — administered together with Stream F's **Operating Profile read** as a single short intake (per the plan, both sit in every audience's default set). Build it in four components, by leverage:
 
**Component 1 — The Two Ends intake (build first).**
- *Guidance:* the three-property rule (answer outside your head; asked off the decision; aimed at the specific failure) and the 2×2 of origination × answerability, with the four quadrants named. State up front that the output is a *route*, not a *grade*.
- *Exercise:* a short, three-instrument read — (a) a small forced-choice block tilting origination vs. answerability (matched on desirability); (b) two or three construct-driven SJT scenarios, one per end (the "agent returns 600 passing lines — now what?" family for the back; a "which of these is the real problem?" family for the front); (c) two behavioral-event prompts ("the last thing you built — did you choose it or did it choose you?"; "the last thing that broke after you shipped — what did you do?"). Keep it to the fewest, most-discriminating items that separate the quadrants and route to a situation.
- *Paired agent skill:* an **intake agent** that refuses self-rating as a sole input, asks for the behavioral artifact behind each claim, scores against the rubric, and emits the route — never a level. Tuned per audience by Stream F's read: more scaffolding and externalized framing for some climates, terser for others.
- *Threshold to change:* optimize for sensitivity to the *unheld* end; when an SJT/forced-choice reading and a behavioral artifact disagree, the artifact wins.
**Component 2 — The behavioral-anchor verifier (build second; the "show me" move).**
- *Guidance:* the salvage condition from Topic 1 — self-report is usable only when anchored to an external standard and real behavior.
- *Exercise:* for each end the respondent claims to hold, produce one artifact that proves it — a PRD or "why now" they actually wrote (front); a diff they rejected and the reason, a postmortem they owned (back). No artifact, no credit for the claim.
- *Paired agent skill:* a verifier that, given the respondent's own repo/PR/issue history, surfaces evidence for or against each claimed end — the observability dashboard pointed at the intake itself.
**Component 3 — The router, not the labeler (build third).**
- *Guidance:* the composition rule — situation picks the module set, persona sets develop-vs-borrow, wiring tunes delivery — with the explicit anti-ladder framing from Topic 3 (DORA's tier→profile move as the precedent; the maturity-model critiques as the guardrail).
- *Exercise:* map the intake placement to the default module collection for the matched situation, flag the one or two borrowable modules most likely to fit, and state the develop-vs-borrow default for the persona.
- *Paired agent skill:* a router that outputs the starter kit and the *reason* for each module, and that re-checks the route if the person's behavior later contradicts the initial read.
**Component 4 — Re-administration as a trailing signal (build fourth; the bridge to Unit 4).**
- *Guidance:* the intake is a gauge to re-read, not a label to wear (Unit 1's observability close: the nine questions are *trend* questions). Re-running the diagnostic over time turns "which end do you hold badly?" into a *trajectory* — is the unheld end being developed or is the gap widening?
- *Exercise:* schedule a re-read; compare the origination ratio and the reframe-vs-accept rate against the prior intake, read against the individual's own baseline (climate), not a population norm.
- *Paired agent skill:* a tracker that stores intakes as time series and reports the trend, feeding Unit 4's dashboard thresholds — which, per Stream F, are read against the person's climate rather than a generic default.
**A library note for Clay.** The Two Ends Diagnostic is the *first* module any audience meets, so its cross-references run everywhere: it administers **Stream A's** can-vs-should gate as a behavioral-event prompt; it is paired with **Stream F** as one intake (content + wiring); its develop-vs-borrow output is what calibrates the **Unit 3** teaching agents (transfer fully vs. teach just enough frame); and its re-read output is the same instrument as the **Unit 4** dashboard at a smaller scale. The closest design precedents to credit are DORA's archetypes (profile-routing over tiers) and the SJT/behavioral-interview traditions (reading behavior over self-rating); the *scoring novelty* — origination ratio, reframe-vs-accept — has no off-the-shelf tool and should be presented as the playbook's own, exactly as the Unit 3 doc flags for the trailing-signal metrics.

## Caveats and reliability gradient
 
- **Build doctrine on (robust / well-replicated):** self-assessment inaccuracy and its low-skill/high-confidence skew (Davis et al. 2006 *JAMA* systematic review; EBM self-vs-objective r ≈ .13–.24; the Dunning-Kruger lineage carried from Stream B); structured/behavioral-interview validity over unstructured self-presentation (U.S. OPM; Hartwell 2019); SJT generalizable criterion validity (McDaniel et al. 2001 meta-analysis, ρ ≈ .34); forced-choice reduction of social-desirability/acquiescence/faking (Brown & Maydeu-Olivares 2014; corroborating meta-analyses); brevity-drives-use plus item-reduction precedent for valid short forms (PHQ-9 team; IRT short-form studies).
- **Cite with the caveat (strong but qualified):** forced-choice **ipsativity** — pure ipsative scores can't be compared across people or to a norm (Hicks 1970); use partially-ipsative or Thurstonian-IRT scoring, and expect lower reliability and more items, which is why forced-choice fits the *intra-person tilt* question and not absolute-capability claims. SJTs are a **method, not a clean construct** — they correlate with cognitive ability and personality, so a score is not a pure read of origination or answerability; build them construct-driven and treat the read as directional.
- **Point-in-time (will go stale; cite the date):** DORA 2025 archetypes, their percentages, and the seven-capability AI Capabilities Model (Google/DORA, Sept 2025; ~5,000 respondents). Durable: the **tier→profile shift** and the **amplifier/mirror** framing. Perishable: the archetype names and shares.
- **Use as framing, not measurement:** the maturity-model critique tradition (CMM/CMMI lineage; Pöppelbuß & Röglinger 2011; Microsoft's "model, not prescriptive process"). It is a guardrail against building a ladder, not evidence about any individual.
- **Methodological tension to keep visible, not paper over:** the rigorous instruments (forced-choice, SJT, behavioral interview) were validated for **between-person selection at scale**, with norms, large samples, and IRT scoring. The playbook uses their *design principles* for a **single person's self-routing**, which is a different job. The honest framing — and the one that keeps *Remaining Viable* from having to apologize later — is that the playbook **borrows the principles** (move the answer outside the head; read behavior; anchor self-report to external standards; route, don't label; re-read as a trend), not the full psychometric apparatus. The intake is a disciplined, evidence-shaped *sorting tool*, not a validated test, and it should say so.
- **Normative-lens note (carried from Streams A, B, E):** the two ends, origination/answerability, and develop-vs-borrow are the author's constructs. The assessment literature supplies a rigorous **method for building an honest instrument** around them; it does not "prove" the constructs. Present the diagnostic as a rigorously-grounded way of *sorting*, not as an established psychometric model.
