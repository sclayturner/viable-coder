# Stream B — Adult Skill Acquisition, Transfer, and De-Skilling
### Cross-cutting research stream feeding Unit 2 of *The Viable Coder Playbook* (Clay Turner, viablecoder.com)
 
## TL;DR
- **The mechanism behind "transfer-not-substitution" is now empirically established, not just intuited.** A 2026 Anthropic randomized controlled trial (n=52) found AI-assisted developers scored 17% lower (two letter grades; Cohen's *d*=0.738, *p*=0.010) on a comprehension quiz than hand-coders — and the decisive variable was *how* they used AI: "delegators" who offloaded code generation clustered at 24–39% (below 40%), while "inquirers" who asked conceptual questions clustered at 65–86%. This is the single strongest piece of doctrine in the stream: the same tool transfers a faculty or substitutes for it depending almost entirely on interaction style, which is a *teachable behavior*.
- **"Self-report lies" is half-right and must be stated precisely.** The human-factors literature (Parasuraman & Manzey 2010; Dunning-Kruger; METR 2025) robustly shows in-the-moment self-assessment is an unreliable gauge of both skill and automation reliance — METR developers estimated AI sped them up 20% while it actually slowed them 19%. But the Anthropic study's *own* self-report data showed AI users rating their learning *lower* and expressing regret, not overconfidence. The correct doctrine: **instrument trailing behavioral signals (can they debug unaided?), not in-the-moment confidence — because confidence is noisy in both directions and degrades together with the faculty that would read it.**
- **The playbook should ship at least four modules from this stream**, each pairing guidance + exercise + a calibrated teaching-agent skill: (1) a *develop-the-faculty teaching protocol* built on cognitive apprenticeship (model → coach → scaffold → fade); (2) an *inquiry-vs-delegation diagnostic* derived directly from the Anthropic interaction taxonomy; (3) a *structural accountability gate* design pattern grounded in automation-complacency research; and (4) a *trailing-signal assessment kit* (Dreyfus-anchored, retrieval-based) to replace vibes-based "before/after."
## Key Findings
 
**1. Robust, mechanism-level (safe to build doctrine on):**
- *Generation builds capability; consumption does not.* Bjork's "desirable difficulties," the testing effect, and the generation effect converge on a single causal claim: retrieval and self-generation strengthen durable, transferable memory, while passive presentation feels easier but produces weaker retention. This is the cognitive-science "why" beneath inquirer-vs-delegator.
- *Cognitive apprenticeship (Collins, Brown & Newman, 1989)* provides a ready-made teaching-agent state machine: **modeling → coaching → scaffolding → fading**, plus articulation, reflection, exploration. "Fading" is the operational definition of transfer-not-substitution: the master deliberately withdraws support as the learner takes the end.
- *Automation complacency and bias afflict experts and novices alike, cannot be trained away by simple practice, and worsen under high workload/multitasking* (Parasuraman & Manzey 2010). This is the load-bearing justification for **structural** accountability gates rather than exhortations to "review carefully."
- *The out-of-the-loop performance problem* (Endsley & Kiris 1995): operators of automated systems lose the situation awareness and skill needed to take over when automation fails — passive monitoring is not enough.
- *Self-assessment is an unreliable instrument* (Dunning & Kruger 1999 and successors): the metacognitive skill needed to judge your own competence is the same skill you lack when incompetent. Hence trailing behavioral signals.
**2. Strong but newer / point-in-time (cite with date and caveat):**
- The Anthropic skill-formation RCT (Shen & Tamkin, 2026) — strong design (pre-registered RCT) but small (n=52), short (<1 hour), measures immediate comprehension not longitudinal retention, used a chat-based GPT-4o assistant (agentic tools expected to be *worse* for learning), and treats the interaction patterns as associations, not causal.
- The METR 2025 RCT — rigorous, but n=16 experienced devs, early-2025 tools, productivity (not skill) outcome; a follow-up was effectively abandoned in Feb 2026 due to selection bias.
- Microsoft/CMU critical-thinking study (Lee et al., CHI 2025) — survey/self-report (n=319), correlational, not causal.
**3. Weaker / correlational (use only as color, flag explicitly):** The "Google effect"/digital amnesia (Sparrow et al. 2011) and GPS/spatial-memory atrophy lines — directionally supportive of cognitive-offloading concerns but small effect sizes and not specific to skill formation. Vendor/blog claims about Copilot ("46% of code," "55% faster") — vendor statistics, useful only as adoption context.
 
## Details (topic by topic)
 
### 1. The Anthropic skill-formation RCT (the keystone)
**Source:** Judy Hanwen Shen & Alex Tamkin, "How AI Impacts Skill Formation," Anthropic (blog dated Jan 29, 2026); arXiv:2601.20245 (rev. Feb 1, 2026). Conducted under the Anthropic Safety Fellows Program. **Reliability: high (pre-registered RCT, peer-review-grade preprint), but small and short.**
 
**Design.** 52 mostly-junior software engineers (≥1 yr weekly Python, some AI-assistant familiarity, none had used the target library) randomized 26/26 into AI-assisted vs hand-coding (documentation + web search only). Three parts: warm-up; two coding features using **Trio** (a Python async/concurrency library, chosen because it requires genuinely new conceptual learning); then a closed-book quiz (no AI for either group). The AI assistant (interface-named "Cosmo," base model GPT-4o) sat in a sidebar with access to the participant's code and could produce correct code on demand. 35-minute task limit. (Originally five tasks; reduced to two after four pilot studies.)
 
**Quiz.** 14 questions / 27 points, drawn from CS-education research, across four skill types explicitly chosen for their relevance to *overseeing AI-generated code*: **debugging** (catch when AI code is wrong), **code reading** (verify before deployment), **code writing**, and **conceptual** understanding.
 
**Headline results.**
- AI group averaged **50%** vs **67%** for hand-coders (these absolute means are stated in Anthropic's blog; the paper itself reports a **4.15-point gap on a 27-point quiz = 17% = ~2 letter grades**, **Cohen's *d*=0.738, *p*=0.010**; controlling for warm-up time, *d*=0.725, *p*=0.016).
- **No significant productivity gain:** the AI group finished ~2 minutes faster, not statistically significant. Several AI users spent up to 11 minutes — ~30% of allotted time — composing up to 15 queries. (Notably, 4 of 26 control participants failed to finish the second task in time; every AI participant finished.)
- **Largest gap was in debugging** (smallest in code reading). The paper states debugging showed the largest gap; the exact debugging sub-score difference appears only in a figure, not in prose. Mechanism: the control group *encountered more errors* (median 3 vs 1) and built debugging skill by resolving them independently — "We attribute the gains in skill development of the control group to the process of encountering and subsequently resolving errors independently."
**The decisive finding — interaction style as a teachable behavior.** Qualitative annotation of screen recordings yielded **six interaction patterns** (the paper is careful to call these *associations, not causal*):
 
*Low-scoring (cluster range 24–39%, "less than 40%"):*
- **AI Delegation (n=4):** wholly relied on AI to write code; fastest; few/no errors; lowest learning.
- **Progressive AI Reliance (n=4):** asked 1–2 questions then delegated all code; failed to master second-task concepts.
- **Iterative AI Debugging (n=4):** used AI to debug/verify their own code but to *solve* problems rather than *clarify their own understanding*; scored poorly AND were slower.
*High-scoring (cluster range 65–86%, "65% or higher"):*
- **Generation-then-Comprehension (n=2):** generated code, then asked follow-up questions to understand it. (Behaviorally near-identical to AI Delegation — the *only* difference is checking their own understanding.)
- **Hybrid Code-Explanation (n=3):** asked for code *plus* explanations of that code.
- **Conceptual Inquiry (n=7):** asked only conceptual questions, coded independently; hit many errors but resolved them; fastest of the high-scorers and second-fastest overall.
(Query-type data reinforce this: 3 of the 8 lowest-scoring participants asked AI to generate code *without* asking for explanations.)
 
**Playbook mapping.** This is the empirical spine of **transfer-not-substitution**. The "producible middle" (code generation) is exactly what delegators hand off — and handing off the *middle* is fine for productivity on mastered skills, but during skill *formation* it hollows out the **answerable** half (debugging, verification, conceptual judgment) that the human must keep to stand behind the result. Inquirer behaviors are the human reclaiming the answerable half while still letting the agent produce. The same knife-edge, observable in screen recordings.
 
**Key author quotes:** "AI-enhanced productivity is not a shortcut to competence." "As companies transition to more AI code writing with human supervision, humans may not possess the necessary skills to validate and debug AI-written code if their skill formation was inhibited by using AI in the first place." On agentic tools: the chat setup is "a lower bound for cognitive offloading"; agentic tools requiring less human participation are expected to be *worse* for learning. On oversight: "the problem of supervising more and more capable AI systems becomes more difficult if humans have weaker abilities to understand code."
 
**Important nuance on "self-report lies."** The arXiv paper's own self-report data does **not** show AI users overestimating their learning. On a 7-point scale, the control (No AI) group reported *higher* self-reported learning; AI users found the *task* easier but the *quiz* equally hard, and expressed post-task regret — e.g., "By using the AI assistant, I feel like I got lazy"; "there are still a lot of gaps in my understanding." So the perception-vs-reality *overconfidence* gap comes from other studies (METR; Lee et al.), not this one. Doctrine must reflect that the failure direction of self-report varies.
 
### 2. Cognitive apprenticeship, deliberate practice, and the teaching-agent register
**Cognitive apprenticeship (Collins, Brown & Newman, 1989; Collins, Brown & Holum, 1991). Reliability: high — foundational, widely validated framework.** Makes expert *thinking* visible (vs traditional apprenticeship's visible *doing*). Six methods, the first three being the core: **modeling** (expert demonstrates, making implicit reasoning explicit) → **coaching** (observes and assists during practice) → **scaffolding** (support — hints, reminders, partial solutions — calibrated to the learner's zone of proximal development) → then **fading** ("once the learner has a grasp of the target skill, the master reduces his participation... providing only limited hints, refinements, and feedback"). Plus **articulation** (learner explains their reasoning), **reflection** (compare own process to expert's), and **exploration** (learner sets own goals). The model insists learning be *situated* in authentic context.
 
This maps almost 1:1 onto how a teaching agent should behave for **develop-the-faculty** learners: model the reasoning, scaffold with progressively thinner support, then *deliberately fade* — the opposite of an agent that silently emits the finished answer. McKinsey's modern apprenticeship program (Christensen et al., 2021) explicitly operationalizes model/scaffold/coach/fade with "bidirectional responsibilities."
 
**Deliberate practice (Ericsson, Krampe & Tesch-Römer, 1993). Reliability: high as a framework; magnitude contested.** Core elements: well-defined stretch goals at the edge of current ability, full concentration, *immediate and continual feedback*, and repeated revision after reflection — guided by a coach/teacher who designs the practice. "Blind repetition is not enough." **Caveat/tension:** Macnamara & Maitra (2019, *Royal Society Open Science*) ran a double-blind replication of the seminal violin study and *failed to replicate* the core claim that accumulated deliberate practice corresponds to skill level; the effect was real but substantially smaller, and teacher-designed practice was not superior to solo practice. Doctrine: use deliberate practice's *structure* (stretch + feedback + revision), don't over-claim that practice hours determine expertise.
 
**Playbook mapping.** These give the two teaching-agent registers their pedigree: the *Explanatory/Socratic* register = cognitive-apprenticeship modeling + articulation; the calibration of how much help to give = scaffolding-then-fading. The agent's job for a faculty-developer is to engineer immediate feedback on the learner's *own* attempts, not to supply the attempt.
 
### 3. Automation complacency and bias — why accountability must be structural
**Parasuraman & Manzey, "Complacency and Bias in Human Use of Automation: An Attentional Integration," *Human Factors* 52(3), 2010, pp. 381–410. Reliability: high — canonical peer-reviewed review, 1,000+ citations.** Integrated findings:
- **Complacency and automation bias are overlapping, attention-driven phenomena.** Complacency = poor monitoring of automation; automation bias = over-relying on automated cues, producing both *omission* (missing what automation didn't flag) and *commission* (following automation against contrary evidence) errors.
- **Both afflict naïve AND expert participants** and **"cannot be overcome with simple practice"** / "cannot be prevented by training or instructions."
- **Worsens under multiple-task load:** complacency "occurs under conditions of multiple-task load, when manual tasks compete with the automated task for the operator's attention."
- **The "usually right" trap:** highly reliable automation trains the heuristic "the machine knows better than I do," and operators of constant-high-reliability systems were found ~50% less likely to detect failures than operators of variable-reliability systems. Trust miscalibration deepens with accumulated uneventful experience.
**Out-of-the-loop performance problem (Endsley & Kiris, 1995, *Human Factors*). Reliability: high.** Full automation produced lower Level-2 situation awareness (comprehension of what data *means*) and slower recovery after automation failure than manual or *intermediate* automation. The shift from *active to passive* information processing was the likely cause. Keeping the operator in the active decision loop (intermediate automation) preserved SA and the ability to take over.
 
**Playbook mapping.** This is the rigorous basis for the claim that **the human at the back end will rubber-stamp agent output unless structurally forced not to.** Good intentions and "review carefully" fail predictably — complacency is not a character flaw correctable by willpower or training; it is an attention-allocation outcome that intensifies exactly when the agent is usually right (i.e., always). Therefore answerability gates must be **structural**: forcing functions that re-insert active processing (e.g., require the human to predict behavior before running, to write the failing test, to explain why the code is correct) rather than passively approve. Intermediate automation > full automation for anyone who must stay answerable.
 
### 4. Competency frameworks and measuring skill acquisition
**Dreyfus model (Stuart & Hubert Dreyfus, 1980, Air Force–funded pilot-training research). Reliability: high as a descriptive framework.** Five stages: **Novice** (follows context-free rules mechanistically, needs supervision) → **Advanced Beginner** (working understanding, sees actions as steps, can do simpler tasks unsupervised) → **Competent** (plans, troubleshoots, owns decisions, works independently to acceptable standard) → **Proficient** (sees situations holistically, pattern-based) → **Expert** (intuitive, fluid, recognition-based, no conscious deliberation). Later extended with a sixth "mastery" stage. Key insight: experts don't just know more — they think differently (pattern recognition over rules). Patricia Benner adapted it influentially to nursing (*From Novice to Expert*).
 
**Bloom's taxonomy / competency-based assessment. Reliability: medium-high (frameworks, not findings).** Competency-based assessment doctrine consistently emphasizes: measure *application in real tasks*, not theoretical recall; use observable evidence (work samples, simulations, behavioral interviews); pre/post assessment plus behavior-change follow-up; standardized rubrics with behavioral anchors. The recurring warning: self-assessment is useful for reflection but unreliable for decisions.
 
**Playbook mapping — develop-faculty vs borrow-frame.** Dreyfus gives a vocabulary for the distinction. **Borrow-the-frame** = get a learner to roughly Advanced Beginner / low Competent: enough working understanding to stay answerable for a call they'll never author. **Develop-the-faculty** = move them toward genuine Competent/Proficient as a real, owned capability. The teaching agent must *know which target it is teaching to* and fade accordingly. Assessment must be trailing and behavioral (can they debug unaided, read and verify, explain the concept) — exactly the four Anthropic skill types — not "rate your confidence 1–7."
 
### 5. The de-skilling / skill-atrophy literature (breadth)
- **Anthropic RCT** — strongest direct evidence (see §1).
- **METR (Becker, Rush, Barnes & Rein, July 10, 2025), "Measuring the Impact of Early-2025 AI on Experienced Open-Source Developer Productivity," arXiv:2507.09089. Reliability: high design, small n.** RCT, 16 experienced OSS devs, 246 real tasks on their own mature repos (avg 5 yrs, ~1M LOC), randomized AI-allowed/disallowed; tools = Cursor Pro + Claude 3.5/3.7 Sonnet. Verbatim: "we find that allowing AI actually increases completion time by 19%... After completing the study, developers estimate that allowing AI reduced completion time by 20%." Developers had *forecast* a 24% speedup; economics experts predicted 39% shorter and ML experts 38% shorter — all wrong in direction. **Tension to flag honestly:** this is a *productivity* result on *experts on familiar code*, the opposite regime from Anthropic's *skill-formation* result on *novices on new material*. They are complementary, not contradictory: AI can speed mastered work in some settings and slow it in others, while separately impeding new-skill formation. METR's follow-up (Feb 24, 2026) found the data "only very weak evidence" — for the returning subset it estimated a speedup of −18% (CI −38% to +9%) — because too many developers refused to work without AI (selection bias), itself a signal of dependence.
- **Microsoft/Carnegie Mellon (Lee et al., 2025), "The Impact of Generative AI on Critical Thinking," CHI 2025. Reliability: medium — survey/self-report, correlational.** 319 knowledge workers, 936 real task examples. Verbatim from the abstract: "higher confidence in GenAI is associated with less critical thinking, while higher self-confidence is associated with more critical thinking. Qualitatively, GenAI shifts the nature of critical thinking toward information verification, response integration, and task stewardship." And the vicious-circle finding directly relevant to oversight: "We find that knowledge workers often refrain from critical thinking when they lack the skills to inspect, improve, and guide AI-generated responses."
- **Cognitive-offloading classics. Reliability: medium — real but small effects, often correlational.** The "Google effect"/digital amnesia (Sparrow, Liu & Wegner, *Science*, 2011): people remember *where* to find information rather than the information itself when they expect future access. GPS/spatial-memory atrophy and "point-and-shoot" photo-memory effects (Henkel 2014) extend the pattern. Risko & Gilbert (2016) frame the general "use it or lose it" risk: offloading removes immediate load but "can also lead to a decrease in cognitive engagement and skill development" if excessive. A 2024–2025 study (Gerlich) found a negative correlation between frequent GenAI use and critical-thinking scores, mediated by cognitive offloading, stronger in younger users — correlational, treat as color.
- **Copilot/classroom studies. Reliability: mixed.** Kazemitabaar (2023, n=69 beginners) found Copilot *improved* completion (1.15×) and evaluation scores (1.8×) for novices on intro Python — a counter-data point showing AI *can* help when used to scaffold low-complexity tasks. Other studies note over-reliance, illegible/partially-correct output (one eval: 28.7% fully correct, 51.2% partial, 20.1% incorrect), and "blindly copying and pasting." Vendor figures are adoption context, not skill evidence: GitHub's own controlled experiment reported developers using Copilot completed a single JavaScript HTTP-server task "55% faster" (1h11m vs 2h41m, *P*=.0017), and GitHub telemetry reports Copilot "generates an average of 46% of code written by users, with Java developers reaching 61%."
### 6. How skill transfers between humans (the cognitive-science "why")
**Barnett & Ceci (2002), "When and where do we apply what we learn? A taxonomy for far transfer," *Psychological Bulletin* 128(4), 612–637. Reliability: high.** Transfer ranges from **near** (similar content/context — succeeds relatively often) to **far** (different knowledge domain, physical/temporal/functional/social context, or modality — fails frequently). Far transfer requires explicit teaching of abstract principles; surface similarity without structural correspondence misleads. Sobering consensus: far transfer in education is hard and often doesn't occur ("what you teach is what you get").
 
**Bjork's desirable difficulties (R. Bjork & E. Bjork, 1994, 2011). Reliability: high.** Conditions that *slow* acquisition *improve* long-term retention and transfer: **spacing, interleaving, retrieval practice (the testing effect), generation, and varied practice.** Storage strength ≠ retrieval strength; the struggle to retrieve is what strengthens future access. The **generation effect**: "any time that you, as a learner, look up an answer or have somebody tell or show you something that you could... generate instead, you rob yourself of a powerful learning opportunity." Practical calibration: aim for roughly 20–40% retrieval failure — enough struggle to be productive, not so much as to overload. **Critical nuance (cognitive-load theory):** desirable difficulties become *un*desirable when working-memory load (element interactivity × low expertise) is too high — i.e., scaffolding must be in place for the struggle to land.
 
**Playbook mapping.** This is the load-bearing "why" beneath inquirer-vs-delegator. Delegation removes the generation/retrieval/error-resolution that *is* the learning. Inquiry preserves it. The agent's correct move for a faculty-developer is to *manufacture desirable difficulty* — withhold the answer, prompt the learner to generate/predict first, then give feedback — exactly cognitive apprenticeship's fading plus Bjork's generation effect. And because far transfer is hard, the playbook must teach abstract principles explicitly and vary practice contexts, not assume a worked example in one setting will generalize.
 
## Recommendations: module-build sequence
 
Build in this order (highest doctrinal leverage first):
 
**Module 1 — The Develop-the-Faculty Teaching Protocol (build first).**
- *Guidance:* the cognitive-apprenticeship state machine (model → coach → scaffold → **fade**) reframed as transfer-not-substitution, with explicit "borrow-the-frame" (stop at Advanced Beginner) vs "develop-the-faculty" (drive to Competent+) target-setting up front.
- *Exercise:* learner attempts first; agent provides graded hints (Bjork-calibrated to ~20–40% productive failure); agent fades support across sessions.
- *Paired agent skill:* an "Explanatory/Socratic" register that refuses to emit finished answers for faculty-developers, asks for prediction/articulation first, and logs how much scaffolding it had to give (a fading metric).
- *Threshold to change:* if the learner can't progress even with maximum scaffolding, the difficulty is *undesirable* (cognitive overload) — drop back to modeling.
**Module 2 — Inquiry-vs-Delegation Diagnostic for Learners (build second).**
- *Guidance:* the Anthropic six-pattern taxonomy turned into a self- and observer-scored rubric; the core teachable distinction is Generation-then-Comprehension (good) vs AI Delegation (bad) — behaviorally identical except for the follow-up "why" question.
- *Exercise:* learners review their own transcripts/screen recordings and classify their pattern; target = conceptual inquiry / hybrid / generation-then-comprehension.
- *Paired agent skill:* a meter that flags pure-delegation sessions (code generated with zero conceptual or explanation queries — the paper's 3-of-8 lowest scorers) and nudges toward an inquiry follow-up.
- *Threshold:* sustained delegation during a *learning* objective triggers an intervention; during a *mastered-skill productivity* objective, delegation is fine.
**Module 3 — Structural Accountability Gates (build third).**
- *Guidance:* Parasuraman & Manzey + Endsley — complacency can't be willed or trained away, so the back-end answerability check must be a *forcing function* that re-inserts active processing.
- *Exercise/pattern:* require the human to (a) predict output before running, (b) write the failing test first, or (c) explain why AI code is correct *before* merge — gates that fail closed.
- *Paired agent skill:* an agent that withholds the "looks good" stamp and instead asks the human to demonstrate comprehension; intermediate-automation defaults over full automation.
- *Threshold:* raise gate friction as task stakes rise; lower it for reversible/low-stakes work.
**Module 4 — Trailing-Signal Assessment Kit (build fourth).**
- *Guidance:* "self-report lies" stated precisely — confidence is noisy in *both* directions (Dunning-Kruger over; experts under; Anthropic AI-users *under*-rated their own learning; METR devs *over*-credited AI) and degrades with the faculty, so measure trailing behavior. Use the four Anthropic skill types (debugging, code reading, code writing, conceptual) as the assessment spine; Dreyfus stages as the scale.
- *Exercise:* closed-book "can you debug/read/explain it unaided" checks days *after* the AI-assisted work, not in the moment.
- *Paired agent skill:* an assessor agent that generates delayed retrieval checks and tracks the gap between predicted and actual performance as a calibration signal.
- *Threshold:* genuine faculty = unaided performance holds after a delay; borrowed frame = can correctly judge/route but not author.
## Clay-experience anchors to capture
Prompts for Clay to mine his 30-year engineering/founder history:
- **Onboarding that transferred vs. that didn't:** Which engineers Clay onboarded actually internalized the codebase/judgment, and which became permanently dependent on being told? What did the transferring cases have in common — did he *withhold* answers and make them struggle (fading), or hand them working code (substitution)?
- **His own model/scaffold/fade instances:** Concrete stories where Clay demonstrated his reasoning aloud (modeling), then progressively stepped back. Where did he fade too fast (learner floundered) or too slow (learner stayed dependent)?
- **Complacency near-misses:** Times Clay or a team rubber-stamped output (a review, a deploy, a vendor's work) because it was "usually right" — and what structural gate he wishes had existed. Maps directly to Module 3.
- **The "borrow-the-frame" calls:** Decisions Clay had to *stand behind* without being able to author them (a security architecture, a legal/financial call, a specialist's recommendation). How did he get *just enough* frame to be answerable? This is the template for borrow-the-frame teaching.
- **Self-assessment failures he's witnessed:** Junior (or senior) engineers who were confidently wrong about their own competence — evidence for trailing-signal assessment over self-report.
- **What "originating" felt like vs. delegating the middle:** Examples where Clay clearly held the front (what to build) and back (answerability) while handing off the producible middle to a contractor/junior/tool — and when that worked vs. when handing off the middle quietly ate the answerable half.
## Caveats and reliability gradient
- **Build doctrine on (robust, mechanism-level, peer-reviewed):** cognitive apprenticeship (Collins/Brown/Newman); Bjork's desirable difficulties + generation/testing effects; Parasuraman & Manzey 2010 complacency/bias; Endsley out-of-the-loop; Barnett & Ceci transfer taxonomy; Dreyfus stages (as descriptive framework); Dunning-Kruger (as "self-report is unreliable," noting post-2020 methodological debate about its exact functional form).
- **Cite with date and explicit caveats (strong but new/narrow):** Anthropic RCT (n=52, <1 hr, immediate comprehension only, chat-based GPT-4o, interaction patterns are associations not causation); METR (n=16, productivity not skill, experts on familiar code, early-2025 tools, abandoned follow-up); Microsoft/CMU (self-report, correlational).
- **Use only as color, flag as weak/correlational:** Google effect/digital amnesia, GPS atrophy, Gerlich critical-thinking correlation, vendor Copilot statistics.
- **Tensions to keep visible, not paper over:**
  1. *Productivity vs. skill:* AI can speed mastered work (Anthropic's own observational 80% finding; Copilot/novice gains on simple tasks) while impeding new-skill formation and even slowing experts on complex familiar code (METR). The regime (novice/new vs expert/familiar; simple vs complex) determines the sign.
  2. *Self-report direction:* "Self-report lies" is correct that in-the-moment confidence is an unreliable instrument — but the failure direction varies (METR devs over-credited AI; Anthropic learners *under*-rated their own learning). Doctrine should say "instrument the trailing behavioral signal," not "people always overestimate."
  3. *Deliberate-practice magnitude:* the framework is sound but the Macnamara & Maitra (2019) replication shrank the effect — don't overclaim that practice hours determine expertise.
  4. *Far transfer is hard:* don't assume a faculty learned in one context generalizes; teach abstract principles explicitly and vary practice.
- **Open questions the evidence does not resolve:** whether the Anthropic comprehension gap predicts *long-term* skill deficits; whether the effect dissipates as engineers gain fluency; whether AI assistance differs from human assistance during learning; and whether agentic tools (expected to be worse) can be configured into "learning modes" (Claude Code Learning/Explanatory; ChatGPT Study Mode) that genuinely preserve faculty formation rather than merely re-skinning delegation.
