# Condition: the only variable left
 
*Companion to the previous unit. That one established that there is a* you *who originates and answers. This one is about the condition of that* you *— and why, once the agent is consistent, your condition is the production input that decides everything else.*
 
The agent brings its best game every time. Not out of discipline — it has no other game to bring. Give it working infrastructure (hardware up, APIs responsive, rate limits clear) and it returns the same caliber of work at 3 a.m. as at 10, on its thousandth request as on its first. Whatever variance it has lives outside itself, in the infrastructure. Keep the infrastructure up and the machine is, for your purposes, a constant.
 
You are not a constant.
 
You have weather. You sleep badly and your working memory narrows. You carry an argument from this morning into an afternoon of decisions. You're three weeks into money you don't have, and every call starts looking short-term. You're inflamed, depleted, grieving, or just flat, and the work still has your name on it. None of this shows up as an error code. It shows up as worse judgment that feels exactly like normal judgment.
 
Here's why that matters more now than it ever did, not less.
 
## The variance got concentrated, not removed
 
In the old world you spread your cognition across hundreds of small acts of production a day — naming things, wiring things, fixing the obvious. A bad day degraded all of it a little, and most of it was self-correcting: the test failed, the build broke, the teammate caught it. Your condition mattered, but it was *diluted* across a high volume of low-stakes acts, and the system had a hundred chances to catch a slip.
 
The agent took the volume. What's left to you is exactly the two ends — origination at the front, the answerable half at the back — and those ends are judgment-dense and low-volume. You make far fewer moves, and each one carries far more.
 
So your condition stopped being diluted. It now flows almost undiluted into the handful of calls that decide whether the whole effort was worth making. A degraded you no longer ships visibly worse code — the agent handles the code. A degraded you originates the wrong problem, accepts a recommendation you should have overridden, and skims the diff you were supposed to be answerable for. The failure moved from the *visible* (bad output) to the *invisible* (bad judgment). And bad judgment does not announce itself.
 
## You can't feel it at the moment you'd need to
 
The machine fails loudly. Rate-limited, it errors. Down, it returns nothing. You always know.
 
Your degradation is silent, and worse than silent — it degrades the very faculty you'd use to detect it. The tired mind reports that it's thinking clearly. The frightened mind reports that it's being prudent. This is the drunk-driver problem applied to cognition: the impairment and your read on the impairment fail together. Which means in-the-moment self-assessment is the one tool you can't trust at the moment you'd reach for it.
 
That has a hard consequence. You cannot manage your condition by checking how you feel right before a big decision. You manage it the way you manage infrastructure: as a standing discipline, maintained on a schedule, monitored by something other than the impaired instrument itself. The machine has health checks and uptime monitoring for exactly this reason. So do you — and you're the only one who runs them.
 
## Where each dimension hits the loop
 
This is the part that usually turns into a wellness list, and a wellness list would be useless here. So tie each one to the specific place it breaks the work.
 
- **Physical.** Sleep and energy are the substrate the judgment runs on. The specific failure of the tired brain is that it *accepts the plausible default* — and the agent's entire output is plausible defaults. Rested, you catch that the clean-looking migration silently drops a uniqueness constraint. Exhausted, you see green tests and ship it. Physical state here isn't about stamina for long hours; it's about whether the override still fires.
- **Cognitive.** Focus and working memory are what let you *hold a frame* — and Think is the half where you own the frame. A scattered mind can't keep the frame in view, so it gets pulled into whatever the agent just surfaced, optimizing hard inside a frame it never checked.
- **Emotional.** Regulation is what lets you sit in an unfinished thing and say "not yet." Dysregulated, you rubber-stamp the recommendation because you're sick of the problem, or reject a good one because it dented your ego. The answerable half requires tolerating discomfort on purpose; emotion is what funds that tolerance or bankrupts it.
- **Spiritual.** Call it whatever you like — your live connection to what's worth doing. It's the home of origination. Depleted here, you don't make worse choices; you stop choosing. You drift into whatever the feed, the market, or the agent's next suggestion puts in front of you. The previous unit said origination atrophies from disuse. This is the disuse: a self with nothing it's reaching for has no direction to point the work, so it lets the work point it.
- **Financial.** This one is sharper than the rest, because the previous unit used scarcity as the *reason* origination matters — your time is finite, you're the one with something at risk. But the same scarcity, past a threshold, corrupts the judgment it's meant to sharpen. Some stake makes you care. Desperation makes you stupid: it collapses your time horizon, makes you answerable to the wrong master, and quietly rewrites "it chose me" as "I chose this." A frightened person rationalizes the engagement they shouldn't take and the cutover they shouldn't ship — and does it with a clean-feeling conscience, because fear is good at sounding like prudence. Financial precarity puts a thumb on the scale of every *did I choose this* question.
The list isn't exhaustive, and the dimensions don't stay in their lanes — bad sleep wrecks regulation, money fear wrecks sleep. The point isn't the taxonomy. The point is that every one of them lands on the *same two ends*, origination and the answerable half, because that's all that's left to land on.
 
## You are infrastructure
 
Put it the way the work already taught you to think. You would never run the agent on degraded infrastructure and trust the output. You'd fix the infrastructure first, because you know the output is only as good as what it runs on.
 
You are the infrastructure for the two halves that can't be handed off. Your condition is not a personal aside to the engineering; it *is* an engineering input, upstream of every origination and every override. Maintaining it is maintaining the system.
 
The difference is that the machine's infrastructure has someone maintaining it. Yours doesn't. There's no SRE paged when your judgment goes down, no dashboard that turns red before you ship the thing you'll regret. You are on call for your own uptime, and the monitoring has to be built deliberately — because the default, checking how you feel in the moment, is the one reading you've already established you can't trust.
 
So the loop gets a question before its other two, asked not at the moment of decision but as the standing condition for being fit to decide at all:
 
> **Am I in shape to originate and to answer — or am I about to let a degraded instrument make the only calls that were ever mine to make?**
 
## Why this sits next to origination
 
The previous unit ended on the point that if you lose origination and the answerable half, there's no longer a *you* to keep viable. This is the other face of it. There can be a you — present, holding both ends — that is nonetheless in no condition to use them well. An unmaintained instrument and an abdicated one fail the same calls; they just fail them differently.
 
It's also why the nine questions of the inquiry presuppose more than a self that's still choosing — they presuppose a self in shape to choose well. *Am I getting sharper, or duller?* is a question about condition over time. *What happens when models exercise my judgment?* assumes the judgment is worth exercising. *Does the time I save actually accrue to me?* only pays off if the saved time is reinvested in the instrument rather than spent straight past it. Becoming viable was always going to require staying in form. The agent will keep bringing its best game. The whole question of the work is whether you bring yours.
