# The Six Audiences (Canonical Definitions)

*This file is the single source of truth for the six audiences the Viable Coder playbook and website serve. Every other artifact that names an audience — the website's mentorship page, the audience research plan, the relocation ledger, the module-skill crosswalk, the Unit 2 outlines, the granular and narrative drafts, the research streams — defers to this file. If a definition here changes, the downstream artifacts named at the bottom must change with it.*

A note on naming: this project uses **"audience"** authorially (the buildership the kit is built for) and **"situation"** builder-facingly (what the builder recognizes as their own spot). They refer to the same six things. Codes — ITW, SVD, EDD, LSD, AID, PPW — are backstage shorthand for matrices, crosswalks, and module pairings; they do not appear in builder-facing prose.

Each audience below carries:
- **Canonical statement.** The one-paragraph definition that governs every other artifact. Written for the builder, in the second person where appropriate.
- **Who recognizes themselves here.** The vivid identifying signals — names, tools, behaviors — that let a builder know this is their spot in the first paragraph.
- **What this audience is *not*.** The boundary that prevents confusion with adjacent audiences.
- **End held badly.** Origination, answerability, or both — the producible/answerable read that routes the kit.
- **Default register.** *Develop the faculty* vs. *borrow the frame* — the persona-overridable default for the audience's modules.

The persona overlay (first-time founder, repeat founder, career-changer, lead) and the wiring read (operating profile) compose with the audience to produce the delivered kit — **situation picks the module set; persona sets develop-vs-borrow; wiring tunes the delivery** — but those compositions are governed by separate artifacts (see [curriculum/modules/audience-research-plan.md](curriculum/modules/audience-research-plan.md) and [curriculum/modules/module-skill-crosswalk.md](curriculum/modules/module-skill-crosswalk.md)). This file fixes only the audiences.

## 1. Internal Talent Worth Developing (ITW)

**Canonical statement.** An individual — or a small team of change-makers — already inside your organization who could be developed into real product owners rather than replaced by hiring outside. The capability you would otherwise rent is sitting in people you already employ; the work is transferring real engineering and product faculty into them, not just running them through a course.

**Who recognizes themselves here.** A leader looking at one or two employees they keep thinking *"if only they could…"* and noticing the cost of bringing in outside hires when the right rawness is already in-house. The "small team of change-makers" framing fits because the developable cohort is rarely a single seat — it is the handful who would shift how the org actually builds, if they were given the depth. Bootcamps, programs, and disruptive offsites are the wrong shape; this audience needs apprenticeship that scaffolds the answerable end, not a syllabus.

**What this audience is *not*.** It is not "training the whole engineering org." It is not "hiring." It is not a generic learning-and-development initiative. The unit is *specific, developable people* — named individuals or a small named cohort — whom you can sponsor into real ownership.

**End held badly.** The organization holds both ends today and wants to transfer the engineering faculty into real people without losing the ownership in the handoff. The teaching is what makes the end stick.

**Default register.** *Develop the faculty, fully.* This is the most teaching-heavy audience in the playbook.

## 2. SaaS Vendor Dependency (SVD)

**Canonical statement.** The operational stack your business runs on is rented from SaaS vendors — at rising prices, with a fit that never quite matches your workflow. The question is which slices of that stack you should bring in-house now that an owner with agents can build and run what you used to subscribe to, and which slices you should keep renting because they are commodities you should never have wanted to own.

**Who recognizes themselves here.** An operator or non-technical founder reviewing the monthly SaaS line items and noticing two patterns: the bill is creeping, and the tools never quite fit. The first agent-built internal tool that quietly replaces a subscription is the moment this audience realizes the kit is real — and the maintenance burden of the thing they just built is the moment they realize the trade is too.

**What this audience is *not*.** It is not a single build-vs-buy call on one tool — it is the *stack*, plural, with judgment running across multiple vendors. It is not a procurement exercise. And it is not the audience that wants to build everything from scratch out of suspicion of vendors; it is the audience that wants to *choose deliberately* what to own and what to rent.

**End held badly.** Both ends sit with the vendor for the slice of operations in question. Bringing it home means deciding what is differentiator vs. commodity, then holding both ends of whatever comes home.

**Default register.** *Borrow the frame, minimally.* Enough engineering literacy to own and run what was rented — not a full engineering faculty.

## 3. External Dev Dependency (EDD)

**Canonical statement.** Your product is built by an outside agency or contractor. You pay for development without owning the capability — and the people who originate decisions about your software and answer for its quality work for someone else. The work is moving origination and answerability back in-house without stalling the product on the way.

**Who recognizes themselves here.** A founder who can direct technical work but cannot independently change, audit, or extend it. The recognition moment is usually a feature the agency pushes back on, a roadmap stretch quoted at a number that doesn't match the founder's intuition, or a handover conversation that surfaces how much tribal knowledge lives outside the company.

**What this audience is *not*.** It is not "I have a CTO and want to fire them." It is not "I want to micromanage my engineers." And it is not "I want to learn to code so I never need an agency again" — the kit may or may not produce that, depending on persona; the audience definition does not assume it.

**End held badly.** Origination is partial — the agency translates your direction into product calls you do not see. Answerability is largely external — they ship, you accept. Both move back in-house under this kit.

**Default register.** *Borrow the frame* for the founder by default; *develop the faculty* if there is internal talent to receive it (in which case the kit composes with ITW).

## 4. Legacy Software Debt (LSD)

**Canonical statement.** Mission-critical software the original developers built years ago, and nobody currently on the team fully understands. You answer for it — uptime, change, security — but you cannot hold the frame, because the frame is missing, not abdicated. The work is building real comprehension before any change, then deciding what to modernize, what to leave alone, and what to replace.

**Who recognizes themselves here.** An operator or technical founder who has inherited a system through acquisition, succession, or attrition — the original authors gone, the documentation partial or absent, and the next change request waiting on a comprehension nobody on the team currently has. The recognition is usually fear-shaped: a ticket comes in for a change that should be small, and nobody can confidently say what depends on what.

**What this audience is *not*.** It is not "I have technical debt I'd like to clean up" — that's everyone. It is not greenfield with a few rough corners. The defining feature is that *the people who knew the system are gone* and the comprehension has to be rebuilt from the code itself.

**End held badly.** The answerable end is held — by responsibility, if nothing else. The frame is missing. The kit develops the faculty because borrowing isn't enough when the system is yours to maintain.

**Default register.** *Develop the faculty.*

---

## 5. AI Development Gap (AID)

**Canonical statement.** A developer team that has not made AI a deliberate, owned capability. This shows up in two shapes — sometimes both in the same organization. The first: a team that hasn't moved AI-native at all, through stalled adoption, evaluation paralysis, or simply not having started. The second: a team that *is* using AI but whom you fear is shipping code with it "blindly" — producing output nobody can fully verify or explain. Either way, the gap is the same: AI is not yet an owned capability, and the work is closing the distance between *AI in the workflow* and *AI as a faculty the team holds both ends of.*

**Who recognizes themselves here.** A leader sponsoring the team's AI posture and noticing one of two things: the team has not moved, or the team has moved but you cannot answer for what is shipping. The hype-fatigued team that has "tried Cursor a few times" and the velocity-drunk team that ships AI-generated PRs faster than anyone reads them are the *same audience* under this definition — both gaps, both kits, one register.

**What this audience is *not*.** It is not a team that has deliberately and competently adopted AI-native engineering with verification practices intact — that team is not in a gap, even if they are still improving. The defining feature is the *ownership shortfall*, in either direction (not started, or started without owning the answerable half).

**End held badly.** If pre-adoption: both ends, but invisibly — the team has not yet met the gap. If post-adoption: the producible middle flows; the answerable back end is unheld (verification debt). Same kit reaches both: spec-driven development, structural review capacity, the vibe-to-engineering ladder.

**Default register.** *Develop the faculty.* Closing the gap *is* building owned capability.

## 6. Prototype-to-Production Wall (PPW)

**Canonical statement.** Built something real on no-code, low-code, or AI-assisted tools — Lovable, Base44, Replit, and the like — got traction, and now facing the gap between *"this works"* and *"this holds up."* The prototype looks amazing in a demo and to early users. Scaling is not happening, not because the idea was bad, but because the system was never designed to hold the answerable half a product owes once real users depend on it.

**Who recognizes themselves here.** The enthusiastic founder who chose a no-code, low-code, or AI-assisted tool specifically because it appeared to remove engineering time and cost from the equation. They built it; it looks amazing; demand showed up; scaling isn't happening. The pattern is the engineering wall the [LinkedIn builder's-journey post](doctrine/voice/linkedin-builders-journey-ai-coding.md) names: founder judgment got them through the first gate, engineering judgment is the second gate, and the wall is what happens when the second gate gets skipped.

**What this audience is *not*.** It is not a traditional engineering team productionizing their own prototype — they have the engineering faculty, even if the prototype was rough. It is not a founder who hasn't shipped anything yet — there has to be traction (real users, real revenue, or both) for the wall to be present. The wall is specifically the gap between *the demo-quality artifact that earned the traction* and *the production-quality system the traction now requires.*

**End held badly.** Origination happened, and the thing exists. The answerable back end — security, reliability, real data, observability, the non-functional registers — was never required by a prototype and is largely unheld.

**Default register.** *Develop the faculty* on the non-functional surface; *borrow the frame* where the founder will rely on agents or specialists.
