# Reader Orientation Pattern

This doctrine defines how the playbook should teach major ideas without becoming either abstract exposition or decorative memoir.

The governing pattern is:

**Scene -> Tension -> Distinction -> Tool -> Return**

Use it as a reader-orientation rhythm, not as a visible formula. The point is to organize material in felt-need order: the reader should meet the human situation before they are asked to care about the framework.

## The Pattern

**Scene**  
Open from a concrete human situation: Clay's lived source, a founder decision, an engineering failure mode, a team behavior, a dependency trade, or a reader's ordinary working day.

**Tension**  
Name the pressure that makes the situation matter. The tension should involve risk, confusion, consequence, speed, misdirection, abdication, scarcity, review burden, dependency, or long-term drift.

**Distinction**  
Introduce the Viable Coder idea that clarifies the scene: the two ends, the producible/answerable split, can-vs-should, develop-vs-borrow, relocation, condition, observability, upkeep, ADLC gates, transfer-vs-substitution, or a long-clock dimension.

**Tool**  
Give the reader a usable move: a gate, diagnostic, ledger, module, agent pattern, prompt discipline, review practice, signal, or exercise.

**Return**  
Come back to the opening scene or situation with changed understanding. The return is where the reader sees what the distinction and tool now make possible.

## Relationship To Seed -> Bloom -> Harvest

Seed -> bloom -> harvest is the book-level teaching architecture. The reader-orientation pattern is the chapter- and module-level rhythm inside that architecture.

- A seed may appear as a scene, question, or small distinction.
- A bloom should attach the distinction to a real task, decision, or gate.
- A harvest should return to the earlier situation as a signal, measurement, or long-clock consequence.

The two patterns should reinforce each other. A chapter can orient the reader locally while the section architecture plants, blooms, and harvests the same idea across the whole book.

## Use Across The Four Sections

Section 1 should use scene and tension to install the stance. Personal material belongs here when it is structural evidence for origination, answerability, ownership, and the cost of error.

Section 2 should use reader situations to locate the person before prescribing modules. The scene should expose which end the reader holds badly, which capability they need to develop, or which frame they need to borrow.

Section 3 should use build situations and ADLC handoffs. The scene should show where speed, agent output, review capacity, or condition creates a decision that needs a gate.

Section 4 should return earlier scenes as trend questions. The scene should now be readable as a long-clock signal: what is happening to the person, practice, or conditions over time?

## Guardrails

- Do not use story as ornament. A scene must carry a doctrine, gate, module, diagnostic, or signal.
- Do not turn Clay's personal material into sentimental memoir. Use it as structural evidence for why the doctrine matters.
- Do not force every subsection into the pattern. Apply it to major reader-facing units: chapters, section openings, major modules, and exercises where orientation matters.
- Do not let the tool arrive without the tension. A practice handed to the reader before they feel the risk will read as procedure.
- Do not let the scene replace the tool. The playbook should move from recognition to action.
- Preserve the four-section architecture. This pattern changes the teaching rhythm inside the pillars; it does not replace the pillars.

## Editorial Test

Before approving a major chapter, module, or exercise, ask:

- What scene or reader situation creates the felt need?
- What tension makes the idea matter?
- Which Viable Coder distinction clarifies the tension?
- What practical move does the reader receive?
- Where does the piece return to the opening situation with changed understanding?
