# The Relocation Lens: Every Escape Is a Trade
## Stream E Module-Ready Synthesis for *The Viable Coder Playbook* (Clay Turner, viablecoder.com)
 
> **What this stream is.** The cross-cutting dynamic that threads all six audience situations: no move sheds risk; it only changes risk's shape. It follows straight from the spine — *the human bears all the risk; the machine bears none* — so insourcing, replacing, or modernizing never *removes* a dependency, it **relocates** it. The teachable reframe: escaping dependency was never the goal; *choosing which dependency is worth holding* is — origination applied to the dependency itself, the *Did I choose this, or did it choose me?* test pointed at the trade. Per the seed→bloom→harvest plan, this stream **blooms** the lens in Section 2 (where the trades are actually chosen) and only **names** the nine dimensions per situation, reserving their definitions for Section 4.
 
---
 
## TL;DR
 
- **"Risk relocates, it doesn't vanish" is not a metaphor — it is the settled core of formal risk management.** Every standard framework (ISO 31000, COSO ERM, the PMI four responses) draws the same line the playbook draws: you can *avoid, mitigate, transfer, or accept* a risk, but **transfer "does not eliminate the risk itself; it merely reallocates who pays for it"** (InsureTutor on the four responses; NIST via ScienceDirect: "risk transference does not reduce the likelihood, harm, or risk"). What remains after any treatment is *residual risk*, and treating one risk routinely manufactures another ("the avoidance of one risk can result in other unknown risks"). Clay's spine sharpens this into something stricter than the textbooks: for the founder, the parties you can "transfer" to — an agency, a SaaS vendor, an agent — never relieve you of answerability. The risk doesn't just move between parties; it **always lands back on the human.** The only real decision is *which dependency you choose to hold.*
- **Economics already proved that the firm's boundary is a choice of dependency, never an escape from one.** Transaction-cost economics (Coase 1937; Williamson) shows the make-or-buy decision is governed by **asset specificity, frequency, and uncertainty**, and that relationship-specific investment creates the **hold-up problem** — and crucially that lock-in is **bilateral**: even the party that *switches* carries exit costs (Joskow, Klein). Shapiro & Varian's *Information Rules* (1999) gives the mechanism a price tag: an incumbent can extract a premium up to the customer's **total switching cost**, so the dependency you're "escaping" was always charging you exactly what it cost to leave. There is no dependency-free state; there is only a portfolio of dependencies you chose well or badly.
- **The "agency-to-agents" trade is the relocation lens at its sharpest, and 2026 makes the new dependency concrete and dated.** Fire the outside shop, adopt agents, and you trade relationship/contract/margin risk for **model-capability and lock-in risk** — now measurable: model lifecycles compressed from ~18–24 months to **6–12** (Presenc AI tracker, May 2026); GPT-4o retired April 3 2026, Claude 3.7 Sonnet shut down May 11 2026; Anthropic's table lists `claude-opus-4-20250514` deprecated April 14 2026 / retired June 15 2026 — **62 days** (TianPan.co). Capability *drift* is the silent half: a replacement model can fail ~12% of your eval suite, discovered "on day 28 of a 60-day sunset window." Your prompts and evals are **relationship-specific assets** — Williamson's hold-up problem in a new costume. The new failure mode the playbook should name: the new dependency is **felt late**, which is exactly "self-report lies" pointed at a balance sheet.
## Key Findings
 
**1. Robust / definitional (safe to build doctrine on):**
- *Risk treatment never reaches zero; it produces residual risk.* The four-response taxonomy (avoid/mitigate/transfer/accept) is common to ISO 31000 and COSO ERM. Transfer reallocates **who pays**, not whether the loss can occur. This is the rigorous, uncontested backbone of the relocation lens.
- *The make-or-buy boundary is a dependency choice.* Transaction-cost economics — "one of the best-developed parts of the new institutional economics" (Springer, *Make-or-Buy Revisited*) — locates the decision in asset specificity, frequency, and uncertainty, and names the **hold-up problem** and **maladaptation costs** that any relocation incurs.
- *Lock-in is bilateral and priced.* Switching costs are borne by both sides; the incumbent's extractable premium ≈ the customer's total switching cost (Shapiro & Varian, 1999; Farrell & Klemperer on switching-cost competition). "Look ahead and reason back" is their explicit buyer's discipline.
**2. Strong but contested / use with a caveat:**
- *Risk compensation / risk homeostasis* (Wilde 1982, 1998; the **Peltzman effect**, 1975). Powerful as an *intuition pump* — make something feel safer and behavior often expands to spend the slack — but the **strong** homeostatic claim (that total risk is held constant, like a thermostat) is empirically disputed. Use the **weak, well-supported** version (interventions trigger compensating behavior and relocate risk) and do **not** claim risk is conserved at a fixed level.
**3. Conceptual / normative (a lens, not a finding — flag as such):**
- *Iatrogenics & naive interventionism* (Taleb, *Antifragile*, 2012). The disciplined name for "the new dependency you only saw later": harm from intervention, arriving as **second- and third-order effects**, hidden by **time delay** and by the interventionist's **distance from consequences / no skin in the game**. This is a popular synthesis, not peer-reviewed mechanism — cite it as a framing device, and note that its "skin in the game" point is *the playbook's own spine* arriving from another direction.
**4. Point-in-time (cite with date; will go stale):**
- The 2026 model-deprecation calendar and capability-drift specifics below. True as of mid-2026; the *pattern* (compressing lifecycles, drift across versions, prompt/eval scaffolding as a sunk asset) is durable, the *dates and model names* are not.

## Details
 
### Topic 1 — The relocation principle: the spine, turned into a lens
 
The playbook's keystone — *the human bears all the risk; the machine bears none* — is usually heard as a claim about the human/agent boundary. Stream E turns it outward, into a claim about **every** boundary the founder draws: in-house vs. outside shop, build vs. rent, modernize vs. replace, agents vs. employees. The claim generalizes cleanly, because risk management already established the general form.
 
**The textbook version.** Every mature framework — ISO 31000, COSO ERM, the PMI/PMBOK responses — offers the same four moves on a risk: **avoid** (don't run the activity), **mitigate/reduce** (lower probability or impact), **transfer/share** (shift who bears the loss), **accept** (carry the residual knowingly). The load-bearing fact for Stream E is what these sources say about transfer, in almost identical language across independent sources:
 
- "Transfer does not eliminate the risk itself; it merely reallocates who pays for it." (InsureTutor, four-response guide)
- "Risk transference does not reduce the likelihood, harm, or risk associated with an event, but typically compensates the organization for losses." (NIST, via ScienceDirect)
- And on avoidance specifically: "The avoidance of one risk can result in other unknown risks or a far greater consequence." (ProjectPractical, PMP guide)
Whatever you do, something is **left over** — the *residual risk* — and the act of treating one risk tends to *spawn* another. Insurance is the cleanest illustration: buying a policy doesn't stop your house from burning; it converts a low-probability catastrophic loss into a certain, smaller premium-and-deductible — a *different* risk you now hold on purpose. Outsourcing a risky process to a specialist vendor is the same move: the original hazard is reshaped into vendor, contract, and dependency hazards.
 
**The Clay version (where the lens earns its place).** Standard risk management assumes a world of parties who can genuinely bear risk: insurers price it, counterparties assume it, the market clears it. The founder's world is stricter on two counts.
 
First, per the spine, the human bears *all* of it. You can transfer the **producible middle** of a task to an agency or an agent, but you cannot transfer the **answerable half** — you remain the party held to the result at 2 a.m. So for the founder, risk doesn't merely *reallocate among parties*; it **relocates in shape but returns in full to you.** The agency can be fired; you still answer for the product it shipped. The model can be deprecated; you still answer for the feature it wrote. This is why "the machine bears none" is not a complaint about machines — it's a statement that **there is no counterparty to genuinely offload to.** Every transfer is, at best, a reshaping.
 
Second, this means the only honest framing of any escape is not *"how do I get rid of this dependency?"* but *"which dependency do I want to be holding when this is over?"* That is **origination pointed at the trade** — the *Did I choose this, or did it choose me?* test applied not to the product but to the dependency itself. The tone the plan insists on matters here: this is **clear-eyed choosing, not nihilism.** "It's all dependencies, why bother" is the failure. "I am trading *up* to a dependency worth holding" is the stance.
 
**The intuition pump, used carefully.** Risk *compensation* — Sam Peltzman's 1975 finding that auto-safety regulation prompted more aggressive driving, and Gerald Wilde's broader *risk homeostasis theory* (1982, 1998) — is the vivid version: make a system feel safer and people often spend the new slack on more risk, so the hazard re-emerges elsewhere (more aggressive driving, fewer precautions). It's a useful way to explain to a founder *why* the relief from escaping a dependency so often gets spent immediately on a new exposure. **But flag the limit honestly:** the strong homeostatic claim that total risk returns to a fixed setpoint is contested and was never cleanly verified experimentally (Wilde himself catalogues the dissent). Use the weak claim — *relief invites compensating behavior; risk relocates* — and never assert that risk is conserved at a constant level. Overclaiming here would force *Remaining Viable* to apologize for it later.
 
### Topic 2 — Why every escape manufactures a dependency: the economics of the boundary
 
The reason no situation's "fix" produces a dependency-free state is not motivational; it's structural, and economics named the structure decades ago.
 
**Make-or-buy is the original relocation decision.** Ronald Coase ("The Nature of the Firm," 1937; "The Problem of Social Cost," 1960) asked why firms exist at all rather than buying everything on the market, and answered: because *using the market has costs* — search, negotiation, enforcement. Oliver Williamson built this into **transaction-cost economics**, identifying three determinants of whether a transaction is best done **inside** the firm (make) or **outside** (buy): **asset specificity, frequency, and uncertainty** (Williamson 1981; the make-or-buy literature is empirically "one of the best-developed parts of the new institutional economics"). Each of the six situations is a make-or-buy decision in disguise — and TCE's whole point is that there is *no free option*, only a trade between two cost structures.
 
**Asset specificity is why the trade locks you in.** Williamson distinguished four kinds of relationship-specific investment — **site, physical-asset, human-asset, and dedicated** specificity (Wikipedia/Williamson 1983). The more you invest in assets that are valuable *only* in this relationship, the more you're exposed to the **hold-up problem**: once the specific investment is sunk, your counterparty can try to "expropriate the rents" — renegotiate against you, because your next-best alternative is now much worse (Tadelis & Williamson). This is the precise mechanism behind every "we can't leave our vendor" story.
 
**And the lock-in is bilateral — which is the relocation lens stated in economics.** Critically, Joskow and Klein observed that **even the party that switches carries exit costs**: "the buyer (the party that does not hold the specific assets) has exit cost associated with time and searching investment if he decides to switch party" (Asset specificity, Wikipedia). There is no clean exit; switching *is itself* a costly, dependency-creating act. You don't escape the hold-up — you move to a new counterparty and begin accruing specificity to *them*.
 
**Shapiro & Varian put a price on it.** In *Information Rules* (1999), the canonical result: a customer's value to an incumbent ≈ that customer's **total switching cost** (the costs borne by the customer *plus* those borne by any new supplier to win them). The incumbent can charge a premium up to that amount, because no rival can profitably beat it by less. Translated for the founder: **the rent your current dependency extracts is, by construction, roughly what it would cost you to leave it.** Their seven canonical lock-in types map onto the six situations almost directly — contractual commitments, durable purchases, brand-specific *training* (human-asset specificity), information and databases, specialized suppliers, search costs, and loyalty programs. Their buyer's discipline is the one Stream E should hand the reader: **"look ahead and reason back,"** dual-source to limit vulnerability, and **bargain for exit terms at the beginning**, when you still have leverage — not after the specificity is sunk.
 
The teachable synthesis: **the boundary of your company is not a wall against dependency; it is a dial that selects which dependency you hold.** Insourcing trades market/relationship risk for coordination, capability, and maintenance risk. Outsourcing trades the reverse. TCE tells you which way to turn the dial (high specificity + frequency + uncertainty → make; low → buy), but it never offers a setting labeled "no dependency."
 
### Topic 3 — The agency-to-agents trade, specifically (the agent era's version)
 
This is the relocation that most distinguishes the AI-native playbook from a generic build-vs-buy chapter, and it's the one the plan calls out by name. When a founder fires the agency (or never hires one) and stands up an agent team, here is the trade, ledger-style.
 
**What you shed:** the agency's margin; communication overhead and context loss across the relay; misaligned incentives (they bill hours; you want outcomes); the "you don't own what you can't change" trap of code you didn't commission; scheduling and capacity constraints of a human shop.
 
**What you take on — and this is the part founders price at zero and discover later:**
 
1. **The deprecation treadmill.** Model lifecycles have compressed from the old ~18–24-month support windows to roughly **6–12 months** (Presenc AI deprecation tracker, snapshot May 15 2026). Concrete, dated examples as of mid-2026: GPT-4o retired April 3 2026; Claude 3.7 Sonnet shut down May 11 2026; Claude 3.5 Haiku July 5 2026; OpenAI's Assistants API removed Aug 26 2026 (a structural break forcing a rebuild to the Responses API); GPT-4.1's final cutoff Oct 14 2026. Anthropic's own deprecation table lists `claude-opus-4-20250514` as deprecated April 14 2026 and retired June 15 2026 — a **62-day** window (TianPan.co, *Model Deprecation Treadmill*). Notice windows vary: Anthropic commits to a minimum of 60 days on public models; OpenAI's range from months to, in some ChatGPT cases, **as little as two weeks** in practice. *The dependency you "escaped" had quarterly invoices; the one you adopted sends 60-day sunset emails.*
2. **Capability drift (the silent half).** The headline danger is retirement; the quieter one is that the **replacement behaves differently.** The cautionary pattern from the field: a team runs its eval suite against the current model, and discovers "on day 28 of a 60-day sunset window, that the replacement model fails 12% of the eval cases" (TianPan.co). Capability mostly rises — but *behavior changes*, and your prompts were tuned to the old behavior. Which is the entry point to the deepest part of the trade:
3. **Your prompts, evals, and agent scaffolding are relationship-specific assets.** This is Williamson's **human-asset and physical-asset specificity** wearing a 2026 costume. The prompt library, the `CLAUDE.md`/`AGENTS.md` config, the Skills that encode your doctrine, the eval harness — all of it is **dedicated investment** tuned to a particular model's behavior. When the model is deprecated, that specificity is partly stranded; you are *held up* by the migration. The defense the literature already prescribes is dual-sourcing in modern dress: **abstraction layers / model routers** so call sites aren't hard-pinned, **model-family-aware** rather than version-pinned references, and an **n+1 eval** run *before* the sunset lands (Syncfusion; TianPan.co). This is Shapiro & Varian's "limit your vulnerability" and "look ahead and reason back," applied to model risk.
4. **A new threat surface.** Per the Section 3 research, agents *expand* the threat model even as they help defend it (e.g., the prompt-injection pathway found in a Claude Code GitHub Action that could reach workflow secrets; AI-generated code carrying materially more OWASP-class issues). The agency had a security posture you could audit in a contract; the agent introduces failure modes you now own.
5. **Review capacity.** Cross-referenced with Stream D: agents open work faster than a human can answer for it. Firing the agency removes their QA; you absorb it. The answerable back end becomes the binding constraint — *condition replaces time* made visible.
**The honest verdict for the module:** the agency-to-agents trade is very often the right one — the upside is real (it's the opportunity the whole book is about). But it is a **trade up to a new dependency, not an escape from dependency.** A founder who books only the shed costs and zeros the adopted ones has not made an origination decision; they've made the *unexamined* version the plan warns against — responding to what the tool surfaced rather than choosing. The relocation lens forces both columns onto the page.
 
### Topic 4 — The dependency you only saw later: second-order effects and the worked example
 
The plan's Clay-anchor — *"a time you traded one dependency for another and only saw the new one later"* — points at a specific, nameable failure mode, and there's a clean conceptual frame plus a clean real-world case for it.
 
**The frame: iatrogenics.** Nassim Taleb's term in *Antifragile* (2012) for harm caused by the intervention itself — "the (usually delayed) damage from treatment in excess of the benefits." Taleb's diagnosis of *why* well-intentioned people keep intervening into systems they don't fully understand is exactly the founder's relocation trap (Farnam Street's gloss): (1) **failure to think through second- and third-order consequences** — "most things in life happen at the second, third, or nth steps"; (2) **distance from consequences / time delay** — when the new dependency's cost arrives months later, it's hard to connect it to the decision, and easy to keep believing the decision was clean; (3) a **bias for action** — "something must be done"; and (4) **no skin in the game.** Note the fourth point is *the playbook's own spine arriving from the opposite direction*: the reason the founder, uniquely, must run this analysis is that the founder is the one party *with* skin in the game — the agency, the vendor, and the agent all advise the trade without bearing its delayed cost. Taleb's prescription — **non-naive interventionism**, intervene only when the benefit is *orders of magnitude* clear — is precisely "trade up to a dependency worth holding," not "never trade."
 
**Caveat on the frame:** Taleb is a persuasive synthesis, not a peer-reviewed mechanism. Use it as vocabulary and as a memorable handle, not as evidence. The *evidence* for "interventions spawn delayed second-order costs" is the boring, robust stuff in Topics 1–2 (residual risk; maladaptation costs; bilateral switching costs).
 
**The worked example: cloud repatriation (37signals).** This is the relocation lens in a single, well-documented, dated case the module can use verbatim. Starting October 2022, 37signals (Basecamp, HEY; CTO David Heinemeier Hansson) left AWS/Google Cloud, citing a cloud bill that had grown past **$3.2M/year** for predictable workloads. The shed dependency was real and the savings were real: a ~$600–700K one-time hardware purchase, cloud spend down roughly 60% the first year (~$2M/year saved), with projected savings of $10M+ over five years, and in 2025–2026 they completed the exit by migrating ~18 PB off S3 (The Register; DCD; Inspectural, 2025–2026).
 
But the relocation is the whole point, and the *adopted* dependency is partly hidden in the headline number. As Data Center Dynamics noted plainly, the savings figures "don't take into account the costs of hardware refreshes, additional operations roles that have to be created as a result of bringing hardware back into 37signals' remit, and the cost of running data centers in general in terms of power, cooling, etc." They traded **vendor/pricing/lock-in risk** for **capacity-planning, hardware-refresh, operations-staffing, and capability risk** — and they had to *develop a new faculty to hold it* (they built and open-sourced Kamal, a deployment tool, to make owned infra manageable). Whether the trade was *good* (for them, with predictable workloads and deep ops talent, almost certainly yes) is a separate question from whether it was *a trade* (unavoidably). The module's teachable move: 37signals booked **both** columns and chose deliberately — which is exactly the discipline, and exactly why their result held up. The cautionary inverse is the founder who repatriates expecting only the savings line and meets the ops-staffing line a year later, as a surprise.
 
### Topic 5 — Pricing the trade so it's legible *before* it's made
 
The plan asks specifically for *"how to price a dependency you're taking on against the one you're shedding, so the trade is legible before it's made."* The instruments already exist; the module's job is to assemble them into one ledger.
 
- **Total cost of ownership, both columns.** The shed dependency's cost is usually visible (the invoice, the margin, the rent). The adopted dependency's cost is usually *latent*: maintenance burden, capability risk, migration/upgrade cost, the new skill you must develop or hire, and the residual risk you now hold knowingly. The 37signals lesson is to force the latent column onto the page — refresh cycles, ops roles, the tool you'll have to build.
- **Switching-cost asymmetry (Shapiro & Varian).** Price the *exit* cost of the dependency you're adopting *before* you adopt it — because the incumbent-to-be will eventually charge a premium up to that number, and your future self will be the one held up. Bargain for portability and exit terms *at the start* (data export, no proprietary formats, escape hatches), when leverage is highest.
- **Asset-specificity audit (Williamson).** Ask which of your investments will be *stranded* if this dependency changes or dies. Model-pinned prompts, a vendor's proprietary schema, an agency's undocumented tribal knowledge — these are the specific assets that create hold-up. Minimize specificity where you can (abstraction layers, standard formats, documentation requirements in the vendor contract); accept it only where the dependency is genuinely a *differentiator*.
- **Commodity vs. differentiator (Wardley mapping as the candidate lens — flag as such).** Simon Wardley's mapping places components on an evolution axis (genesis → custom-built → product → commodity) against a value chain. The strategic rule of thumb: **rent/buy the commodity, own/build the differentiator, and never custom-build what the market has already commoditized.** For the relocation decision this answers the *which dependency is worth holding* question directly: hold the dependencies that *distinguish you*; shed (or rent) the ones that don't. (Treat Wardley as a *useful candidate lens*, not a validated model — it's practitioner doctrine, widely used but not empirically established.)
- **Residual-risk statement.** Borrowed from formal risk management: after the trade, write one explicit sentence naming the risk you are *now choosing to hold* and why it's acceptable. This is the artifact that converts a hidden cost into a chosen one — the documentary form of *Did I choose this, or did it choose me?*
### Topic 6 — The relocation map across the six situations (name the axis only)
 
For each situation: what's **shed**, what's **adopted** (the new dependency), and which Section-4 dimension the trade **lights up** — named only, consistent with the pointers already in the research plan, deliberately not defined here (that's Section 4's harvest). The "which end" tags carry over from the plan.
 
- **1. Internal Talent Worth Developing (ITW)** — *shed:* dependence on hiring/contracting external capability. *Adopted:* the risk of developing people into **delegators who atrophy** rather than **inquirers who build real capability**, plus attrition risk (you develop them, they leave). *Dimension lit:* **Identity** and **Cognitive** — the trade for an internal capability is the long-run sharpness of your own bench.
- **2. SaaS Vendor Dependency (SVD)** — *shed:* perpetual rent and the vendor's control over a slice of operations. *Adopted:* the **maintenance burden** of the tool you now own and run, plus the model/build risk if it's agent-built. *Dimension lit:* **Temporal** and **Economic** — you trade a subscription for upkeep; the open question is whether the saved time and money actually accrue or get eaten by the burden you took on. (This is the cleanest SaaS-replacement case of the 37signals dynamic in miniature.)
- **3. External Dev Dependency (EDD)** — *shed:* the agency margin and the "you don't own what you can't change / can't see" trap. *Adopted:* in-house origination and answerability — and, once the same agents are available to everyone, the question of what's left that's yours. *Dimension lit:* **Differential** and **Economic** — when the margin is gone and the stack is commodity, the trade surfaces what distinguishes you from the next founder with the identical agents, and whether what you uniquely add still pays.
- **4. Legacy Software Debt (LSD)** — *shed:* a system you couldn't see, couldn't safely touch, and feared. *Adopted:* a modernization in which a **model now makes change decisions on your behalf** over code you still don't fully hold the frame on (and the METR brownfield result warns AI can *slow* you on mature code you know well). *Dimension lit:* **Judgment** — the trade buys comprehension at the cost of how much of the change-judgment is still yours.
- **5. AI Development Gap (AID)** — *shed:* the bottleneck of producing by hand. *Adopted:* the standing burden of **verification debt** — output you can ship but can't fully verify or explain. *Dimension lit:* **Epistemic** and **Cognitive** — you trade raw speed for the obligation to keep telling true from merely plausible as the volume grows.
- **6. Prototype-to-Production Wall (PPW)** — *shed:* the freedom of a prototype that never had to hold the answerable half. *Adopted:* accountability in **registers the prototype never owed** — real user data, security, reliability, the consequences that land on people once it's live. *Dimension lit:* **Ethical** — the trade for "it ships" is answering in registers you didn't previously owe.
The cross-cutting teaching point under all six: **each "fix" is a relocation, and the new dependency is the thing Section 4 will later measure.** Naming the axis here plants the seed; defining and instrumenting it is the harvest.

## Recommendations (module-build sequence)
 
Stream E feeds **one universal module** (the Relocation Lens sits in every audience's default set per the plan), built from four components. Sequence by leverage:
 
**Component 1 — The Relocation Ledger (build first).**
- *Guidance:* the spine-as-lens (Topic 1) + the two-column discipline. Every escape is a trade; book **both** columns or you haven't decided.
- *Exercise:* for the move under consideration, fill a two-column ledger — **Shed** (visible costs/risks leaving) vs. **Adopted** (latent costs/risks arriving: maintenance, capability, migration, new skill, residual risk). Force at least three entries in the *Adopted* column, because that's the one founders leave empty. Close with a one-sentence **residual-risk statement**.
- *Paired agent skill:* a "relocation auditor" that, given a proposed trade, surfaces the likely *adopted* dependencies the founder under-counted (drawing on the TCE/switching-cost/iatrogenics patterns) — and that **refuses to accept an empty Adopted column**. Tuned per audience: transfer the *reasoning* to develop-the-faculty audiences (explain *why* each adopted cost appears); produce the ledger more fully for borrow-the-frame audiences while still requiring the human to sign the residual-risk statement.
**Component 2 — "Trade up to a dependency worth holding" (the origination reframe).**
- *Guidance:* the can-vs-should gate (Stream A) pointed at the *dependency*, not the product — *Did I choose this dependency, or did it choose me?* Plus the commodity/differentiator rule (own the differentiator, rent the commodity).
- *Exercise:* classify the dependency you're adopting as **commodity** (rent it; minimize specificity; demand portability) or **differentiator** (own it; accept specificity deliberately). If you're about to *own a commodity* or *rent a differentiator*, stop and re-examine.
- *Paired agent skill:* a "why-this-dependency" critic that rejects "to escape the old one" as the sole rationale and demands a positive case for the new dependency on its own merits.
**Component 3 — The Agency-to-Agents Trade checklist (build third; flagship for EDD/AID, borrowable widely).**
- *Guidance:* Topic 3 — the specific shed/adopted ledger for replacing human dev capacity with agents, including the deprecation treadmill, capability drift, scaffolding-as-specific-asset, the new threat surface, and review capacity.
- *Exercise:* a pre-adoption hardening list — abstraction layer / model router in place; references model-family-aware not version-pinned; an n+1 eval run scheduled *ahead* of any sunset; data/prompt portability secured; review-capacity budgeted.
- *Paired agent skill:* a "deprecation watch" that tracks the sunset calendar for the models the stack depends on and runs the n+1 eval before the email lands — the **non-naive** version of intervention.
**Component 4 — The dimension-pointer map (Topic 6).** Ships as the bridge artifact: per situation, the shed/adopted/dimension-lit triple, **named only**, seeding Section 4. Keep definitions out; this is a pointer, not a payload.
 
**A library note for Clay:** per the plan, the Relocation Lens is already slated as a universal module. Stream E's strongest cross-references are **Stream A** (the can-vs-should gate it borrows), **Stream D** (review capacity as the adopted cost in the agency-to-agents trade), and **Section 3's B.6** (the GitHub board as the place a relocation's adopted costs become *trailing signals* you can actually read — the origination ratio, the reframe-vs-accept rate — rather than surprises discovered late).
 
## Clay-experience anchors to capture
 
Prompts for Clay to mine his 30-year engineering/founder history — the parts research can't supply:
 
- **The plan's named anchor:** a time Clay traded one dependency for another and **only saw the new one later** — what was shed, what arrived late, how long the lag was, and what trailing signal (or its absence) finally revealed the adopted cost. This is the spine of the whole module.
- **A build-vs-buy call and what it taught** (the SVD anchor): a commodity Clay correctly rented vs. a differentiator he was right to own — or a time he got the classification backwards (custom-built a commodity, or rented something that should have been his).
- **A hold-up he lived through:** a vendor or contractor relationship where sunk, relationship-specific investment let the counterparty extract rent — and what exit terms he wishes he'd bargained for *at the start*.
- **An insourcing/repatriation he ran** (the 37signals analog at his scale): what the headline savings hid, the new faculty the team had to develop to hold the adopted dependency, and whether it accrued or got eaten.
- **A naive-intervention near-miss:** a "something must be done" fix he made (or watched a team make) whose second-order cost outweighed the first-order benefit — and how skin-in-the-game (his vs. an advisor's) shaped who saw it coming.
- **The agent-era version, current:** where Clay has already felt the deprecation treadmill or capability drift bite — a model change that broke a prompt/eval, and what the migration actually cost in scaffolding rework.
## Caveats and reliability gradient
 
- **Build doctrine on (robust / definitional):** the four-response risk taxonomy and the transfer≠elimination / residual-risk principle (ISO 31000, COSO ERM, NIST, PMI — multiple independent corroborating sources); transaction-cost economics and the make-or-buy / asset-specificity / hold-up apparatus (Coase 1937, 1960; Williamson 1981, 1983; Tadelis & Williamson — empirically well-developed); switching-cost economics and bilateral lock-in (Shapiro & Varian 1999; Farrell & Klemperer; Joskow, Klein).
- **Cite with an explicit caveat (strong intuition, contested in strong form):** risk homeostasis (Wilde 1982, 1998) and the Peltzman effect (1975). Use the *weak* claim (compensation / relocation), never the *strong* claim (risk conserved at a fixed setpoint), which is disputed and was never cleanly verified experimentally. If the playbook leans on the thermostat metaphor, it must footnote the dissent or *Remaining Viable* will have to walk it back.
- **Use as vocabulary / framing, not evidence:** iatrogenics and naive interventionism (Taleb, *Antifragile*, 2012) — a memorable, persuasive synthesis, not peer-reviewed mechanism. Its "skin in the game" point is genuinely load-bearing precisely because it *coincides with the playbook's own spine* arriving from another direction; that convergence is worth naming, but the spine, not Taleb, is the authority.
- **Point-in-time (will go stale; cite with the mid-2026 date):** the model-deprecation calendar and capability-drift specifics (Presenc AI tracker, May 2026; TianPan.co, April 2026; Syncfusion, April 2026; vendor deprecation pages). The *pattern* — compressing lifecycles, drift across versions, prompt/eval scaffolding as a stranded specific asset, abstraction layers as the dual-sourcing defense — is durable; the dates and model names are not.
- **Case study, single instance (illustrative, not representative):** 37signals' cloud repatriation (DHH; The Register, DCD, Inspectural, 2022–2026). A vivid, well-documented worked example of booking both ledger columns — *not* evidence that repatriation generally pays. Self-reported savings come from the company; the adopted-cost critique (ops roles, refreshes, power/cooling) is the third-party (DCD) corroboration that makes it useful here. Their result held *because* they had the ops talent and predictable workloads to hold the adopted dependency — a boundary condition, not a universal.
- **Tension to keep visible, not paper over:** the lens must avoid collapsing into fatalism. "Every escape is a trade" is true; "so don't bother escaping" does not follow. The disciplined conclusion is *non-naive* — trade when the new dependency is clearly worth holding, priced with both columns on the page, with exit terms bargained at the start. The relocation lens is a tool for *choosing well*, not an argument for standing still.
- **Normative-lens note (carried from Streams A & B):** "the two ends," "answerability," and the relocation framing are the author's constructs. The economics and risk-management literature support them strongly but do not "prove" them; present the lens as a rigorously-grounded way of seeing, not as an established academic model.
