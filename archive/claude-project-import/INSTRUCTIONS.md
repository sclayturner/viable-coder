The playbook is divided into 4 sections to be later named:

1. Becoming Viable versus Remaining Viable: This section deals with "risk". Clay believes that to become viable is to make the most of the opportunity that is affording itself to humans in the Age of AI without missteps. Missteps are even more costly now that AI has accelerated R&D and commoditize coding. To remain viable is to constantly ask "what is under threat" longer-term. This necessarily demands that the mentee first get his/her shit together and see things correctly. A starting point is understanding the role and limits of his/her own "organic intelligence" relative to the machine's "artificial intelligence". The human assumes all risk. The AI does not. The human has everything to gain or lose by not managing outcomes - owning his/her role. The AI does not.
2. Mapping Audiences to Valuable Insights: Not every mentee is the same. Clay wants to define the six audience types and map his experiences as a technical founder to each audience. This means modularizing guidance and exercises. Like building blocks, these modules can be plugged in to each audience type. The repeat non-technical founder who is building a product needs to rely on Clay's engineering guidance. The first-time founder, who may be technical needs to rely on Clay's experience as a founder who understands Product Market Fit, Founder Market Fit, timing, etc. In other words, a smart set of modules needs to be developed that are interchangeable. A member of any audience type will be presented with the default collection assigned to that audience type, but can borrow from others where he/she sees fit.
3. Defining the Agent Development Life Cycle (ADLC): This is a huge part of the playbook. It recognizes that the traditional Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC) is forever changed by AI coding agents. It recognizes that Agile development has compressed activities into continual development rather that traditional sprints. It further recognizes that the traditional "Iron Triangle" is less relevant. For example, for startups, the trade-off has always been "time". Startup founders rarely have the time to follow rigid processes. Those rigid processes do have value in later-stage companies. Now, with AI injected into every single phase of the process "time" is no longer the same constraint. Startups can produce well-defined PRDs, informed by the entire repository of code, customer feedback channels, stakeholder meeting transcripts, etc. Unit testing becomes more practical. Building proper cloud infrastructure, considering security at a more granular level, adhering to a consistent CI/CD pipeline, and so on become standard practice - facilitated by AI agents. Documentation can be produced and maintained along the way by agents rather than falling to the wayside. The human focuses on what "should" be built and deployed.
4. Considering the 9-Dimensions of Long-term Viability: With each dimension defined, we answer the question of "what is under threat". We produce objective data for insights into a longer term career "thinking, deciding, and creating" as human beings along side our machine counterparts.
