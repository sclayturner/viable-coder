Internal Talent Worth Developing ITW A specific, promising employee you'd rather develop into a real product owner than replace by hiring outside.
The Situation

You have seen something in this person. Maybe this person is in customer support and keeps proposing better workflows. Maybe this person is in operations and built an internal tool with AI tools that actually works. Maybe this person is a self-taught generalist who keeps finding ways to be useful in roles that didn't exist before. You don't want to lose this person to a bigger company that develops talent faster, and you don't want to keep paying outside vendors when this person could plausibly own the work.

The Fear Underneath

"I'll spend money on outside help again and still not have anyone internal who can own this. And eventually this person will leave because we didn't invest."

What You're After

A multiplier on someone you already trust. Capability that stays inside the company. A clear, structured path that doesn't require your employee to disappear into a bootcamp or quit to attend a program. You want to be the leader who developed someone — that's a meaningful identity.

SaaS Vendor Dependency SVD The operational stack your business runs on is rented from SaaS vendors — at rising prices, with a fit that never quite matches your workflow.
The Situation

Your operational stack is a portfolio of monthly subscriptions — CRM, billing, support, analytics, workflow. Each solves a real problem. Each charges more this year than last. Each shapes your workflow rather than fitting it. You watch the line items grow and wonder when the math turns — when the rent on someone else's general-purpose product exceeds the cost of building exactly the tool your team needs. The 2026 "SaaSpocalypse" framing in the press makes it sound binary. In your business it's a question of which subscription, which year, and what would replace it.

The Fear Underneath

"I'm paying premium prices for software that doesn't fit, locked in by switching costs that compound every quarter. The longer I wait, the more I owe to the lock-in."

What You're After

A clear-eyed audit of which SaaS line items are insourceable now, which aren't, and what internal capability would have to exist to replace them. A staged path that doesn't require a "rip and replace" moment. The tailored fit and pricing leverage that comes from owning the tools you depend on.

External Dev Dependency EDD Your product is built by an outside agency. You pay for development without owning the capability.
The Situation

Your product lives at an outside shop. Every change is a negotiation. Every timeline is a hope. Every invoice is a small grief. The agency holds the keys, the context, and increasingly, the power. You built the company; someone else owns the engine.

The Fear Underneath

"I don't actually own my product. If this vendor relationship ends, I have nothing."

What You're After

Control. Leverage. Sovereignty. The ability to say "we'll handle that internally" and mean it. To stop discovering pricing changes in invoices. To know — for the first time — what's possible without asking permission.

Legacy Software Debt LSD Mission-critical software the original developers built years ago, and nobody currently on the team fully understands.
The Situation

The software runs your business. It works, mostly. But every change request feels riskier than it should. The team maintaining it inherited it from people who left. The documentation is partial. The knowledge is lossy. Every quarter brings a new "we should probably modernize this" conversation that gets tabled because the path forward is unclear and the risk of getting it wrong is enormous.

The Fear Underneath

"This codebase is a liability and nobody can touch it without breaking something. Eventually it will fail in a way we can't fix."

What You're After

First: stability. Second: an honest assessment of what you actually have, what's realistic to keep, and what isn't. Third: a path forward that doesn't require ripping everything out and starting over. You want clarity before action.

AI Development Gap AID A developer team that hasn't yet moved AI-native — whether through stalled adoption, evaluation paralysis, or simply not having started.
The Situation

You have developers. Some of you bought Copilot seats. Some of you tried Cursor. Some of you have not seriously evaluated any of it yet. None of you are operating AI-native — which is to say, none of you are working meaningfully differently than you did three years ago. Meanwhile, every week brings another LinkedIn post from a competitor or peer claiming 10x productivity gains. You can't tell if those posts are real, exaggerated, or true and yours is the problem. You feel behind and don't know how far.

The Fear Underneath

"We're falling behind in a way we can't easily measure. By the time it shows up in our financials, it'll be too late."

What You're After

Competitive parity first. Genuine advantage second. You want someone who can look at your team and say, with credibility, "here's what's actually possible, here's what isn't, and here's what we'd need to change." You are exhausted by hype and starved for honest specifics.

Prototype-to-Production Wall PPW Built something real on no-code, low-code, or AI-assisted tools — got traction — and now facing the gap between "this works" and "this holds up."
The Situation

You built it. Often in days or weeks, not months. You got users. Maybe revenue. Maybe enough revenue to quit your job. And now you're staring at the gap between your Bubble app or your Lovable build or your Cursor-vibe-coded thing and what it would actually take to serve 10x the users, integrate with real systems, pass a security review, or be acquired. The prototype was the easy part. The thing underneath wasn't built to last. You feel both proud and exposed.

The Fear Underneath

"What I built isn't real. I'm one outage, one security question, one big customer away from being exposed."

What You're After

Legitimacy. The transformation from "I made a thing" to "I run a software business." You want to keep the speed you discovered but stop feeling like an imposter. A path that doesn't require throwing it all away and starting over with a traditional team.

02 The Engagement

A focused engagement designed to leave capability inside your company — not a retainer behind it.

01 · Start

Honest capability assessment

Where you are. What's realistic. The technical debt and constraints that shape what comes next.

02 · Setup

AI-native product roadmap

A clear plan for what you'll build, what tools you'll use, what your customers will feel.

03 · Every week

Weekly working sessions

Structured, purposeful, tied directly to your product reality and the person doing the work.

04 · As needed

Direct access between sessions

Questions don't wait for the calendar. Real decisions get real input when they matter.

05 · Network

Community of builders

Introductions, meetups, and workshops with current and past mentees doing the same hard thing.

06 · Handoff

Built to end well

The goal is internal capability, not retainer. Engagements conclude when ownership has genuinely transferred.

The engagement is one thing. The inquiry is another.

I conduct an ongoing public inquiry — measuring the long-term viability of practice across nine dimensions, on Remaining Viable and Data. The subject of that inquiry is me, by default. Mentees who want their own work studied the same way can opt in to the cohort, with granular consent. It is never required. The mentorship engagement does not ask you to share data, change how you work, or participate in any measurement. If you want only the mentorship, you get only the mentorship.
