The AI-native Enterprise: A Playbook for Long-term Viability.

The North Star I bring to every mentee on day one. Doctrine, video, working agents, exercises — kept current, sold openly. Edition 1 ships Q4 2026.

01 What This Is

My own guide — before it was anyone else's.

Every mentorship engagement starts the same way: with a single claim, and a set of working principles built on it. The claim is this — the AI does the work; you carry the risk. Everything else is that asymmetry worked out in practice: which questions get asked first, which trade-offs get named out loud, what "viable" actually means when conditions shift. Until now that doctrine has lived in my own notes, in conversation, and in the work itself.

The Playbook codifies it — and ships with what you need to put it into practice. The written doctrine carries the position. Recorded workshops walk through how the position becomes practice. The included agents — the same kind I run in my own day-to-day — let you execute the doctrine on your own product, immediately. The exercises ask you to produce real artifacts: a PRD, a pull request, a roadmap. Comprehensive enough to be used. Specific enough to be argued with.

It moves in four parts: knowing who does what, finding your situation, running the day-to-day, and playing the long game. The center of gravity is the third — running the day-to-day is the Agent Development Life Cycle, the ADLC: the old software lifecycle, re-staffed by a crew of agents, with you holding the two jobs that never transfer. You start the work, and you answer for it. The agents take everything in between.

It is grounded in industry best practices as they evolve, in my own day-to-day practice as technical co-founder of Soaring Titan and Maximum Automotive Intelligence, and in the research stream you'll find on Remaining Viable. It will be updated as the field moves — because the field is not done moving.

02 Working Table of Contents

Four parts, one stance.

A working outline — subject to revision as drafting continues. The four sections build on each other: who carries the risk, where you specifically stand, how the day-to-day actually runs, and what to watch over a career. Each chapter pairs a position with a practice — a written argument, a recorded workshop, a working agent or two, and an exercise that asks you to produce a real artifact. Not just what to believe, but what to do on Monday morning.

Section 1

Knowing Who Does What

The opportunity is real, and it's on you. Two kinds of intelligence at the table, and the two jobs that never transfer to the machine.

The Wager

The AI does the work; you carry the risk. Why this is the best time there's ever been to build — and why missteps cost more now that building got fast and cheap.

Two Kinds of Intelligence

What the machine is for, and what you're for. It decides how; you decide why — and only you can care whether the thing is actually right, not just whether it works.

The Two Jobs That Stay Yours

Origination at the front, answerability at the back. You start the work and you answer for it; the agents take everything in between. The first question of the book: did I choose this, or did it choose me?

You Are the Infrastructure

Your condition is the binding constraint now. How to check yourself when you can't trust the mirror, and how to wire an alarm that fires on the days you'd rather ignore it.

Section 2

Finding Your Situation

This book doesn't have one reader. Find the spot you're actually standing in, get the kit built for it, and learn when to borrow from the others.

The Intake

A short, honest read that points you to your situation — based on what you've actually built, shipped, and inherited, not on how you'd grade yourself. A route, not a label.

Build It, or Borrow It

For every skill in front of you, one call: own it for real because you'll live with it, or borrow just enough to direct it and stay in charge. The trap on each side, and the good news.

Your Six Situations

Growing a team, renting what you could own, depending on an outside shop, inheriting a mess you're scared to touch, shipping what you can't explain, or pushing a demo to a real product. Each drops a different job and makes a different trade.

Your Kit Isn't a Cage

Every escape is a trade — no move sheds risk, it relocates it. Borrow across situations on purpose, and choose the dependency worth holding instead of waking up inside one.

Section 3 · The Centerpiece

Running the Day-to-Day: The ADLC

The Agent Development Life Cycle — the software lifecycle you may never have been taught the name of, re-staffed by a crew of agents, with you on the two gates that never transfer.

From SDLC to ADLC

The path every app on your phone was built on, and why each step existed. The path stays — the team in the middle becomes a crew of agents, producing everything a full human team once did.

Your Shop

One place the work lives. A set of specialized agents, each with one narrow job. The house rules that make them build it your way. And the adapter that lets them see everything the company already knows.

One Job, Start to Finish

Follow a single piece of work across the board, left to right. The agent fills the column; you own the gate between the columns. That rhythm is the whole job.

What You Can't Hand Off

Where you start, and where you answer — and the in-flight move (steering) that keeps both gates real when the middle runs at machine pace. Because agents absorb the time the middle used to cost, real tests, granular security, and solid infrastructure become the floor — not a late-stage luxury. And the one place that claim breaks.

What You Can't Leave to Default Settings

The agent will reach for the frontier model on every task unless you configured otherwise — and your token bill, your rate-limit exposure, and your provider dependency will climb without you ever signing for them. The classify-and-route discipline that meets that default, with open-source and self-hosted models as a first-class option.

Teaching the AI Your Way

The same rulebook can make you sharper or hollow you out. Set your agents to explain where the skill is yours to own; set them to just produce where you'll only ever direct. The knife-edge under every teaching agent — and the same setting that decides whether you can see into the middle while the work is running.

The Board Is Watching You, Too

Because the whole build runs through one place, it quietly records how you worked — what you reframed, what you actually read before approving, what you chose versus rubber-stamped. The honest instrument you could never keep on yourself.

Section 4

Playing the Long Game

Becoming viable is this build. Remaining viable is every build after. A handful of honest questions you can carry for a career — readings your work takes for you, not a quiz you grade yourself on.

A Different Clock

Every reading on the short clock flatters you — a shipped feature, a happy client. The second clock ticks in years, and it's the one almost nobody is watching.

The Nine Dimensions

Three families — the person, the practice, the ground. Nine things actually under threat over a career, each one planted earlier in the book and coming due here. A working translation of the framework from Remaining Viable.

Questions You Can't Lie To

Trends, not snapshots — read against your own baseline. The most honest readings come straight from your work — your transcripts, your invoices, your last ten bios — not from your gut on a given afternoon.

The Loop Closes

A bad reading isn't a verdict; it's the next thing worth building. Remaining viable feeds straight back into becoming viable — and the whole loop starts over, one build smarter than last time.

Chapters and ordering will shift as drafting continues. The intent — comprehensive, prescriptive, honest — will not.
