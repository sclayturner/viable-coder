## Lesson 1 — What the Day-to-Day Becomes (When a Crew of Agents Does the Middle)

Two units back, we agreed on the floor. You bear the risk; the machine bears none. Two jobs at the ends — one to start the work and one to answer for it — and a long middle in between. One unit back, we found the kit built for where you actually are, named the trade you were going to make to get there, and signed the residual risk. None of that runs. It is stance and configuration. This unit is where it becomes a day of work.

Before we walk that day, I have to name the thing we're transforming, because most builders — even technical ones — were never taught its name. For decades, building software has followed a set path. Figure out what's needed. Design it. Build it. Test it. Ship it. Keep it alive. That path has a name — the **Software Development Life Cycle**, the **SDLC** — and whether or not you've ever heard the term before, every app on your phone was built on some version of it. Every back-end system you depend on. Every release of the operating system you're reading this on. The path is not arbitrary. Each step was there to kill a specific risk at the right moment, *so you didn't build the wrong thing, design yourself into a corner, or ship something broken to real people.*

There was a catch that shaped the last forty years of how software actually got made. Doing those steps *well* took a whole team and took real time. It took a product manager who could write a spec the engineers wouldn't curse at, an architect who could see two years ahead, a tester who actually broke things, a security reviewer who didn't just nod, an infrastructure engineer, a documentation owner, a release manager. That's why scrappy startups skipped most of it. Not because they didn't know better. Because they couldn't afford it. The compromise was: cut the middle, ship fast, pay the interest later. We all paid the interest later. The pipeline was a luxury good, and most of you have been working without it for as long as you've been building anything.

What just changed — what is changing as you read this — is that AI agents can now do the work *inside* every one of those steps. The product manager who used to take a week to write a spec is now an agent that drafts it in twelve minutes and asks you the three questions a senior PM would have asked. The architect, the tester, the security reviewer, the documentation owner — each becomes a specialized agent in a crew. The path stays. The team in the middle becomes a crew of agents. We claim a name for this, because the industry hasn't settled on one and the ones it's reached for so far point at something else: the **Agent Development Life Cycle — the ADLC.** *The same old SDLC, re-staffed by agents, producing everything a full human team once produced.*

The headline is the one to hold across this whole unit, because every lesson walks back to it. **The agents do the middle; you keep the two jobs from Unit 1 — you start the work, and you answer for it.** Origination at the front. Answerability at the back. The two jobs you were already going to hold, now living at specific spots on a board you can see.

There is a third move that lives between those two, and the lesson that names it as doctrine is Lesson 4, so I will only name it here. What you do *while* the agent's column runs — read what it's actually doing, redirect when its trajectory drifts off the *why* you set at the front, interrupt when the path it's taking is one you cannot stand behind at the back — is what holding the two jobs looks like in flight. We call it **steering**, and it is not a third job. The two jobs do not multiply. Steering is the active form of holding the two jobs *while the work is running*, and it is what makes the gates at the ends real instead of ceremonial. We will spend Lesson 4 on it, because it is the lesson where it earns its place. That's the unit.

## Lesson 2 — Your Shop: Where the Work Lives, the Team, and the House Rules

The kit from Unit 2 has to land somewhere. *Shop* is the right word for what we're building, because what you're going to run is not a feature, and not a project, and not even a codebase. It is a small operation with four pieces. None of them are exotic; all four of them you can see and audit. I'm going to name each in plain terms first and put the technical name in parentheses once, the way Unit 1 did when it introduced *origination.* You'll see these terms again everywhere — in vendor docs, in your harness map, in the crosswalk. Once you've met them, they read clean.

The first piece is one place the work lives. The code, its full history, the to-do list, and the plan all sit in one place you can audit. *The to-do list is the plan.* Nothing important lives only in someone's head, on a sticky note, in a chat thread that scrolls off, or in a tool that drifts out of sync with the code. One audit surface. This is the piece that makes everything else honest, because the rest of the shop cannot run if it cannot see itself. It is also the piece that, two lessons from now, will make the agents *readable as they work* — every reasoning step, every choice, every edit recorded in flight, not summarized after. The same substrate that keeps the shop honest is what lets you see into the middle of any column while it's running. Hold that aside for Lesson 4; here in Lesson 2 the substrate is what we're naming.

The second piece is the team — the *subagents.* Instead of hiring a product manager, an architect, a tester, a security reviewer, you direct a set of specialized agents. Each has one narrow job. Each hands you back clean work to check. You can keep adding them; you do not run out of headcount. The math the last forty years of software hiring was built on — *another person costs $200K loaded, takes three months to ramp, leaves in eighteen* — that math is dissolving inside this piece. You direct one of these the way you'd give a brief to a contractor: tell it the goal, give it the constraints, hand it the materials, take what it produces and decide whether you're going to stand behind it.

The third piece is the house rules — the *Skills.* A Skill is a short, reusable file that teaches an agent *how your shop does a thing.* How you write a spec. What "done" means to you. Your security checklist. Your code style. The way you want a PR description written. Every agent in your shop reads the relevant Skills before it produces anything, so every artifact comes out *your* way, every time, instead of some generic way. The Skills are the place the agent learns your standards. We will spend the whole of Lesson 6 on the most consequential decision you'll make about how to write one, because how you write them decides what they do to *you* — not just what they do to the work.

The fourth piece is the thing that lets your agents see everything you know — the *MCP.* You have truth scattered across a dozen places. Support tickets in one system. Sales calls in another. Design files somewhere else. Customer-success notes, threat models, meeting transcripts, the actual chat threads where the real decisions got made. The MCP is a universal adapter that lets your agents read across all of that. The work the shop produces is shaped by everything the company knows, not just what you happen to remember on a given afternoon. This piece quietly does more than people expect. Most of what you'd call *intuition* about your product is information you'd remember if it were in front of you. The MCP puts it in front of the agent every time.

Four pieces. One place it lives, a team of subagents, a rulebook of Skills, an adapter that pulls in everything the company knows. That's the shop. And here is where Unit 2 plugs in, because this is the lesson where you find out the last unit was not just orientation. Your situation already decided which of these agents step forward and which house rules they run. The kit you got wasn't advice to read and forget. It was the setup sheet for this shop. The founder who came in through SaaS Vendor Dependency walks into a shop with a Build-vs-Buy subagent foregrounded at the start of every ticket. The lead who came in through Internal Talent Worth Developing walks into a shop where every agent is set to teach. The team that came in through the Prototype-to-Production Wall walks into a shop where the production-hardening Skill runs at every gate that touches deploy. Same four pieces. Different foreground. The phrase that runs the whole thing underneath — and that you will never need to remember — is this: **the ADLC pipeline is invariant; Unit 2 configures only the interaction layer.** You get the same board everyone else gets. Your kit decides which corners of it are lit hardest for you.

You're not really coding anymore. You're running a shop. A team, a rulebook, and one place it all lives.

## Lesson 3 — One Job, Start to Finish

Now the day. We are going to follow one piece of work — one ticket — left to right across the shop, from the first time it has a name to the moment a real user is on the other side of it. There are nine gates on the board. Every gate has an agent in it. You are standing between the gates, and the lesson's question is the one the builder is already asking: if every gate is staffed, what am I still doing here?

The answer takes nine beats to land, but it's one sentence, and I will give it to you now so you hear it under each beat. **The agent fills the column; you own the gate.** Nine times. The columns are the agents' work. The gates between them are yours. Two of those gates — the first and the seventh — are the two jobs from Unit 1, now living at specific spots on the board. The other gates are real, and we'll walk them, but those two are the ones the next lesson is going to call out by name as gates that never transfer.

You hold the gates by reading the columns. Owning a gate is not standing at its boundary with a stamp — it is having read enough of what happened *inside* the column to be able to stand behind what comes out of it. The next lesson names that motion; here in Lesson 3 you should feel it under the rhythm, because every beat below has a *gate* I will narrate and a *column* I will narrate next to it, and you will be doing both.

You decide it's worth doing. You start it. The agents pull in what the company already knows about it — every support ticket that mentions the area, every sales call where it came up, every chat thread where the founders argued about it — and surface what you'd otherwise miss. But the *why-now* is yours. The ticket carries your name on top, not the agent's, even if the agent surfaced the idea first.

You spell it out — or rather, the agent does, and you decide whether you read it or wave it. A PRD lands in your inbox at 9:04 a.m. for a ticket you originated at 9:02. It is three pages long. It is well-written. It is the spec a startup never had time to write. Your job is the problem and the trade-offs encoded in it, not the typing. If the agent writes the spec it then implements — the *self-spec* — it has crossed onto your end of the line, and the rest of the build is downstream of that crossing. We instrument for that here.

You break it down. The spec becomes a list of work items. Another agent sorts them, deduplicates, estimates. *You* say which ones are truly ready, on purpose, out loud. There is a column called Backlog with forty-seven items and a column called Ready with six, and you are the only thing that moves an item from one to the other. Your hand wants to skip it. We are not skipping it. Every item in Backlog *can* be built. The Ready gate asks which one *should.*

You design it — or rather, an agent proposes the shape and you judge whether it's *right,* not just whether it will work. Three options, scored. The agent has a recommendation. You have a feeling. Sometimes the feeling is the thing the agent's scoring couldn't see. Sometimes it's a reflex you should set aside. The gate is not picking option A or option B; the gate is being the one whose name goes on the choice.

You build it. The agent writes the code, opens a PR. This part is genuinely the agent's. The diff is two hundred lines in fifteen minutes. The commit message is fluent. *This column is the one the lesson does not pull back from, and it should not.* The risk is not here. The risk is two columns over, when the PR is green and you have to read it.

You test it, secure it, wire it up. The work that used to get skipped — real tests, a security pass, the plumbing, the infrastructure-as-code — agents now produce as a matter of course. A test report. A security findings list. An infrastructure change-set. All generated. All reading clean. Maybe too clean. The next lesson will tell you why the *too clean* is the part to watch, and where to read against it.

You review it. The PR is green. The CI is green. The agent's summary is fluent. Your finger is over Approve. *Did you read the diff?* This is the gate the next lesson is going to call the most dangerous gate on the board for everyone. It is the answerable end of the two jobs, and it is the place automation complacency lives. It is also the place that, when it goes well, is where you actually held the work — where you can stand behind what shipped because you actually read it.

You write it down — or rather, an agent keeps the documentation true *as the code changes,* instead of letting it rot the way it always did. The docs page updates itself when the PR merges. You haven't opened it in three weeks. Documentation, the formerly-skipped work, is now nearly free. *Free is not the same as honest.* Sample one doc per release against the running system. The agent writes the doc the way it thinks the system works, which is not always how it does.

You ship it, and you watch it. It goes live behind your say-so, and you own what happens to real people on the other side. The build now talks back — dashboards, traces, the first user's first session. And the build is silently observing *you* too. We hold that for Lesson 7.

That's the loop. The ticket is closed. The next ticket is named — by you, again, with a why-now you can defend. What you learn out in the world feeds the next start it. The board doesn't end; it cycles. And the same board has been quietly emitting the signals Unit 4 will read on the long clock. We come back to that twice — in Lesson 7, where the board reads *you,* and again at the end, where it reads you for years.

**The agent fills the column; you own the gate.** That rhythm is the whole job.

## Lesson 4 — What You Can't Hand Off

Walk the board twice and you start to see something. Seven of the nine gates have an agent doing the producing inside the column. Two of them don't, not really — and these two are the ones we owe the lesson to.

The first is the gate where you started it. The second is the gate where you reviewed it. The two ends. Origination at the front and answerability at the back. *These are the two jobs from Unit 1, now living at specific spots on the board.* Every lesson that follows reads back to this sentence, because the spine of this unit is the same spine as Unit 1, now relocated to the loop.

The reason these two can't transfer is the one I'm going to say plainly, because the procedural answer flatters everyone and explains nothing. They can't transfer *because they're about wanting the thing to be right and being the one who gets the call at 2 a.m., and the machine does neither.* The originate gate requires *caring* whether this is worth building — not whether it's interesting, not whether it's tractable, whether it's *worth.* The review gate requires being willing to put your name on what shipped to actual users, knowing that if it goes badly the call comes to you and not to the model. There is no setting on an agent that gives it stake in the outcome. We do not pretend there is.

The good news first, and we should not be shy about it, because it's the news that makes the rest of this kit different from anything you could have built five years ago. Because the agents absorb the *time* the middle used to cost, the things only big, late-stage companies could once afford — real tests, proper security, solid infrastructure, docs that stay true — are now standard from day one. The Iron Triangle dissolved. The luxuries became the floor. The pre-2020 startup compromise of *skip the pipeline, pay the interest later* is structurally over for new work. You do not get to choose anymore between speed and the pipeline; the pipeline is what produces the speed.

The honest catch — and we put it in this lesson, in the unit that most celebrates the headline, because the alternative is to bury it in back-matter and let the headline run unqualified into rooms it shouldn't. *Time is no longer the constraint* holds best for *new* work built from scratch. For the tangled system you inherited and can't fully see — the Legacy Software Debt situation, if that's you — the agent can speed the typing but not the *understanding,* and the understanding was always the slow part. The lesson that most loudly celebrates the new pace is also the lesson that says: there is a system in your shop the new pace doesn't quite reach. Your kit knows this; your characterization tests run before your implementer touches anything; your archaeology runs before your design. The unit is honest about this in the lesson that most exposes it.

Now the part of the lesson that earns the rest of it. Two gates that never transfer is doctrine; that is what we've said and what we mean. The question we have not yet answered — and the unit cannot be honest without it — is *what makes those two gates holdable at all,* because no human can stand behind a fluent 200-line PR they didn't watch get written at the pace an agent writes it. The fix isn't *be more careful at G7.* The fix is structural, and it is the same instrument-don't-introspect move Unit 1 made for the self, ported to a second scale. The agent's reasoning has to be **readable while the work is running** — not summarized after, not handed over as a polished explanation attached to a finished artifact, but produced *alongside* the producing so the human can see what the agent is doing as it does it. That readability is what doctrine calls **observability of the middle**. It is the same principle as the self-observability that closed Unit 1: instrument the thing you can't trust to report on itself, and make the reading something that lives outside the impaired part. The self piece was about a human reading themselves. This piece is about the human reading the *agent* in flight. Two scales, one doctrine.

What observability of the middle enables, in motion, is **steering**. Steering is what the human is doing *while the agent's column runs*: reading the agent's reasoning as it produces, redirecting when the trajectory drifts off the *why-now* you set at the front, pressing the interrupt key when the path it's taking is one you cannot stand behind at the back. The interrupt key on every major coding agent today — Claude Code, Cursor, Codex, Cline — is the most primitive form of this. It is a steering primitive: a button that turns *wait for the artifact* into *stay in the work.* The more elaborate version is configuring your agents to produce *and* explain, so the reasoning runs alongside the producing and your in-flight reading has something real to read. Done right, steering doesn't slow the work; it spreads the human's attention across the middle in small, in-flight increments so the gate at the back isn't asked to do all the holding by itself. Done wrong — or skipped entirely — it leaves the human standing at the gate with a fluent diff in hand and a finger over Approve, and from that position there is exactly one outcome the lesson is honest about, and that is the rubber-stamp.

**Steering is not a third job.** Origination and answerability remain the two jobs. The geometry doesn't multiply. Steering is what holding them looks like *while the work is running* — the in-flight form of the same two jobs you already had — and observability of the middle is its structural precondition. Without it, both gates collapse, the same way and for the same reason: at the front, you accept the agent's next suggestion because nothing forced you to read its reasoning for *why this one*; at the back, you approve the agent's diff because nothing forced you to read what it actually did. Both failures look like motion; both are the in-flight motion gone missing. The lesson that named the two gates as the ones that can never transfer owes the rest of the unit the answer to *how* the human holds them when the middle runs at machine pace, and steering is that answer. The rest of the unit configures it. Lesson 5 names the *sibling* discipline — what you can't leave to default settings, on the economic axis. Lesson 6 names the register that makes the middle observable. Lesson 7 reads, after the fact, whether the human actually steered or only said they did.

One thing to be careful about, because the move slides easily: opacity in the middle now is not a property of the work. It is a configuration choice. The same agent that can produce a fluent 200-line diff in silence can, with one register flip, produce the same diff with the eight decisions it made along the way exposed in order, in flight, with a deliberate pause at the third trade-off for the human to weigh in. The first configuration produces a rubber-stamp at the back; the second configuration produces a gate you can actually hold. The playbook's position is that opacity in the middle is the wrong default for any work whose answerable half is yours, and that choice is the practitioner's, not the tool vendor's. We will choose it explicitly in Lesson 6.

Now the failure mode at each gate, because both gates fail the same way and the way is what steering's absence looks like — and the way is the one that feels like progress.

The originate gate fails when you wave a piece of work to *ready* because the agent suggested it. You didn't choose it; it chose you. The agent surfaced the next ticket, and you accepted it, and you accepted the one after that, and three weeks later your roadmap is the agent's roadmap with your name on top. *Did I choose this, or did it choose me?* The first question of the kit, failed at a gate. *Now read it through the lens we just built:* the failure isn't a moment of weakness at the gate. It is the absence of a steering motion that should have happened *before* the gate — the human reading the agent's reasoning for *why this one* and either confirming it or steering toward a different ticket. The fix is structural and it has two halves: every ticket carries a human-authored *why-now* line, or it doesn't enter the board (the gate); and the agent's surfacing of the candidate is produced *with its reasoning exposed in flight*, so the human's confirmation is an act of steering, not an act of accepting (the steering precondition). The fix isn't exhortation. The fix is that the steering is real, which makes the gate real.

The review gate fails when you approve a PR without really reading it. The CI is green. The agent's summary is fluent. The diff is two hundred lines and you have eleven of them in your queue. Your finger has hovered over Approve thirty times this week and the muscle memory says click. This is the single most dangerous gate on the board, for every builder, in every situation. The literature on automation complacency is blunt on this: it hits experts too, and you cannot train it away by deciding to be careful. *And what we now know structurally:* rubber-stamp is not a character failure. It is the inevitable outcome of an opaque middle running faster than human review capacity. No human reads a 200-line fluent diff with full understanding at the pace an agent writes it. The only honest review at G7 is one informed by readings taken *across the middle* — small, in-flight, while the artifact is still being written — and the human who hasn't been steering across the column is the human who is asked to do all the holding in one impossible inspection at the end. The fix is structural, and it lives in two lessons: in Lesson 6, the produce-and-explain register that makes the middle observable in flight so the human can steer across the column; and in Lesson 7, the instrument that records whether the human actually steered or only said they did. The number is yours to face, and the reason it can be honest is that the substrate that runs the work is the same substrate that recorded what you did across the middle.

Both failures feel like progress. The ticket moved. The PR merged. The board cleared. Both are you quietly handing off a job that was yours to keep. We are not going to pretend otherwise, because the entire point of naming these two gates is that the rest of the kit holds together only if you actually held them.

The agent has a second default that hollows the practice the same way opacity in the middle does, and the lesson that names what you can't hand off owes the lesson that names what you can't leave to default settings. The second default is on the *economics* of the work, not the work itself. It is what we owe the unit now.

## Lesson 5 — What You Can't Leave to Default Settings

Two of the agent's defaults will quietly hollow your practice if left running. Lesson 4 named the first one — opacity in the middle — and the discipline that meets it. This lesson names the second one. Same shape, different axis: a silent default that looks like progress while it costs you something you didn't sign for; an explicit structural discipline that meets it; a configuration choice that is yours to make, not the harness's to make on your behalf.

The default this lesson refuses is the one most coding harnesses run by default today, including the ones the most disciplined practitioners are using. They let one model — usually the frontier model the harness ships with — run every subagent, in every column, on every task, regardless of how complex the work actually is. A boilerplate refactor that needed a 70B open-source model gets handed to a frontier reasoning model. A documentation pass that needed a small fast model gets the same. A test scaffold, a commit message, a config sweep — all handed to the most expensive model in the rack, because no one configured otherwise. The harness produces fluent results, the practitioner gets used to the fluency, and the economics quietly become a dependency on the frontier provider continuing to subsidize the way the work runs. The cost of that absent discipline is mostly invisible until a bill arrives that doesn't match what the practitioner believed they were running, or a rate-limit notice surfaces a dependency they had never explicitly accepted. *You didn't choose the policy; the policy chose you.* The same shape as the originate-by-drift trap in Lesson 4 — same feels-like-progress signature, same kind of silent handoff, the agent's hand quietly on a piece of the practice that should have been the practitioner's.

The discipline that meets the default is two primitives plus a posture. I will name them in plain terms first and put the technical words in parentheses once, the way Unit 1 handled *origination* and Lesson 2 handled *subagents, Skills, MCP.* The first piece is a small, fast model whose only job is to read the complexity of the incoming task and emit a routing decision — *the thing that decides which model gets this one.* The technical name is **classifier**. It runs cheap because reading complexity is not what frontier capability is for; it can do its work on a small open-source model in milliseconds, hand off a complexity score and a routing recommendation, and step out of the way. The second piece is the **router** — the thing that takes the classifier's recommendation and assigns the work to a model in the fleet *you* configured. *And* the router is the seam where your explicit policy lives: the rules that say *for this kind of work, escalate to frontier even if the classifier thinks mid-tier is fine* (stakes), and the rules that say *for this kind of work, run mid-tier even if the classifier thinks frontier is warranted* (cost). The classifier reads the work. The router applies the policy. The frontier model runs only when the policy says it should.

The third piece is the one the doctrine is least apologetic about, because it does two jobs at once. The classifier-router architecture is hollow if the router can only choose between the same vendor's three closed-source tiers; right-sizing only means *down-routing among the same dependency* in that arrangement, and the dependency itself is what was producing most of the threat. The honest version of the discipline is a wider model stack — open-source code models for boilerplate and deterministic transforms, mid-size open-source models for summarization and extraction and classification, self-hosted models for internal-only tooling, locally-served models for high-volume background work, and the frontier model for work that genuinely needs it. The open-source / self-hosted option is doing two jobs at once. It is the cost-control option, and it is also the lever on the *concentration-of-power* register the Unit 4 Ethical dimension already names — decision power pooling inside three or four labs whose internal debates set the rules of the field. The practitioner who right-sizes is simultaneously controlling their token bill and refusing a dependency the broader field has not yet reckoned with. Treating it as cost-only undersells the doctrine, and I am going to be plain about that in the lesson that most exposes the bigger frame.

The honest catch sits in the same place it sat in Lesson 4. Lesson 4 punctured its own headline — *time is no longer the constraint* holds best for new work and breaks for the inherited mess — and this lesson has the same obligation. **Right-sizing only works where the classifier can read complexity reliably.** For genuinely judgment-dense, open-ended, or first-of-its-kind work — the work where Unit 4's Judgment dimension is most live — the classifier will under-read the task. It will see surface complexity and miss the *judgment density* underneath, and it will down-route a piece of work that should have gone to the frontier model. Frontier capability is still the right call for that work, and the discipline is not "always go cheap." The discipline is "stop defaulting upward." The same skepticism that applies to the self-spec agent at G2 — the one that quietly drafts its own marching orders — applies to the classifier in mirror: a classifier that quietly down-routes work it didn't understand has stepped onto the practitioner's side of the line in exactly the same way. The discipline against *that* failure mode is the same one the corpus uses everywhere: the policy is the practitioner's; the router carries explicit *escalate-when* rules; the trailing signal — *how often the policy overrode the classifier upward* — is itself a reading of whether the classifier is trustworthy on the work this practitioner is running, against this practitioner's baseline. Same idiographic principle as everywhere else.

The lesson has the highest operational density of any in the unit, and it earns that. AI coding harnesses do not classify and route by default, and a practitioner whose harness ships frontier-only is going to need actual moves to stand up the discipline. The body of the lesson — not this top-of-unit narrative pass, but the lesson content pass — carries the tips and tricks the harness doesn't ship with: manual routing rules per Skill, model-fleet configuration patterns, the policy-override rule pattern, the locally-served-model setup pattern, the cost-line-as-its-own-dashboard discipline. I am going to name what the lesson will teach without pretending to write the lesson here.

The board reads this discipline the way it reads steering. Most of the readings are *Auto* — your work hands them to the board without your help. Cost per unit of output, broken down by tier — the board reads which model ran which column for each ticket and what it cost. The *frontier-share of output* — what fraction of your work ran on closed-source frontier versus mid-tier or open-source — reads the dependency-concentration directly, and that one pairs with the Ethical dimension on the long clock. *Override-up rate* and *override-down rate* read whether the policy is in agreement with the classifier or whether the practitioner is constantly steering against it. *Rate-limit incidence* is the leading indicator of the same dependency the cost line lags. *Self-hosted utilization* reads whether the open-source option is being used or only available on paper. The lesson doesn't expand these — that's the work of the next lesson on the board — but it names what's being produced so the next lesson reads as harvest rather than introduction.

And Unit 2's kit was here too. Your audience pre-configured the default routing posture you walked into. SaaS Vendor Dependency builders walk into a shop with right-sizing foregrounded, because the kit knows that's the trade the situation is most exposed to. AI Development Gap builders walk into a shop where *classifier-readability* is a teaching surface — the bench is learning to *read* what right-sized looks like before they're asked to configure it. Legacy Software Debt builders walk into a shop where archaeology-first routing rules run characterization-test work and code-comprehension work at specific tiers, not at the default. External Dev Dependency builders walk into a shop where the routing posture is part of what the founder retains from the agency. Internal Talent builders walk into a shop where routing decisions double as a teaching surface for the bench. Prototype-to-Production Wall builders walk into a shop where deploy-tier and observability-tier routing rules exist that the prototype never had. The relocation ledger from Unit 2 books the routing choice as part of the trade — *not just which tool, but which model fleet runs that tool.* If you've been wondering why your shop's policy felt right out of the gate, that's why.

The agent will pick the most expensive option on your behalf, every time, unless you configured otherwise. Right-sized routing is how you stop renting capability you didn't choose to depend on — and how you keep the option of running on models you can actually run yourself.

## Lesson 6 — Teaching the AI Your Way (and the Knife-Edge Under It)

Back to the house rules, because we owe them more than Lesson 2 gave them. The Skills aren't just consistency machinery. They decide what the work does *to you.*

The same rulebook can be written two ways. One just hands you the finished thing — *here is the spec, here is the PR, here is the security review, here is the deploy plan, sign off when ready.* The other does the thing *and explains why it made each call* — *here is the spec; here are the three trade-offs I made and why; here is the place I left blank for you to fill, because this one is yours to decide.* The two rulebooks produce the same artifact. They do not produce the same human.

This is not a small choice and I am not going to soft-pedal it. The research is blunt: people who just take the output get measurably worse at the work over time. People who make the AI explain stay sharp. Same tool, opposite result. The Anthropic skill-formation work — the cleanest evidence we have on this so far — shows the effect is large, it shows up fast, and it sorts people into two populations that do not look like the same population a few months in. The mechanism is the one teachers have always known: you keep a faculty by exercising it, and a tool that lets you stop exercising it is a tool that lets your faculty go.

Your Unit 2 kit has been quietly doing this work for you the whole time, and this is the lesson where the mechanism becomes visible. For a skill that's yours to own — one you'll live with, one you'll be paged about at 2 a.m. — your agents are set to *teach.* They explain. They leave blanks. They make you reach. For a skill you'll only ever direct — one a specialist or an agent will always do the producing on — your agents are set to *just produce.* Enough for you to stay in charge. No more. The same agent, the same Skill, two modes. The authoring rule, which you will never need to remember but which decides whether the unit actually works, is the one the crosswalk holds: **write each Skill once, with both registers as switchable modes; select the mode by persona tag at intake — never maintain two Skill libraries.** The persona tag was set by the check in Unit 2 Lesson 2. The mode follows.

The other thing this same register choice does — and this is what Lesson 4 set us up to see clearly — is that it decides whether the middle is observable while it runs. *Produce-and-explain* doesn't just narrate to you for your own development; it exposes the agent's reasoning *in flight,* alongside the producing, so you can read it as it lands and steer before the artifact is finished. *Produce-only* doesn't just hold back the explanation; it is the opacity setting we named two lessons back, and it produces the rubber-stamp configuration at the back of the loop, because there is nothing for you to read in the middle. So the register choice is two decisions in one, and that is the way to hold it in your head: it decides what the work does *to you* (sharpen vs. hollow), and it decides what you can *do to the work* in flight (steer vs. wait). The two decisions are the same knob. You set it once, by persona tag, at intake. Get this right and you walk into a shop where the middle is legible and your hands are on the steering wheel. Get it wrong and you walk into a shop where every column produces a fluent artifact you then squint at from the outside.

The thing to police, and it's a different failure than the rubber-stamp from Lesson 4, is the one Lesson 4 already foreshadowed at the spec gate. An agent that quietly starts deciding *what* to build, not just *how* — writing its own marching orders — has stepped onto your side of the line. The G2 self-spec. The agent that drafts the spec it then implements has originated. It has done the front job and made the back job circular. The fix is plain: the spec must carry a human edit trail, or the gate rejects it. The mode the agent is running in — *explain, leave a blank* — is what makes the human edit trail likely to be real. *Produce* without *explain* invites the self-spec; *explain with a blank* refuses to write the blank in. *Read this through Lesson 4's lens, too:* the explain register is what catches the self-spec *in flight.* The agent's exposed reasoning is exactly where its frame begins to set yours, and that is the moment to steer — to interrupt, to edit, to redirect — before the marching orders are written. Without observability of the middle, the self-spec is silent; with it, the self-spec is the most visible moment in the gate. Steering is what closes the gap between the silent failure mode and the audible one.

This is the lesson that decides whether the rest of the unit makes you sharper or softer over time, and you pick which by how you set up the house rules. Choose to stay sharp.

## Lesson 7 — The Board Is Watching You, Too

Unit 1 ended with a problem we said we couldn't solve from inside the same body. You cannot trust the mirror, because the tired part of you is the part doing the checking. We promised an instrument. Here it is, and it was built right in the moment you set up the shop in Lesson 2.

Because the whole build runs through one place — the one place the work lives — it has been quietly recording how *you* worked. Not how you *felt.* How often you reshaped the agent's plan versus just accepting it. Whether you actually read the code before you approved it. How many of the things you built you genuinely *chose* versus rubber-stamped. The board does not need to ask you anything. It already knows.

The signals are small in number and load-bearing in importance. *Origination ratio* — what fraction of your tickets you originated versus accepted from the agent. *Reframes-vs-accepts* on the spec — did you edit it materially or wave it through. The *self-spec flag* — did the agent write the spec it then implemented. *`Ready`-gate decisions* — how many items you marked Ready versus how many the agent surfaced. *PR / diff review depth* — did you actually read the diff. The *inquiry-vs-delegation ratio* — across all your interactions, did you ask the agent to *explain* or only to *do.* Each signal is named at the gate it lives at; each gets hardened for whichever audience tends to rubber-stamp that gate. Your kit picked which to harden. You will see them. *Now read them through Lesson 4's lens,* because it changes what they actually are: almost every one of these is a **steering signal.** They are not measurements of whether you were diligent at the gate. They are measurements of whether you *held the in-flight motion* across the column. Reframes-vs-accepts is, literally, the count of steering actions per accepted artifact. The self-spec flag fires when the human's frame was replaced by the agent's mid-flight — a steering failure caught after the fact. The inquiry-vs-delegation ratio reads whether you stayed in conversation with the agent across the column or only at its boundary. The board reads steering depth, and what you are about to face is not whether you were careful on Tuesday; it is whether you stayed in the work.

Unlike *am I rested?,* none of this can be fudged. You can lie to a Likert scale; you can't lie to a diff that records whether you opened the file. The dashboard-don't-mirror move from Unit 1 — *instrument; do not introspect* — now operates on the work itself, not on the body. The work-system is the instrument. You did not have to build it. You built it the moment you set up the shop. And it is now operating at three scales, the way the observability doctrine already names: the self-dashboard from Unit 1 (the human reads themselves), the in-flight reading off the agent's reasoning that we named in Lesson 4 (the human reads the agent), and this — the work-system reading the human after the fact. One substrate. Three readings. Same instrument-don't-introspect doctrine across all three.

The real limit it exposes is the one you might not have been looking for. Agents can produce faster than any human can truly check. The thing that caps you isn't typing speed, and it isn't your kit, and it isn't your team. It is your capacity to review and to answer. That capacity is part of your condition — Unit 1's *you are the infrastructure,* now with an actual number on it. The number doesn't have a polite version. You will see it. The instrument that lets you see it is also the instrument that tells you when your condition is depleted, when your review depth dropped, when your origination ratio slipped because you spent a week shipping the agent's roadmap. You can do something about every one of those, but only if you see them. The board sees them whether you do or not.

That last point is the handoff. These quiet measurements are the seeds of the long-game questions — the ones you'll track not over a sprint, but over a career. The same signals, on a much longer clock, become the readings Unit 4 takes off you over years. Unit 4 reads what this unit measures. The board you set up in Lesson 2 is the seed of the dashboard you'll live with for the rest of your work.

## Lesson 8 — Where to Start Today, and How the Rest Helps You

We have set up the shop, walked the board, called out the two gates that never transfer, named the knife-edge under the house rules, and turned the work-system into your scoreboard. Before you close the unit, here is the one thing to do on your very next build, and a short map of how everything else helps you.

Your first move. On your next real piece of work — not a toy task, not a test, the next real thing you owe somebody — set up the shop for *one* task. One place it lives. The agents you have, configured by the kit your situation pointed at. The agents set to *produce and explain* in any column whose answerable half is yours, so the middle is observable in flight. The router configured to size each subagent's model to the work it actually has to do — not frontier on every column by default. And then run that one task all the way across the board, gates and all. Don't skip the originate gate; write the why-now in twelve words. Don't skip the Ready gate; reject one candidate on purpose. Steer at least once on purpose: while the spec or the design or the diff is still being produced, read the agent's reasoning, and press the interrupt key once to redirect — even if the trajectory looks fine. The deliberate interrupt is the calibration that confirms you are still inside the work, not outside it; treat it as a positive move, not an emergency one. Don't skip the review gate; read the diff. Don't skip the deploy gate; read one user-side outcome, not just the dashboard. Note the cost of this task's run on its way through the shop; the next task is the one where the board has a baseline to read against. One ticket. Nine gates. One deliberate steering action. One explicit routing policy. The exercise is small on purpose; everything else in this unit is a deeper version of it.

The unit in one line, and it's the line to hold when the lessons blur: **you run a full team's worth of work, but the two jobs at the ends stay yours — held in flight by steering through the middle, with explicit policy refusing the defaults — and the board keeps you honest about whether they really did.**

What's coming, and what it does for you. In the next unit, the quiet measurements your board emits grow into a handful of honest questions across nine dimensions — so that over years, not weeks, you can see what's actually under threat and choose your path instead of drifting into it. The signals you watched on the sprint clock get read on a career clock. Most of the dimensions Unit 4 will ask about are already lit. Unit 2's six situations seeded eight of the nine. The trades you made in Lesson 5 of that unit become the dimensions you track here. One ninth remains for Unit 4 to pick up; that is a known gap, and we will hold it.

The two clocks return one last time, because every unit ends here. *Becoming* is this build, this week, this first task across the gates. *Remaining* is the answerability you signed at the review gate, watched across every build you'll ship for as long as you ship. Same instrument. Two clocks. Now you know where the instrument is pointed, and you know what the clocks are reading.
