# Playbook Concept Manifest

*A sequenced ledger of what each lesson puts on the page, in book order. Read top-to-bottom to see what the playbook is introducing and building toward; scan the **Open** lines to find the holes worth filling next.*

*Companion to the four unit outlines and narratives ([unit-1.md](curriculum/lessons/unit-1.md) — graduated to lesson content, [unit-2-narrative.md](curriculum/outlines/unit-2-narrative.md), [unit-3-narrative.md](curriculum/outlines/unit-3-narrative.md), [unit-4-narrative.md](curriculum/outlines/unit-4-narrative.md)) and the [playbook-lexicon.md](doctrine/playbook-lexicon.md). Not lesson content.*

## How to read this

Each lesson row has the same six fields:

- **Promise** — the one-line "what you get" the lesson commits to.
- **Introduces** — concepts, terms, distinctions, scenes, or instruments first appearing on the page here.
- **Builds on** — prior lessons or doctrine the lesson depends on. A *Builds on* that points at something never introduced is a structural hole.
- **Argues** — the load-bearing claims the lesson advances. These are the places evidence, allegory, figure, table, or scene will likely be needed.
- **Returns to** — how the lesson closes its own felt-need loop (per [reader-orientation-pattern.md](doctrine/reader-orientation-pattern.md)).
- **Questions** — first questions opened on the page; answers indicated where they close downstream. A question never answered is a dangling thread.
- **Open** — known holes called out in the unit's source assumptions, plus structural gaps surfaced by this audit.

Status indicators in **Open**: ◇ already named in source assumptions; ◆ surfaced by this manifest audit.

## The arc in one scroll

**Unit 1 — Knowing Who Does What.** Names the stake asymmetry, the two intelligences, the two jobs, the condition problem, the self-instrumentation move, the watchdog, and the first build. Doctrine spine: *you bear all the risk; the machine bears none.* Plants the maintenance trilogy (condition, observability, upkeep).

**Unit 2 — Finding Your Situation.** Splits the floor builder by builder. Intake reads behavior (situation) + wiring (operating profile). Build-vs-borrow per skill. Five universal moves. Six named situations, each a trade. Doctrine spine: *every escape is a trade; choose which dependency is worth holding.*

**Unit 3 — Running the Day-to-Day.** Re-staffs the SDLC with agents (ADLC). Sets up the shop (system of record, subagents, Skills, MCP). Walks one ticket across nine gates. Names the two non-transferable gates (G1, G7) and the in-flight motion that holds them (**steering**). Refuses two agent defaults: opacity in the middle (Lesson 4) and frontier-on-every-task (Lesson 5). Skill register is a knife-edge (transfer vs. substitution). Board reads the human after the fact.

**Unit 4 — Playing the Long Game.** Pivots from short clock to long clock. Names nine viability dimensions in three families. Hands a menu of strategies per dimension. The same board reads on the long clock. The un-delegable move is *picking and doing*; two demonstrations on Clay's own readings. Door 1 (think). Door 2 (measure / cohort). Loop closes back to *Did I choose this, or did it choose me?*

# Unit 1 — Knowing Who Does What

## Lesson 1 — Why This Is the Best Time to Build (and Why It's on You)

- **Promise:** The opportunity is real, the stakes are personal, and the curriculum teaches you to win and keep it.
- **Introduces:** The six things the work has given Clay (canvas, discipline that travels, life-shape, living, world, teaching) as the stake; *the wager* — **you bear all the risk; the machine bears none**; *Becoming viable* / *Remaining viable* as the two horizons of one stance; *vigilance is the opportunity's bodyguard*; the *seed → bloom → harvest* teaching contract (implicit, via the "what's coming" map).
- **Builds on:** Nothing in-book (first lesson). Background: builder has lived enough of pre-AI software to feel what is being put in play.
- **Argues:** (1) AI capability to hand a goal and get working software is real and improving; (2) the asymmetry of stake is the load-bearing claim of the whole kit; (3) mistakes cost more now because building got fast and cheap; (4) the same skills that *win* the opportunity are the ones that *hold* it.
- **Returns to:** The six things in play — every one of them in flux right now; which way it goes is on you.
- **Questions:** Opens "*Did I choose this, or did it choose me?*" implicitly (formal naming arrives in Lesson 3). Opens the *what stays yours* question (answered Lesson 3).
- **Open:**
  - ◇ Clay-grounded personal opening confirmed; *scarcity-to-capability* is deliberately deferred to Lesson 6.
  - ◆ The "you bear all the risk" claim is argued by sentence, not evidence. Likely needs a concrete scene of mis-priced risk — Clay's own or one the builder can feel — to keep it from reading as slogan.
  - ◆ "Building got fast and cheap" is asserted without quantification. Either an allegory (the kitchen-table demo that shipped in a week and broke at scale) or a small figure (before/after of a typical product cycle) would prove it without leaving the voice.

## Lesson 2 — Two Kinds of Intelligence at the Table

- **Promise:** Stop competing with the AI; learn what each side is for.
- **Introduces:** *Artificial Intelligence (AI)* and *Organic Intelligence (OI)* as paired terms; the AI's autonomy over *means but not ends*; the agent's *consistency* (3 a.m. = 10 a.m.); the OI's *finitude* (alive, time runs out, choices land on people); *weather* (introduced lightly as "you have weather").
- **Builds on:** Lesson 1's wager.
- **Argues:** (1) AI has real autonomy over means; (2) AI has never crossed into authoring ends; (3) only OI is answerable in the registers that matter (economic, legal, ethical); (4) you suffer something the agent does not — your weather changes; (5) the smart move isn't competition, it's role division.
- **Returns to:** "*The smart move isn't you-vs-the-machine. It's giving each side the half it's built for.*"
- **Questions:** Implicitly raises "*which half is which?*" — answered in Lesson 3.
- **Open:**
  - ◆ The claim "AI has never crossed into ends, and shows no sign of crossing" is a strong empirical bet. It needs a footnote pointer to evidence or a near-future caveat — or it dates badly.
  - ◆ *Weather* is introduced too lightly here for its later weight. By Lesson 4 the builder needs it loaded; this lesson could plant it more deliberately as a named instrument-reading rather than a metaphor in passing.
  - ◆ No concrete allegory yet for the *autonomy-over-means/not-ends* distinction. A scene (the agent that built a perfect caching layer for the wrong problem) would land it.

## Lesson 3 — The Two Jobs That Stay Yours Forever

- **Promise:** Your worth doesn't move with the capability frontier; these two jobs never transfer.
- **Introduces:** *The two ends* / *the two jobs* (the load-bearing geometry); *Origination* (the front job); *Answerability* / *Accountability* (the back job); *the producible middle*; the *Think / Decide / Create* split (producible vs. answerable halves); the first formal naming of "*Did I choose this, or did it choose me?*"; the *two failure modes of origination* — abdication by disuse (atrophy) and abdication by over-distribution (more parallel than you can stand behind).
- **Builds on:** Lesson 1 wager; Lesson 2 two intelligences.
- **Argues:** (1) Drawing the line at *what AI can't do* is the wrong line; (2) drawing the line at *start* and *answer for* survives capability advances; (3) generation ≠ authorship; getting work done ≠ being right to do the work; (4) the AI does not seize origination — you let it go; (5) parallel capacity is bounded by what you can stand behind (Clay's own ceiling: one to three things really well).
- **Returns to:** Both ends belong to you, until you stop using them; then nobody holds them, and the work loses its anchor.
- **Questions:** Asks "*Did I choose this, or did it choose me?*" — recurs Unit 2 Lesson 4 (at module/kit layer), Unit 3 Lesson 4 (at gate layer), Unit 4 Lesson 6 (at career layer, as loop-close).
- **Open:**
  - ◇ The teaching-agent / transfer-vs-substitution distinction is held back; it will land in Unit 2 (Lesson 3 foreshadow) and bloom in Unit 3 Lesson 6.
  - ◆ The "one to three things really well" claim is Clay's lived ceiling, but the lesson doesn't anchor it in a specific story. A scene of the moment Clay realized he was originating in name only would convert a number into a felt truth.
  - ◆ The Think/Decide/Create split is given three crisp one-liners but no extended example. A small worked example (the caching-layer scene, sketched but not built out) appears here — could be the place a figure or table belongs.

## Lesson 4 — The Catch: You're the Weak Link Now (and That's Fixable)

- **Promise:** You'll spot when you're the problem — the quiet kind — before it costs you.
- **Introduces:** *Condition* as concept; *the drunk-driver problem* (impairment and self-read fail together); the maintenance-trilogy frame (instrument yourself like a system); **you are the infrastructure** as named doctrine; *the five dimensions of condition* (Cognitive, Emotional, Financial, Physical, Spiritual / sleep, focus, mood, drive, money) — these are the *condition* dimensions, not the long-clock viability nine; *condition is the binding constraint (time → condition)*; the *failure-moves-from-loud-to-quiet* claim (the agent took the volume; what's left is judgment-dense and low-volume).
- **Builds on:** Lesson 2 weather, Lesson 3 two ends.
- **Argues:** (1) The middle's high volume used to dilute your condition; with the agent doing the volume, your condition flows nearly undiluted into the dense calls; (2) failure moved from loud to quiet; (3) in-the-moment self-assessment fails because the impaired faculty is the one doing the assessing; (4) you would never run an agent on degraded infrastructure — stop doing it with yourself.
- **Returns to:** "*Am I in shape to originate the work and to answer for it — or am I about to let a degraded instrument make the only calls that were ever mine to make?*"
- **Questions:** Opens "*how do I read myself, given that I can't?*" — answered Lesson 5.
- **Open:**
  - ◇ Narrative draft seeds the maintenance trilogy across Lesson 4–6 (bloom), inconsistent with the conceptual outline's seed-only reading. If the seed-only reading wins, Lesson 4–6 compress.
  - ◆ Five condition dimensions are named but not anchored in research or trial scenes. This is the lesson where field/clinical evidence (sleep & judgment, financial stress & risk tolerance) would not be out of place — and it would head off the builder who hears "physical, spiritual" and dismisses the lesson as wellness writing.
  - ◆ The *failure-loud-to-quiet* shift is one of the unit's most distinctive arguments and currently lives in prose alone. A side-by-side (old pipeline: hundreds of small calls / hundreds of catches; new pipeline: handful of dense calls / no catches) would carry it visually.

## Lesson 5 — How to Check Yourself (Since You Can't Trust the Mirror)

- **Promise:** An honest read on your own state, using the trick you already use on every other system.
- **Introduces:** *Instrument; don't introspect*; the *three properties of a real self-check* (answer lives outside your head, ask on a schedule not mid-decision, target your specific failure); the *am I rested? vs. how many diffs did I wave through?* dishonest/honest pair; the *condition dimensions reread as instruments* (physical = override-fired; cognitive = reframes-vs-accepts; emotional = rejects-on-merit-vs-on-mood; spiritual = origination ratio; financial = the tripwire set in advance); the *financial-tripwire-built-in-advance* discipline; two cautions (optimization reflex / agent can read but cannot care).
- **Builds on:** Lesson 4 you-are-infrastructure; Lesson 2 weather.
- **Argues:** (1) You already know how to instrument every system *except* yourself; (2) the structural reason you've left yourself dark is that observer and observed collapse into a single impaired instrument; (3) self-report lies; behavioral trace doesn't; (4) the optimization reflex is the corporate dashboard creeping back in — refuse it.
- **Returns to:** "*What am I not letting myself ask — and what signal would answer it that isn't my own say-so?*"
- **Questions:** Opens "*who reads the dashboard if I'm the only builder and I can't be trusted?*" — answered Lesson 6.
- **Open:**
  - ◆ The three properties (outside head / on schedule / targets your failure) are doctrine-clean but currently abstract. A worked example showing one practitioner's actual schedule, actual reading, actual prior week's data would land the doctrine.
  - ◆ The five-dimensions-reread-as-instruments is the single highest-density passage in the unit. It's a table waiting to be drawn.

## Lesson 6 — Staying in Shape and Building Your Own Alarm

- **Promise:** Keep your edge over a whole career, and build the alarm that fires even when you'd rather ignore it.
- **Introduces:** *Memory as condition* (working memory = context window; long-term memory = the store); *memory is what the judgment is made of*; the four upkeep practices (*externalize the frame before you prompt*, *make the store double as the dashboard*, *keep the muscle you actually need in use*, *protect the dense calls*); the *watchdog* / *coffee cup on the dash* / the *drowsy-driver-alert analogy*; the *page is delegable; the heeding is not* knife-edge; the *this time is different* failure mode.
- **Builds on:** Lesson 5 instrumentation move; Lesson 4 condition; agent-memory analogy assumes the builder has touched modern AI tooling.
- **Argues:** (1) The upkeep problem is one level up from the reading problem — someone has to look at the dashboard, and that someone is the same impaired instrument; (2) the car solved this by reading behavior, not asking the driver; (3) the same move works for cognition; (4) a monitor built to keep you engaged is worse than none — point it at judgment, not output.
- **Returns to:** "*When the signal I pre-committed to trips — when the coffee cup lights up — do I pull over, or do I explain to myself why this time is different?*"
- **Questions:** Opens "*will I heed?*" — answered Unit 4 Lesson 6 as the un-delegable move.
- **Open:**
  - ◇ Strongest remaining slot for a named Clay scene: a watchdog he built or wished he'd built, and the page he did or didn't heed. Currently posture, not history.
  - ◆ The drowsy-driver analogy is the unit's load-bearing allegory and lives in two paragraphs of prose. Worth a single small figure (car telemetry → coffee cup; your telemetry → page) — it would also do work in Unit 3 Lesson 7 and Unit 4 Lesson 5 where the analogy is ported.

## Lesson 7 — Where to Start Today, and How the Rest Helps You

- **Promise:** One thing to do on build #1, plus a map of what's ahead.
- **Introduces:** *The first-build rung* (externalize the frame + notice one number — count outputs accepted without really reading); the *whole-kit-in-one-line* compression; the forward map to Units 2/3/4.
- **Builds on:** All prior Unit 1 doctrine — this is the unit's return lesson.
- **Argues:** (1) The smallest possible practice is sufficient on build #1; (2) every later practice in the book is a deeper version of those two moves; (3) there is one stance held at two horizons.
- **Returns to:** "*Two clocks. One stance. Same you.*"
- **Questions:** None new; sets up Unit 2 (which job do you drop?), Unit 3 (the ADLC), Unit 4 (the long clock).
- **Open:**
  - ◆ The first-build rung is two practices given as instructions. A "before and after" — what build #1 looks like with and without the rung — would convert instruction to demonstration.

# Unit 2 — Finding Your Situation

## Lesson 1 — This Kit Doesn't Have One Builder

- **Promise:** Stop reading a generic playbook; start getting the one built for where you actually are.
- **Introduces:** The *splits-after-Unit-1* move (same two jobs, different end dropped); the *six common spots* preview; the *not-a-decision-tree* contract (single lesson sequence; configuration runs behind the scenes); the *situation / persona / wiring* three-layer composition (preview); the *kit isn't a cage* preview.
- **Builds on:** All of Unit 1 (assumes wager, two ends, condition).
- **Argues:** (1) The two jobs are universal; the dropped end is not; (2) the same module library serves all builders if configured by situation/persona/wiring; (3) the builder walks one sequence — branching happens behind the scenes.
- **Returns to:** "*Six common spots. Each with a kit. None of them a cage. That is the offer.*"
- **Questions:** Opens "*which end do I drop?*" — answered Lesson 2.
- **Open:**
  - ◆ The three-layer composition (situation / persona / wiring) is named here, defined in Lesson 2. A single line distinguishing them at first mention would prevent the builder from collapsing them into a vague triad.

## Lesson 2 — The Check That Points You to Your Spot

- **Promise:** An honest read on where you stand, even though you can't grade yourself.
- **Introduces:** *The intake* (the check); *the Two Ends Diagnostic* (content half — which end you hold badly, read off behavior); *the Operating Profile* (wiring half — your climate, distinct from Unit 1's weather); *climate vs. weather* fully named; *functional, not categorical* / *instrument the constraint, don't type the person*; *route, not a grade*; *the composition rule* — **situation picks the module set; persona sets develop-vs-borrow; wiring tunes the delivery**.
- **Builds on:** Unit 1 Lesson 5 (instrument-don't-introspect); Unit 1 Lesson 2/4 weather.
- **Argues:** (1) The mirror problem from Unit 1 doesn't shrink for the situation question; (2) pop-psychology personality typing is noise even when valid, because the job is to *tune delivery*, not *describe the person*; (3) the intake's output is a route, not a label; you re-take it as you change; (4) climate must be read because the agent is consistent across builders and you are not.
- **Returns to:** *Three reads, one composition that quietly runs the rest of the kit.*
- **Questions:** Opens "*how does the same module land on different climates?*" — answered Lesson 6 (borrow the module, not the register) and Unit 3 Lesson 6 (register switch by persona tag at intake).
- **Open:**
  - ◇ The *Operating Profile* is bloomed here, but Unit 1 contains no seed for it (drift carried forward, called out in source assumptions).
  - ◆ The intake itself is described conceptually but not shown. A worked example or sketched form (three behavior questions, the resulting situation + climate read) would convert the doctrine into an artifact a builder could point at.
  - ◆ The *climate-not-categorical* argument needs at least a footnote pointer to the validity-of-personality-types literature, or the builder is asked to take a strong empirical claim on the lesson's say-so.

## Lesson 3 — Build It, or Borrow It — Knowing Which

- **Promise:** The single call that saves the most wasted effort, per skill: own it or direct it.
- **Introduces:** *Develop the faculty / Borrow the frame* as the unit's sharpest tool; the *both-traps-real* framing (over-build vs. under-borrow); the *same situation, opposite calls* observation (persona overlay); the *register switch* foreshadow — **write each Skill once, with both registers as switchable modes; select the mode by persona tag at intake — never maintain two Skill libraries** (the authoring rule, bloomed in Unit 3 Lesson 6); borrow-now-can-become-build-later (the decision is reversible by intent).
- **Builds on:** Lesson 2 intake (persona tag); Unit 1 Lesson 3 two ends, narrowed to per-skill.
- **Argues:** (1) Both answers (develop / borrow) are legitimate; the lazy move is not picking; (2) you can direct a craft you don't own, but you cannot stand behind work in one; (3) build the full faculty in a skill you'll never personally exercise and you atrophy from disuse; (4) same situation can hand two builders different calls — persona changes the default.
- **Returns to:** The register-switch standard — same Skill, two modes, select by persona tag.
- **Questions:** Opens "*how does the same Skill teach me differently than it teaches someone else?*" — answered Unit 3 Lesson 6.
- **Open:**
  - ◇ Strong Clay-scene slot — a build/borrow call he got wrong both ways (develop-when-should-have-borrowed and the reverse). Currently posture, not memoir.
  - ◆ The four-way grid (situation × persona → develop/borrow default) is conceptually crisp but never drawn. A 2×N table would carry it in one glance.

## Lesson 4 — The Moves Everyone Makes

- **Promise:** Five moves that go in every kit, whoever you are.
- **Introduces:** *The five universal moves*:
  1. *Did I choose this, or did it choose me?* — pointed at the kit, not the build.
  2. *You are the infrastructure.* (ported from S1 Lesson 4 as operating condition of the kit.)
  3. *Every fix is a trade.* (planted here; bloomed in Lesson 5 as the *relocation lens*.)
  4. *Stay able to check work you didn't write.* (review capacity; bloomed in S3 Lesson 7 as a gate that cannot be handed off.)
  5. *Say what "right" means before you build.* (the front-end discipline that makes the back-end discipline possible.)
- **Builds on:** Unit 1 Lesson 3 (origination), Lesson 4 (you-are-infrastructure); Lesson 1 (the wager — the risk does not leave you).
- **Argues:** (1) The kit configures the middle; these five hold the ends regardless of situation; (2) the AI writes faster than anyone can read — review is the unique unaccelerated bottleneck; (3) a frame written in advance is one you can check the output against.
- **Returns to:** "*Five moves. These are the floor. Your situation adds the rest on top.*"
- **Questions:** Opens "*what trade does my situation actually ask of me?*" — answered Lesson 5.
- **Open:**
  - ◆ Move 4 (review capacity) is asserted as a discipline; its dual placement (Unit 3 Lesson 7) is signaled but not yet structurally connected. Worth a single sentence here noting that this discipline becomes a *gate* in Unit 3 — to bind the unit transition.
  - ◆ Move 3 (*every fix is a trade*) is named here as universal, then immediately re-introduced in Lesson 5 as the unit's reframe. Risk of builder feeling the same idea twice without the seam being visible. A clearer "*we name it here; we bloom it next lesson*" hand-off would help.

## Lesson 5 — Find Your Situation

- **Promise:** The six most common spots people are stuck in; find yours, meet the kit.
- **Introduces:** *Every escape is a trade* / *the relocation lens* (bloomed); *you are trading up to a dependency worth holding* (origination pointed at the dependency itself); *the relocation ledger* (Shed / Adopted columns); the *force-three-Adopted-entries + sign a residual-risk statement* discipline; *the residual-risk statement* as the converting artifact; the **six situations** as scenes (authoring code + builder-facing name + risk + kit + trade + long-game question + lit dimensions):
  1. *Internal Talent Worth Developing* — "you've got people worth growing" → Identity + Cognitive.
  2. *SaaS Vendor Dependency* — "you're renting what you could own" → Temporal + Economic.
  3. *External Dev Dependency* — "someone else builds your software" → Differential + Economic.
  4. *Legacy Software Debt* — "you inherited a mess you're scared to touch" → Judgment.
  5. *AI Development Gap* — "your team ships what it can't explain" (two shapes: stalled / blind-velocity) → Epistemic + Cognitive.
  6. *Prototype-to-Production Wall* — "your demo has to become a real product" → Ethical.
- **Builds on:** Lesson 3 develop/borrow; Lesson 4 universals (especially *every fix is a trade*).
- **Argues:** (1) The instinct to seek the *fix that gets rid of* the dependency is wrong — no move sheds risk; it relocates it; (2) the discipline is mechanical (ledger + residual-risk signature), not philosophical; (3) each situation drops a different job and makes a different trade; (4) eight of nine Unit 4 dimensions are seeded here — Vocational is the deliberate exception.
- **Returns to:** "*Each spot drops a different job and makes a different trade — but the trade is the part you'll be tracking for the rest of your career.*"
- **Questions:** Each situation hands the builder a long-game question that recurs in Unit 4 Lesson 3 by name:
  - ITW: *Is my bench actually getting stronger, or quietly going dull?* (→ S4 Cognitive sibling.)
  - SVD: *Did the time and money I saved actually show up — or get eaten by upkeep?* (→ S4 Temporal/Economic.)
  - EDD: *What makes me different from the next founder with the same stack — and does it still pay?* (→ S4 Differential/Economic.)
  - LSD: *How much of the judgment about this code is still mine?* (→ S4 Judgment.)
  - AID: *Can I still make that call as the volume keeps climbing?* (→ S4 Epistemic/Cognitive.)
  - PPW: *Am I ready to be accountable in ways a prototype never asked of me?* (→ S4 Ethical.)
- **Open:**
  - ◇ Five per-situation Clay-scene slots open and named (build/borrow gone wrong, build-vs-buy year-two reveal, agency-relationship lesson, legacy untangling method, prototype crossing). All currently posture.
  - ◇ Vocational is harvest-forward — no Unit 2 seed exists. Source assumption flags this; manifest confirms it.
  - ◆ Six situations are written as scenes in the narrative but the outline carries them in bulleted-list form. A consistent "scene + risk + kit + trade + long-game question" template (already implicit) would benefit from being drawn once as a one-page-per-situation reference — though that may be a downstream asset, not a lesson inclusion.

## Lesson 6 — Your Kit Isn't a Cage

- **Promise:** Freedom to borrow, so the playbook fits your real life.
- **Introduces:** *Default collection / borrowable modules*; the *deliberate-not-random* borrow discipline; the *two near-universal modules* every kit usually needs (review capacity, say-what-"right"-means-first); the *borrow the module, not the register* rule — borrows still get taught in your persona's mode (develop or frame).
- **Builds on:** Lesson 5 six situations; Lesson 3 develop/borrow; Lesson 4 universals.
- **Argues:** (1) Real life rarely fits one box — most builders cross situations; (2) borrows must be deliberate; random borrowing hollows the kit; (3) your persona governs how a borrowed module is taught, not which one you borrow.
- **Returns to:** The kit is built to travel; borrowing is how it travels with you.
- **Questions:** None new; refines kit-as-cage worry.
- **Open:**
  - ◆ The "two near-universals" appear in Lesson 4 as universal moves and again here as near-universal borrows. The distinction (always-in vs. usually-in) is real but not crisp on the page — a one-sentence reconciliation would close the seam.
  - ◆ A *borrow grid* is referenced ("use it the way a working chef uses someone else's knife") but never shown. Either commit to drawing it (a 6×N grid of borrowable modules per situation) or remove the reference.

## Lesson 7 — Where to Start Today, and How the Rest Helps You

- **Promise:** One thing to do this week, plus a map of what's ahead.
- **Introduces:** The *take-the-check + name-your-situation + run-the-first-exercise* rung; *one stance, two clocks, same instrument — now you know where it is pointed*; the eight-of-nine dimension count (Vocational gap surfaced).
- **Builds on:** All Unit 2.
- **Argues:** (1) The smallest practice is sufficient; (2) there is no single right path; (3) the trades made here become the long-game readings in Unit 4.
- **Returns to:** *Becoming = this situation, this kit. Remaining = the trade just signed, watched across every situation you'll move through.*
- **Questions:** None new; opens forward to Unit 3 (kit becomes setup sheet) and Unit 4 (trades become dimensions).
- **Open:**
  - ◇ Vocational seed gap acknowledged in body. Decision held: does Vocational belong only to the long clock, or does Unit 2 need a situation pointer to it?

# Unit 3 — Running the Day-to-Day

## Lesson 1 — What the Day-to-Day Becomes (When a Crew of Agents Does the Middle)

- **Promise:** One person can now run the whole build; that doesn't change who's in charge.
- **Introduces:** *Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC)* taught in plain language (the lesson's one place to assume zero prior knowledge); *the agentic-SDLC catch* — doing the steps well used to take a team; *the Agent Development Life Cycle (ADLC)* (claimed as the playbook's term); the *the-agents-do-the-middle; you-keep-the-two-jobs* compression; *steering* named lightly (bloomed in Lesson 4); the *relay race → continuous flow* shift (from prior literature).
- **Builds on:** Unit 1 two ends; Unit 2 kit (preview of how it lands in Unit 3 Lesson 2).
- **Argues:** (1) The SDLC is not arbitrary — each step kills a specific risk; (2) scrappy startups skipped most of it and paid interest; (3) AI agents now do the work inside every step; (4) the path stays, the team becomes a crew of agents.
- **Returns to:** *The agents do the middle; you keep the two jobs; you stay in the work in between (steering).*
- **Questions:** Opens "*what is steering?*" — answered Lesson 4. Opens "*how does the kit land here?*" — answered Lesson 2.
- **Open:**
  - ◇ Outline open decision: do we want a small plain-language before/after figure (human-team SDLC vs. agent-crew ADLC) alongside the prose? Manifest read: yes — Lesson 1 is the unit's one assumes-no-prior-knowledge moment, and the figure earns its keep.
  - ◇ Endnote-vs-body call on the "Agentic Development Life Cycle" disambiguation. Outline recommends endnote.

## Lesson 2 — Your Shop: Where the Work Lives, the Team, and the House Rules

- **Promise:** Know the handful of pieces that make up an AI-run build; see how your Unit 2 kit configures them.
- **Introduces:** The *shop* metaphor and its four pieces, each named in plain terms with the technical name in parentheses once:
  1. *One place the work lives* (system of record) — *the to-do list IS the plan*; substrate that makes the agent's work readable as it runs (steering precondition, surfaced Lesson 4).
  2. *The team* (*subagents*).
