## Lesson 1 — A Different Clock
 
*What you get: you'll see why winning the opportunity and keeping it are the same job measured on two clocks — and why the second clock is the one almost nobody is watching.*
 
- Three units in, you can do the thing: hand a goal to a crew of agents, hold the two jobs at the ends, and run a real build start to finish. That's *Becoming* viable — the short clock, this build.
- But there's a second clock running underneath, and it ticks in years. The question on it isn't "can I build this?" It's "will I still be worth anything as a builder a decade from now — sharp, trusted, and paid?"
- The trap of the short clock: every reading on it is flattering. A great week, a shipped feature, a happy client. None of it tells you whether the slow things — your judgment, your edge, your reasons for doing this at all — are gaining or quietly draining.
- This is the same stance you've held the whole kit, just held longer. *One instrument — you — pointed at this build, and kept fit across every build.* The faculties that win the opportunity are the exact ones you have to keep in form to hold onto it.
- What this unit does: turns the long clock into nine plain questions you can actually read — and hands you a menu of strategies for each, instead of one right answer.
*The takeaway: becoming viable is this build; remaining viable is every build after. Same you, longer clock.*

## Lesson 2 — Questions You Can't Lie To
 
*What you get: you'll learn why the long-game questions are trends, not a test — and why the answers come from your work, not your gut.*
 
- Back to Unit 1's hardest truth: you can't trust the mirror, because the part of you doing the checking is the part that goes first. That problem doesn't shrink over a career — it grows. Ask yourself once a year "am I still sharp?" and you'll get the most flattering answer available every single time.
- So none of these are snapshot questions. They're **trend** questions — read over months and years, off a string of readings, not a feeling on a given afternoon.
- And the readings come in two kinds. Most of them, your work hands you without your help — call these the **automatic** ones: how often you reshaped an agent's plan versus rubber-stamped it, how your pricing moved, how your token bill moved, how you described your own work in your last ten bios. These sit in your transcripts, your repo, your calendar, your invoices already. You can't fudge them, because you're not the one taking them.
- A few questions, though, genuinely need *you* to answer — call these the **asked** ones: could you still explain, cold, a system you built three months ago? Did this week's work leave you lit up or hollowed out? You answer in the lightest form the question allows — a one-line note at session's end, a longer walk-through now and then, an essay once a year — and then that answer *joins the pile* and gets read as a trend like everything else. An asked answer isn't trusted self-report; it's self-report turned into a tracked reading.
- The two kinds run at their own speeds. Some readings come every session, some weekly, some once a year — and the slow ones aren't lazy. An annual question deserves an annual answer; you don't take your career's temperature hourly.
- One honesty note, said plainly so we never have to apologize for it later: this is a way to read *yourself against your own past*, not a graded test with a right answer. It tells you which way you're drifting. It doesn't stamp a number on your worth.
*The takeaway: trends, not snapshots — and the most honest readings are the ones you don't get to grade.*

## Lesson 3 — The Nine Questions, in Three Families
 
*What you get: the nine things actually under threat over a career — grouped so you can see what each one is really about, and how you'd read it.*
 
Each question below comes with the same three things: what's under threat, how you'd read it (automatic, asked, or both), and where in the curriculum you first met it. The full list of readings for each lives on the inquiry's pages — here we're after the shape, not the spreadsheet. *Lesson 3 names what's under threat and how to read it; Lesson 4 lays out strategies to try in response (plural per dimension, written as patterns to adapt); Lesson 6 carries the harder question of whether you'll actually heed what you see, modeled on one or two of Clay's own readings.*
 
### Family I — Is the *person* still intact? *(Practitioner)*
 
- **Am I getting sharper, or going dull? (Cognitive)** — Under threat: the thinking that built your judgment in the first place, and the focus to hold a hard problem in your head for an hour, as you hand more of the work to the model. *Read it:* automatically, from how much of the reasoning in your transcripts is yours versus the agent's, and how often you accept, edit, or reject what it hands you; and once a year, asked — could you still solve a fixed set of problems with the AI switched off? *(First met in Unit 1: "you are the infrastructure." Unit 2's grow-your-people situation asked the team version: is my bench getting stronger or quietly going dull?)*
- **Do I actually understand what I shipped? (Epistemic)** — Under threat: whether you *know* a system the way you'd know code you wrote by hand, when it got built through a conversation with an agent. *Read it:* asked, monthly — explain a system you built 30–90 days ago, cold, no agent; draw its shape from memory and check it against the truth. Automatically — how much of its documentation you wrote versus the agent. *(First met in Unit 2's "ships what it can't explain" situation, and Unit 3's review gate.)*
- **Does this still feel like work I want to do? (Vocational)** — Under threat: whether the work still pulls you, as it shifts from solving problems to steering and reviewing. The soft one everyone dismisses — and the one most likely to predict who quietly leaves the field over the next ten years. *Read it:* asked, weekly and yearly — what are you noticing about the texture of the work; does a session leave you charged or drained; would you want to work exactly this way for another decade. *(The newest of the nine — lightly seeded earlier, harvested mostly here. Worth a flag below.)*
- **When the machine builds it, what's still mine? (Identity)** — Under threat: the sense that what you bring is meaningful — that you can stand behind the work with pride when an agent wrote the code and you steered. *Read it:* automatically, from how you describe your own contribution in bios and bylines and transcripts — "I built," "I orchestrated," "I shipped" — and which role you speak from. Asked, once a year — what do you actually believe the human adds now. *(First met in Unit 1's two jobs, and Unit 2's grow-your-people trade.)*
### Family II — Is what I *sell* still worth selling? *(Practice)*
 
- **When everyone can do this, what's the value of *me* doing it? (Differential)** — Under threat: the slice of what you're paid that comes from being *early*, not from being *good*. The early-adopter premium is, by definition, temporary. *Read it:* automatically — how your pricing moves for comparable work, whether prospects bring you "help me start" questions or harder "what next" ones, how fast your distinctive language spreads into everyone else's mouths. *(First met in Unit 2's "someone else builds your software" situation — once everyone has the same agents, what's left that's yours.)*
- **What happens when the models do the judgment I sell? (Judgment)** — Under threat: the architectural, strategic, and design judgment that AI accelerates today but doesn't yet originate — and that is migrating into the models faster than anyone has reckoned with. *Read it:* on a locked set of hard problems, how the gap between your call and the frontier model's call moves with each new release — and each week, the three calls you made that the model wouldn't have. *(First met in Unit 2's "inherited a mess" situation — how much of the judgment is still yours.)*
- **Does the time I "save" actually come back to me? (Temporal)** — Under threat: the whole promise — that the hours AI gives back become *yours*. Every past productivity boom suggests the dividend gets eaten by rising expectations instead of reclaimed. *Read it:* automatically — how your hours split between reactive and generative, whether your total weekly hours are climbing; asked, quarterly — how many hours did you actually take back, and what did you do with them? *(First met in Unit 2's "renting what you could own" situation — did the savings show up, or get eaten by upkeep?)*
### Family III — Is the *ground* still ground? *(Conditions)*
 
- **Will what's profitable today still be profitable tomorrow? (Economic)** — Under threat: the unit economics of the whole way of working, currently subsidized by investors chasing market share. What happens when the subsidy ends and the real cost meets the list price. *Read it:* automatically — your token cost per unit of output, your margin with AI tooling as its own line, your dependencies' prices month over month, and the signals providers send (rate limits, deprecations, new enterprise tiers). *(First met in Unit 2's renting/agency situations — the cost side of every trade. Now also lit operationally by Unit 3 Lesson 5 — the classify-and-route discipline emits the long-clock readings this dimension reads.)*
- **Is what I'm doing defensible — to myself, my clients, the field, and the people downstream? (Ethical)** — Under threat: whether the practice can answer for itself across every register it touches — the environmental cost of the compute, the humans you used to pay and no longer do, work whose origins you can't trace attributed to you, decision power pooling inside three or four labs, and code you didn't write shipped to people you'll never meet. *Read it:* automatically — how you disclose AI's hand in your work, what fraction of what you ship is machine-made, whether you reach for the smaller model when it would do; asked, once a year — where you actually stand. The practice doesn't get to call itself ethical while the people downstream don't yet agree. *(First met in Unit 2's "demo to real product" situation — being accountable in registers a prototype never owed. Concentration-of-power register operationally addressed by Unit 3 Lesson 5: open-source / self-hosted-as-first-class-option refuses lab concentration as a silent dependency.)*
*The takeaway: three families — the person, the practice, the ground. Nine questions. Every one of them was planted earlier in the kit; here is where they come due — and the next lesson hands you strategies for each.*

## Lesson 4 — Strategies for the Nine
 
*What you get: a menu of strategies for each dimension — multiple per dimension, drawn from Clay's practice, the field's existing approaches, and reasonable experimentation — presented as patterns to evaluate against your own readings and adapt to your own situation. Not the right moves. Moves worth considering, in a shape you can borrow without copying.*
 
- A playbook is about strategies, not answers. The lesson that just named the nine threats is followed by a lesson that lays out *plausible responses to each*, plural per dimension, written so you can read across them and pick the one or two that fit your situation. The builder's job is to evaluate and adapt, not to copy.
- **The shape of a strategy.** Each carries the same four things, kept short:
  - *Reading it addresses* — which Auto or Asked signal from Lesson 3 it's a response to.
  - *What the move is* — the practice itself, named in one or two sentences.
  - *Where it fits* — which kind of practitioner or situation tends to need it.
  - *Watchpoint* — what would tell you the strategy isn't working, so you don't run it as ritual.
- **What this lesson is not.** Not a prescription. Not a curriculum the builder is meant to complete. Not the right moves — just *moves worth considering*. The honesty ceiling from Lesson 2 holds here: *you read these against your own situation, not against a model practitioner's checklist.* If a strategy is obviously not a fit, skip it. If a dimension's strategies all look wrong for your situation, the right move may be one you have to originate yourself — and Lesson 4 gives you the shape to write your own with.
 
### Family I — Strategies for the person
 
- **Cognitive (am I getting sharper or going dull?)** — Candidate strategies:
  - **The agent-off slice.** A scheduled block of work each week where you reach for the model only after solving by hand. *Fits:* practitioners whose reframe-vs-accept rate is creeping toward accept. *Watchpoint:* if the slice keeps getting bumped for "real work," the slice *was* the real work.
  - **The annual hard-problem benchmark.** A small set of personal problems you re-solve annually with AI off, watching how long they take. *Fits:* anyone who wants a falsifiable Asked reading. *Watchpoint:* if you skip a year, the next year's reading isn't a trend.
  - **The reshape-don't-accept rule.** A per-PR practice of restating the agent's plan in your own words before approving. *Fits:* practitioners whose acceptance rate has crept up under deadline pressure. *Watchpoint:* if your restatement is "looks good, ship it" rather than substantive reframing, the rule has become ritual.
- **Epistemic (do I actually understand what I shipped?)** — Candidate strategies:
  - **The monthly cold walk-through.** Explain a system you built 30–90 days ago from memory, then check against the code. *Fits:* practitioners signing off on agent-built systems at G7. *Watchpoint:* if the memory-vs-truth gap widens consistently, the review gate wasn't real for those builds.
  - **Self-authored reason-for-being.** Write the *why* and *trade-offs* unit of each system's documentation yourself, before the agent fills in the rest. *Fits:* practitioners whose docs are entirely agent-generated. *Watchpoint:* if you can't write the unit without the agent, you don't yet understand what you shipped.
  - **The replacement test.** Periodically rebuild a small system by hand, comparing your reconstruction to the agent-built original. *Fits:* practitioners with the capacity for a deliberate slowdown. *Watchpoint:* if you skip the comparison, the test was nostalgia.
- **Vocational (does this still feel like work I want to do?)** — Candidate strategies:
  - **The protected engagement.** Keep one piece of work in the queue you'd take if AI vanished tomorrow. *Fits:* practitioners feeling the work shift toward orchestration. *Watchpoint:* if even the protected work starts to drain, the dimension is in trouble — and the Asked reading isn't being hidden by the in-the-moment feeling.
  - **The Friday texture note.** A one-line entry each week on how the work felt. *Fits:* anyone willing to make weekly self-report a tracked reading rather than a feeling. *Watchpoint:* if the notes start to repeat — *fine, fine, fine* — you've stopped looking.
  - **The annual would-I-still.** Once a year, write whether you'd want to keep practicing exactly this way for another ten years. *Fits:* every practitioner. *Watchpoint:* if you keep moving the question, you're already answering it.
- **Identity (when the machine builds it, what's still mine?)** — Candidate strategies:
  - **Verb-attention.** Review your last ten bios and bylines for honest verbs — *built / orchestrated / shipped.* *Fits:* anyone whose public language hasn't caught up with their actual practice. *Watchpoint:* if all verbs read the same regardless of the work, the verbs aren't tracking anything.
  - **The solo signature.** Keep a regular slice of work you produce and sign without agent involvement, so the answer to *what does the human add now* is a thing you made, not a thing you said. *Fits:* practitioners whose contribution is increasingly orchestration. *Watchpoint:* if the slice keeps shrinking, the answer is changing without your permission.
  - **The contribution sentence.** Write annually, in your own words, what you actually believe the human adds. *Fits:* every practitioner. *Watchpoint:* if the sentence reads as field consensus rather than your conviction, it isn't yours.
 
### Family II — Strategies for the practice
 
- **Differential (when everyone can do this, what's the value of *me* doing it?)** — Candidate strategies:
  - **The queued-next distinction.** Each quarter, deliberately build or learn something whose value isn't *being early to AI*. *Fits:* practitioners whose pricing is still propped by the early-adopter premium. *Watchpoint:* if the next distinction is always "next quarter," the queue is empty.
  - **Pricing-trend tracking.** Watch comparable-work pricing across quarters; correct when it slides. *Fits:* anyone billing for skills the field is catching up on. *Watchpoint:* if you keep discounting *this client is different,* the trend is the trend.
  - **The conversation diagnostic.** Note whether prospects bring *help me start* or harder *what next* questions. *Fits:* practitioners who can read their pipeline as data. *Watchpoint:* when *help me start* crowds out *what next* for two quarters, the next distinction isn't standing up.
- **Judgment (what happens when the models do the judgment I sell?)** — Candidate strategies:
  - **The locked benchmark set.** A small set of judgment-heavy problems; run your call vs. the frontier model's each major release. *Fits:* practitioners who sell architectural, strategic, or design judgment. *Watchpoint:* the gap that matters most is the one where the model would have been wrong six months ago and isn't anymore.
  - **The three-calls log.** Each week, list three calls you made that the model wouldn't have. *Fits:* practitioners willing to make a weekly Asked reading. *Watchpoint:* if the list gets shorter quarter over quarter, the calls you sell are migrating into the model.
  - **The migrating-judgment audit.** Annually, name the judgment category most at risk and the next distinction it points to. *Fits:* practitioners doing annual planning. *Watchpoint:* if the audit names the same category two years running and nothing changes, the audit was performative.
- **Temporal (does the time I "save" actually come back to me?)** — Candidate strategies:
  - **The quarterly accounting.** Record reclaimed vs. absorbed hours; name what each was spent on. *Fits:* practitioners with calendar discipline. *Watchpoint:* if the absorbed column always exceeds the reclaimed, the dividend is overhead.
  - **The reactive/generative split.** Track your calendar week by week. *Fits:* anyone whose calendar is the truthful source. *Watchpoint:* if the reactive side keeps growing, the speed itself is generating new expectations you're meeting.
  - **The protected-reclaim block.** Book reclaimed hours on the calendar before they get absorbed. *Fits:* practitioners who can defend calendar holds. *Watchpoint:* if the block keeps moving, the practice doesn't yet take reclaimed time seriously.
 
### Family III — Strategies for the ground
 
- **Economic (will what's profitable today still be profitable tomorrow?)** — Candidate strategies:
  - **The classify-and-route discipline.** From Unit 3 Lesson 5 — refuse the frontier-on-every-task default; route by tier. *Fits:* every practitioner running an AI-heavy shop. *Watchpoint:* if frontier-share of output keeps climbing, the routing line has bent quiet — and the cost line will follow.
  - **The unit-economics line.** Track cost-per-unit-of-output as its own line; watch quarter over quarter. *Fits:* practitioners with billable units to measure against. *Watchpoint:* margin compressing under stable pricing is the subsidy ending, said quietly.
  - **The dependency-price audit.** Quarterly check on whether subsidies are still flowing where you rely on them — rate-limit incidence, enterprise-tier introductions, deprecation notices. *Fits:* anyone with provider concentration in their cost line. *Watchpoint:* the early signals of subsidy pulling back are operational, not announced; if you wait for the announcement you've lost a quarter.
- **Ethical (is what I'm doing defensible?)** — Candidate strategies:
  - **The labor-displacement ledger.** Track which roles you stopped paying a human for and what would make you start again. *Fits:* practitioners whose practice has displaced labor in the last 18 months. *Watchpoint:* if no role ever returns to the ledger as a *pay again* candidate, the answer to *what would make me start again* was never honest.
  - **The dependency-line decision.** Name which lab dependencies you'll carry silently and which you won't, with self-hosted / open-source held as a live option rather than a fallback. *Fits:* practitioners who want concentration-of-power addressed operationally, not just rhetorically. *Watchpoint:* if the line keeps moving in the lab's direction, you've ceded the decision.
  - **The disclosure default.** Adopt a standard for how AI's involvement is named in the work you ship. *Fits:* practitioners shipping to clients or audiences who can't easily tell. *Watchpoint:* if the disclosure is buried in fine print, it isn't disclosure.
  - **The smaller-model-first posture.** Reach for the smaller model by default; document deliberately when you don't. *Fits:* practitioners who want frontier-share to be a chosen number rather than an emergent one. *Watchpoint:* if the default keeps slipping in practice, the posture isn't installed.
 
- **How to use the menu.** Pick by the reading you can't ignore. Adapt the strategy to your situation — the *what fits* line is a starting point, not a rule. Expect to retire strategies as your readings change, and to originate new ones that the lesson doesn't name. The catalog is a starting point, not the ceiling. And the lesson that follows the catalog — the dashboard — is what tells you whether the strategies you chose are actually moving the readings.
*The takeaway: strategies plural per dimension, written as patterns to evaluate and adapt. The builder chooses; the playbook doesn't choose for them. Lesson 6 shows what choosing among the menu looks like for one practitioner on one or two of his own readings — not a prescription, a demonstration.*

## Lesson 5 — Your Own Dashboard, Read Over Years
 
*What you get: you'll see that you already built this instrument in Unit 3 — and learn to read it on the long clock instead of the daily one.*
 
- You're not starting from scratch. The board you stood up in Unit 3 has been quietly taking these readings the whole time — how often you reframed versus accepted, whether you read the diff before approving, how much you chose versus rubber-stamped. The daily version kept you honest this week. The same readings, lined up over a year, are the long game.
- The shift is just the zoom level. A bad day is weather; you ignore it. A line that bends the wrong way for two quarters is climate — and climate is the thing worth changing course over.
- Read it against *yourself*, never against a crowd. Your normal isn't anyone else's. If you're wired to chase novelty and originate fast, your healthy numbers look "alarming" on somebody's generic chart and fine against your own baseline. The reading that means something is the drift from *your* established normal — which is exactly why the intake back in Unit 2 captured that baseline in the first place.
- Port the watchdog to the long clock. Unit 1 wired an alarm for your daily state — the coffee-cup-on-the-dash that fires when you're skipping reviews. The career version is slower and quieter: an agent that watches the trend lines and tells you when one has been bending the wrong way long enough to matter, on a day you'd never have looked.
- The limit is the same one as always: the instrument can take the reading; it cannot care about the result. It will watch your sharpness drain forever and feel nothing. There is exactly one party for whom your long-term viability matters, and it isn't the agent. *(Sets up Lesson 6: the move from seeing to heeding is the un-delegable one — and the strategies in Lesson 4 are only worth the menu they're written on if someone is actually willing to pick.)*
*The takeaway: you already own the instrument. Unit 4 just teaches you to read it in years instead of days.*

## Lesson 6 — What the Readings Ask of You
 
*What you get: the un-delegable move — and a demonstration of one practitioner choosing among Lesson 4's strategies on his own readings, so you have a worked example of how a real picking happens.*
 
- The instrument is built to be delegable. The agent takes the automatic readings, files your asked ones, watches the trend, sounds the alarm. Every part of *seeing* can be handed off. The menu of strategies from Lesson 4 is also delegable in a sense — the agent could read the menu to you, score the strategies against your readings, even recommend which one to try first. Every part of *deciding what's possible* can be supported.
- Exactly one part can't be handed off: deciding to actually do it, and then doing it. A trend line bending the wrong way is information, not action. A strategy chosen but never enacted is a list, not a practice. The agent can show you that your origination is sliding, present you with three plausible Cognitive strategies from the Lesson 4 menu, and walk you through which one fits your situation — and then it's done. It will not change your course, because it has no course.
- So the last question of the kit is the one from Unit 1's watchdog, grown up: the alarm is sounding, the menu is laid out — *do I pull over, or tell myself this time is different?* You can read a dashboard for years and pick the right strategy off the right menu and still drive straight off the cliff both of them warned you about.
- Here's the turn: a bad reading isn't a sentence — it's a brief for the next thing worth building. Sharpness draining? That's a new thing to originate — one of the Cognitive strategies from the menu, or one you write yourself because the menu doesn't quite fit. Edge gone commodity? That's the real work, named for you by the data and shaped by the Differential strategies, of finding what's actually yours. The trade you made years ago turning out badly? Now you get to choose the next one with your eyes open — and Lesson 4 hands you a starting point for the choosing.
- **What choosing looks like, on one or two of my own readings.** Three units of building the judgment to do this and then walking off the page without showing it being done would be a missing lesson — so here is what picking from the Lesson 4 menu currently looks like for me, on the one or two dimensions I'm most worried about. **Demonstration, not curriculum.** Written in the same three-line shape the menu uses: *what the reading is showing me, which strategy I chose and why, what would tell me it's working.* The lesson carries **one or two demonstrations** rather than nine, because the work of the lesson is the *heeding distinction*, not the strategies themselves — those are in Lesson 4 for the builder to read across. The two most load-bearing for the lesson's credibility (and the two Clay is currently most worried about): **Vocational** — which Vocational strategy from the Lesson 4 menu Clay is currently running, why it pulls, and what his weekly Asked reading is telling him; and **Ethical** — which Ethical strategies (the labor-displacement ledger, the dependency-line decision) Clay is currently running, what's on those ledgers right now, and what's changed in his practice as a result. Both are first-person, both are at the honest depth Clay actually has on them, both are framed as *one practitioner's current picking* — not as the right picks.
- Which is how the long clock hands you back to the short one. Every honest reading on the career clock becomes a *why* on this-build's clock — and every strategy chosen from the Lesson 4 menu becomes a thing the next build either makes room for or doesn't. *Remaining viable* feeds straight back into *becoming viable* — and the whole loop starts over, one build smarter than last time.
*The takeaway: the instrument can sound every alarm, the menu can lay out every plausible response, and neither will move a single thing in your practice until you pick one and do it. Reading the trend is delegable; choosing from the menu is delegable; doing the thing is the job that was always yours — and here is what doing it looks like on one practitioner's current readings, so you have a demonstration of the un-delegable move.*

## Lesson 7 — A Study You Can Join
 
*What you get: you'll learn that this whole inquiry is one Clay runs on himself, in public — and exactly what it would mean to point your instrument at the shared question too, if you ever want to.*
 
- Here's the part that's easy to miss — and the demonstrations in Lesson 6 would have read differently if we'd said it first: the nine questions aren't a framework Clay invented for *you*, the menu of strategies in Lesson 4 isn't a checklist written for an imagined practitioner, and the one or two demonstrations in Lesson 6 weren't an illustration. They are the menu Clay is choosing from for himself, on his own day-to-day work as a technical founder, measured in public, drift lines and all. *One instrument, two directions:* the same readings that sharpen his own practice are the ones he publishes.
- Which means there are two doors out of this unit, and you can take either, both, or neither.
- **The first door is just thinking.** The point of these nine questions is that you can't un-see them once you've met them — in your own week, in your own bills, in the way you caught yourself describing your work at the last dinner party. You may try one of the strategies from Lesson 4 against a reading of your own, without ever telling anyone. If all this unit ever does is make you *notice* what's under threat, and try one strategy in response, it did its job. (That's the side of the inquiry made of words — essays, field signals, the things being argued out loud.)
- **The second door is measuring — and, if you choose, sharing.** You already built the instrument. You already have the menu of strategies. You already saw a demonstration of choosing among them. You can point the whole thing at the shared question and let your readings join a cohort's, which makes the whole picture richer and less lonely than one person staring at his own charts.
- And the rules of that door are written to protect you, not recruit you. It is **never required** — your mentorship, your playbook, your work change nothing if you skip it. Consent is **granular**: you choose which families and which readings you're willing to share, and the most personal ones — what the work is doing to you, who you stopped paying — are opt-in by the line, not the page. The mentorship is one thing; this inquiry is another; opting out of the second takes nothing from the first.
- Why share at all, then? Because self-report lies in a population the same way it lies in one head — everyone reports they're fine — and the only honest reading of where this whole way of working is taking people is the trailing one, taken across many practitioners over time. You'd be making that reading possible. That's the entire pitch, and it's allowed to be a quiet one.
*The takeaway: it's Clay's self-study first, made public — and an open invitation second. Think because you can't help it; join only if you mean to.*

## Lesson 8 — Where to Start Today, and How the Loop Closes
 
*What you get: one thing to do this week, the whole kit in a line, and the place it all points back to.*
 
- Your first move (do it this week): pick *one* of the nine questions — whichever one you're a little afraid of the answer to — write it down, and find two readings for it.
