# Unit 2 — Configure: Finding Your Situation

*Structural map of the polished Unit 2 lesson content at `curriculum/lessons/unit-2.md`. Working titles preserved. Each lesson teaches one move of the configuration — locating the builder in a recognized situation, confirming via behavior, picking develop-vs-borrow per skill, naming the five universal moves, framing the borrow discipline, and handing the builder to Unit 3 — and ends with a kit moment so the doctrine is both taught and made operable.*

---

**The promise stated before Lesson 1.**
By the end of this unit you'll know the situation you are actually starting from, the trade it asks you to make, the call to develop or borrow per skill, the five moves that travel with every kit, and the first thing to do this week. The kit will be configured against your situation, and the relationship between your aim and your situation will be written down where the agent's first suggestion cannot quietly overwrite it.

**What this unit hands the builder to.** Unit 3: a configured kit waiting to run against real work, with the situation tag deciding which agents foreground at which gates, which gates harden, and how each skill is taught.

**A note on lesson order.** Unit 1 closed with the line *I want to do what I want to do, damn it. You do too. Unit 2 begins there.* This unit opens on that handoff. Lesson 1 lands the builder in recognition — the six situations as scenes — before any framing lessons. The reframe (*every escape is a trade*) is earned inside Lesson 1 as the unit's lead distinction; the check, the develop-vs-borrow call, the five universals, and the borrow discipline follow in the order the builder needs them. An earlier seven-lesson draft (superseded) opened on meta-commentary before recognition; this revision corrects that.

## Lesson 1 — Find Your Situation

*What you get: you stop reading a generic playbook and meet the situation your aim has to move through. You see the trade your fix is actually making. You sign the residual-risk statement that turns the trade from a story into a chosen dependency.*

### The opening hook — aim meets situation

- Open on the explicit handoff at the end of Unit 1: *The last unit closed on the line "I want to do what I want to do, damn it. You do too." I meant it as a handoff. This unit is where the wanting meets the world it has to move through.*
- The hinge sentence the lesson rests on: *your aim runs through the place you are actually standing.* Not where you wish you were standing, not where a different builder is standing. The work that gets you there is shaped by where you are starting from.
- The lesson's promise stated plainly: there are six common spots that builders are stuck in right now; you are probably in one of them, sometimes in more than one. *Read the one you recognize carefully; let the others pass for now.* The kit will confirm behaviorally in Lesson 2; recognition can happen first because the scene is what it is whether or not you fit it.

### The reframe before the six (the relocation lens, in full)

- The instinct everyone arrives with, named so the right reading can land: *you walked in looking for the fix that gets rid of the dependency you are worried about — the contractor, the vendor, the framework, the legacy code, the brittle prototype. Find the move that makes the thing go away. That move does not exist.*
- The reframe: **every escape is a trade.** No move sheds risk; it only changes its shape. Follows straight from the line at the front of Unit 1 — if you bear all the risk and the machine bears none, none of these moves *remove* a dependency; they *relocate* it. *You shed one dependency and pick up a different one. The fix is real; the escape is a story.*
- The teaching reframe: **escaping dependency was never the goal. Choosing which dependency is worth holding is.** The first question of the kit — *Did I choose this, or did it choose me?* — pointed at the dependency rather than the product. *Origination applied to the trade itself.*
- The tone the reframe must land in: not nihilism. **Trading up to a dependency worth holding.** Clear-eyed choosing.
- The mechanical move that makes the trade real: **the ledger.** Two columns — *Shed* and *Adopted*. Most people fill in *Shed* easily; that is what they came to get rid of. Force at least **three entries into the *Adopted* column** and sign one **residual-risk statement** — one sentence naming what you are choosing to hold, why it is acceptable, and what you instrument or do to make it acceptable. *That signature is the whole move. An empty Adopted column does not mean the trade was easy. It means the trade was not decided.*
- Reading instruction: **Read the one you recognize.** The other five are useful as borrowable scaffolding for Lesson 5, not as reading you owe right now.

### The six situations (each as one scene)

- **In-scene rapport beat (each situation).** Between the kit paragraph and the trade paragraph, each scene carries a one-to-two-sentence Clay-experience beat — *I have done this work / made this call / been on both sides / walked into this / lived both shapes / built things that found users before the infrastructure was built to hold them.* The beat is general by design, written so Clay can substantiate with specific stories in revision without rewriting surrounding prose. The placement is deliberate: after the kit names the work, before the trade is booked — the seam where empathy earns the trade-naming that follows. Carries the dual-judgement frame implicitly through *which* trades Clay names having lived.


- **You've got people worth growing.** *(Internal Talent Worth Developing.)*
  - *Recognition scene:* an individual standout or a small team of change-makers already on payroll — the handful who would shift how the company actually builds, if developed. Not bootcamp-shaped, not org-wide training, not hiring outside.
  - *Kit:* apprenticeship-shaped — teaching protocol, inquiry-versus-delegation diagnostic, manager's module on sponsoring without smothering.
  - *Trade — Shed:* dependence on external hiring. *Adopted:* the risk of developing *delegators who atrophy* instead of *inquirers who build real capability*; attrition (the capability walks out the door); ongoing sponsorship load (inquiry over delegation has to be protected, not assumed).
  - *Long-game question:* **Is my bench actually getting stronger, or quietly going dull?** Lights up *Identity · Cognitive.*
- **You're renting what you could own.** *(SaaS Vendor Dependency.)*
  - *Recognition scene:* monthly tool that does something close to your core. Both ends sit with the vendor — they originate its direction, they answer for its uptime, you live downstream.
  - *Kit:* borrow the frame, minimally — enough engineering literacy to own and run what you used to rent, plus a build-versus-buy decision module sharp enough to tell differentiator from commodity.
  - *Trade — Shed:* perpetual rent and the vendor's control of a slice of your operations. *Adopted:* the maintenance burden (the answerable half of *build it yourself*); capability and model risk if the new tool is agent-built (deprecation, drift); the upkeep that can quietly eat the savings, discovered a year later in the ops-staffing line you didn't know to budget.
  - *Long-game question:* **Did the time and money I saved actually show up — or get eaten by the upkeep I took on?** Lights up *Temporal · Economic.*
- **Someone else builds your software.** *(External Dev Dependency.)*
  - *Recognition scene:* an agency or contractor writes your code; you direct it but do not own it. Origination is partial — they translate your vision into product calls you do not see. Answerability is largely external.
  - *Kit:* moves both ends back in-house — literacy to direct the work credibly; engineering faculty developed in internal talent if there's any to receive it; audit and handover discipline that keeps tribal knowledge from walking out with the contract.
  - *Trade — Shed:* the agency margin and the *don't own what you can't change or see* trap. *Adopted:* origination and answerability you must now actually hold (review capacity, directing literacy); a differentiation question that lands harder than founders expect — once the same agents are available to everyone, what distinguishes you from the next founder with the identical stack; the real risk of transition stall and lost tribal knowledge during handover.
  - *Long-game question:* **What makes me different from the next founder with the exact same stack — and does it still pay?** Lights up *Differential · Economic.*
- **You inherited a mess you're scared to touch.** *(Legacy Software Debt.)*
  - *Recognition scene:* a codebase you didn't write, can't fully see, and answer for. **Unusual in the unit:** you hold the answerable end *but cannot hold the frame* — and not because you abdicated. *The frame is missing.* Previous authors never wrote it down or are no longer there.
  - *Kit:* develops the faculty (borrowing isn't enough when the system is yours to maintain). Code comprehension and legacy archaeology run first. **Characterization tests** — the safety net under code whose intent is undocumented — run *before* you change anything. Modernize-versus-replace decision module sits at the end.
  - *Trade — Shed:* the system you couldn't see, couldn't safely touch, and feared. *Adopted:* a modernization in which a model now makes change decisions on your behalf over code you still don't fully frame; the brownfield risk that AI can actually *slow* you on mature code you know well; the slow, unborrowable work of building the situation model before any safe change.
  - *Long-game question:* **How much of the judgment about this code is still mine?** Lights up *Judgment.*
- **Your team hasn't really moved on AI — or it has, and you can't answer for what it ships.** *(AI Development Gap, two shapes.)*
  - *Recognition scene — Shape A (pre-adoption):* developers with Copilot seats, a few Cursor tries, no real evaluation; not operating AI-native; not working meaningfully differently than three years ago; every week a LinkedIn post claims 10x gains you cannot verify; you feel behind and don't know how far.
  - *Recognition scene — Shape B (post-adoption, blind):* AI producing plenty of working code; PRs landing fast; *looks like* the team adopted; nobody fully verifying or explaining what's shipping. Velocity up; standing behind down.
  - *Kit:* develops the faculty in both directions through one destination. **Spec-driven development** runs first — the spec as the executable contract. A *can-you-verify-what-you-shipped* diagnostic sharpened by shape. Pre-adoption: a credible first-move pathway that does not just buy seats. Post-adoption-blind: the *vibe-to-engineering ladder* from prompt-and-pray to structured, gated work.
  - *Trade — Shed:* the drag of staying structurally behind (Shape A) / the bottleneck of producing by hand (Shape B). *Adopted (Shape A):* change-management load; tool-evaluation cost; the risk of crossing into the blind shape if velocity is chased before the answerable end. *Adopted (Shape B):* verification debt — shippable output nobody can fully verify or explain; review-capacity load that does not scale by hiring; automation complacency that keeps the gap invisible; the *self-spec* substitution — the agent writing the spec it then implements, hiding origination inside a producible artifact.
  - *Long-game question (same either way):* **Can I still make that call as the volume keeps climbing?** Lights up *Epistemic · Cognitive.*
- **Your demo has to become a real product.** *(Prototype-to-Production Wall.)*
  - *Recognition scene:* built on Lovable, Base44, Replit, Bubble, a Cursor-vibe-coded stack — *chose those tools specifically because they appeared to remove engineering time and cost from the equation.* Days or weeks, not months. Looks amazing. Traction showed up. Maybe revenue. *The prototype was the easy part. The thing underneath was not built to hold.* Both proud and exposed.
  - *Kit:* develops the faculty on the non-functional surface (production-readiness hardening as the flagship; a security module specifically for AI-generated code beside it); borrows the frame where the founder will rely on agents or specialists.
  - *Trade — Shed:* the freedom of a prototype that never had to hold the answerable half. *Adopted:* accountability in registers the prototype never owed — real user data, security, reliability; an expanded threat surface from AI-generated code that the literature already documents; observability, SLA, and compliance obligations as actual release requirements rather than nice-to-haves.
  - *Long-game question:* **Am I ready to be accountable in ways a prototype never asked of me?** Lights up *Ethical.*

### The takeaway

- **Six spots. Six trades.** The kit is what runs through them. The takeaway carried past the unit: ***each spot drops a different job and makes a different trade — and the trade is the part you will be tracking for the rest of your career.*** That tracking is Unit 4. Seeded here so the kit is honest about the inheritance it leaves you.
- **The bridge to the check (the author's stake stated out loud).** Between the *Six spots / six trades* takeaway and the hand-off to the check, a short bridging beat in Clay's voice names that he has stood in most of the six and watched the rest from close enough to see the trade play out. The beat catalogues the lived ground without becoming a credentials lesson — *I have grown developers and lost some / made the build-versus-buy call wrong and right / signed off on architectures I could not push back on / inherited code whose authors had walked out / shipped things that found users before the infrastructure was built to hold them / stopped writing code in the line-by-line sense* — and closes on the contract for the rest of the kit: ***I can advise where I have stood.*** Placement chosen deliberately: after recognition, before the kit-moment hand-off, so the credibility lands without front-loading meta-commentary ahead of the six scenes.
- **Kit moment.** If one of those scenes felt unmistakably like your own, that is the situation we will configure your kit against. If two or three felt half-true, that is normal — *most lives put you across more than one of the six, and the borrowing discipline two lessons from now is for exactly that.* The next lesson is the kit confirming what you just recognized, from a direction you cannot fake.

## Lesson 2 — The Check

*What you get: a behavioral confirmation of what you just recognized — and the second read most checks skip, which is how the kit tunes its delivery to your wiring.*

- Open on the hook from Lesson 1: *You and I both saw which scene was yours. The kit, which has been running on your behavior since the start of the last unit, can confirm it from another direction. The check is how it does that.*
- Why the check exists in a unit that already let you pick. The mirror callback to Unit 1 Lesson 5: *the broken part is the part doing the checking.* Recognition from a scene is honest in a way self-rating is not — the scene is what it is whether or not you fit it. The kit's behavioral read is honest in a third way that neither recognition nor self-description can manage on its own, *and it is worth letting the kit have the second pass.*
- What the check refuses to ask: how good you are, what you wish were true. What it reads instead: *the last thing you built, the last thing you shipped, the last thing you inherited.* Behavior is the gauge. Self-rating is the mirror. The check uses the gauge.
- Two reads in one pass:
  - **The first read — which end you hold badly.** The producible/answerable split from Unit 1 Lesson 3 ported here as the sorting key. *Which end do you hold badly?* The answer points to one of the six and to its default kit, and *confirms or quietly corrects the one you recognized in the last lesson.*
  - **The second read — your climate.** The agent is consistent across builders; you are not. Unit 1 instrumented your *weather* (day-to-day variance). The check now adds your **climate** — the stable wiring underneath. *An ADHD creative and a methodical sequential thinker have different climates, and the same bad night degrades each of them differently.*
- The methodological line, drawn early and held out loud: **this is functional, not categorical.** No personality letters, learning-style labels, or generational cohorts — the pop-psychology industry would do nothing useful here even if it were perfectly valid, because the job is to *tune delivery*, not *describe the person*. **Instrument the constraint; do not type the person.**
- What comes out — a starting point, not a label: a *situation* (selects the kit), a *persona* tag (first-time founder, repeat founder, career-changer, lead — decides develop-vs-borrow per module), a *wiring* profile (tunes pacing, density, how aggressively the kit pushes the frame onto paper before you prompt). *Re-take the check as you change. You will. The output is meant to move.*
- The composition rule restated as a sentence to hold: ***situation picks the module set; persona sets develop-versus-borrow; wiring tunes the delivery.*** The kit honors it whether you watch it doing so or not.
- **Kit moment.** Open the kit. The check is the next thing on the screen. Three behavioral prompts pulled from the telemetry already running; you confirm what it already mostly sees. Out comes the routing — situation, persona tag, wiring profile. **If the routing names the situation you recognized in the last lesson, you have a clean configure. If it names a different one, sit with the gap — *the gap itself is information*.** You can override the kit's read; the kit logs your override, and you can re-take it any time.

## Lesson 3 — Build It, or Borrow It

*What you get: the single call that saves you the most wasted effort — for each skill, whether to own it or just direct it.*

- The two jobs narrowed from career to per-skill: not for the rest of your work, for *this skill, in front of you, right now.* The sorting question: **do you need to *own* this skill, or just *direct* it well enough to stay in charge?**
- The two honest answers: **develop the faculty** (the kind you can hold both ends with for years) vs. **borrow the frame** (enough understanding to stay answerable for a call you will never personally author). Both legitimate. Most builders need a mix. The lazy move is not picking — building too much by reflex or borrowing too little out of fear.
- Build for real when the skill is yours to live with — when you'll be the one paged at two in the morning about it; when consequences accrue to your name, your team, your customers. *You can direct work in a craft you do not own, but you cannot stand behind work in one — and standing behind it is what we are here for.*
- Borrow just enough when you'll always hand it off — when a specialist, agency, agent, or vendor will do the producing and your job is to direct credibly and review honestly. *Enough frame to stay answerable for a call you will never author. Not more.* Building a full faculty in a skill you'll never personally exercise costs you weeks and ends in atrophy from disuse.
- **Both traps lived, named, owned.** Two Clay-experience anchors that earn the doctrine:
  - **Borrow when you should have built →** *I have signed off on architectures because the contractor's plan was internally consistent and I had no frame to push against it. Later, when the production load made the wrong assumption visible, the cost was mine. The expert had moved on; the dependency had not.*
  - **Build when you should have borrowed →** *I have done this too — built infrastructure I did not need to own, while the actual scarce thing, my judgment on what was worth doing in the first place, sat unused. The work felt productive while it was making me poorer.*
- The persona overlay restated and made concrete: same situation, two builders, two different calls on the same skill, because the persona tag changes the default. A first-time founder insourcing dev work may be building the founder faculty for the first time and needs to develop it; a repeat founder in the same situation already holds it and only needs to borrow the engineering frame. *Same situation, opposite calls. The persona overlay is what makes the same kit land correctly on both builders.*
- The reversibility move worth holding: **borrowing now can turn into building later.** The decision is reversible by intent, not by accident. Start with just enough frame to be answerable; a year later, if the skill turns out to be central, start developing the faculty. The kit does not punish you for changing your mind in the direction of more capability.
- **Forward reference to Unit 3 (the register switch).** The same call also configures the agent's teaching register at every gate of the build. Develop → narrate the *why*, leave deliberate blanks, teach. Borrow → produce, deliver the artifact and the minimum frame to field a question. **Same skill. Two modes. The mode is selected by your persona tag at intake; you never see the switch flip. But it is your call that sets it.**

## Lesson 4 — The Floor

*What you get: the five moves that travel with every kit. Three of them you already met in Unit 1, restated as operating conditions of any configured kit. Two are new disciplines the speed of agent-driven work now requires.*

- The opening hook: *your situation configures the middle of your kit. Some things hold the ends regardless of which situation claimed you.* Five moves total. *Three from Unit 1, restated in the form they take here; two new because the kit you are about to run does something the bare practice of Unit 1 didn't yet require.*

### The three from Unit 1, restated tightly

- ***Did I choose this, or did it choose me?*** In Unit 1 you asked it about a build. Here you ask it about the modules you load, the borrows you make, the situation you claim. *If the modules you pick up are the ones the situation reflex hands you, you have abdicated at the level of the kit while doing all the right things inside it. The first move is to make the kit yours.*
- ***You are the infrastructure.*** Every module, every borrow, every develop-vs-frame call routes through the piece of the system you're most likely to forget to maintain. *The check captured your wiring; the dashboard from Unit 1 reads your weather. None of the moves below works on a degraded instrument. The kit assumes you. Stay you.*
- ***Every fix is a trade.*** *You met your trade by name in Lesson 1.* Naming it here, after the six rather than before them, lands it differently than naming it before would. The discipline is to keep meeting it. Each time you cross between situations — and you will — the move is the same: *book the Shed, force the Adopted, sign the residual-risk statement. Three sentences in a notebook. The whole move.*

### The two that are new

- **Stay able to check work you didn't write.** The AI writes faster than anyone can read; the one thing in the loop that doesn't speed up is your ability to actually review what came back. *I will instrument this in Unit 3, formally, as a gate that cannot be handed off. Here it is the discipline you carry into the kit: do not accept a pace your review cannot honestly keep.* Treat review capacity as a constraint to plan around, not a function of effort.
- **Say what *right* means before you build, not after.** Write the target down where you can compare against it — what would make this the right answer, in plain language, before the agent has a chance to hand you something that *looks finished* and quietly redefines right for you. *A frame written in advance is one you can check the output against. A frame held in your head on a tired afternoon is one the agent reframes for you without either of you noticing.* The Unit 1 move on this — *externalize the frame before you prompt* — was the first-build version. **Here it grows up: every build, every situation, every borrowed module gets a written target.**

### The takeaway

- The five named in one breath: ***Did I choose this. You are the infrastructure. Every fix is a trade. Stay able to check work you didn't write. Say what right means before you build.***
- *The first three hold the ends. The last two protect the speed at which you are now able to work.* **These are the floor. Your situation adds the rest on top.**

## Lesson 5 — Your Kit Isn't a Cage

*What you get: the freedom to borrow — so the playbook fits your real life, not just the box you landed in.*

- The opening admission: **the kit you just got is real. Use it.** But it cannot be the only thing you use, because real life almost never fits one box.
- Three composite builder sketches: the founder bringing work back in-house *who also inherited a mess*; the team closing the AI gap *who also hit the prototype-to-production wall in the same quarter*; the leader growing engineers *who also needs a vendor-economics call to stay ranked on what's worth building yourselves.* *The six situations are not fixed addresses. They are common shapes, and most lives put you across more than one of them.*
- The move: **borrow on purpose.** When your reality calls for a move from another situation's kit, pull it in — **deliberately, not at random.** Three named borrows that earn the rule: the inheritance protocol from the legacy kit if you just absorbed someone else's code, even if the check pointed you at the AI gap; the build-versus-buy module from the SaaS kit if the agency you're insourcing leaves a tool decision behind; the production-readiness hardening from the prototype kit the day a feature crosses from internal to external.
- The two borrows almost everyone needs: **Checking work you didn't write. Saying what *right* means first.** Met as Moves 4 and 5 in Lesson 4; named again here because they're the two universals builders most often drop when their situation doesn't foreground them. *If you take nothing else from outside your default kit, take those two.*
- The discipline that protects coherence: **borrow the module; do not borrow the register.** A develop-the-faculty builder picking up a borrow from a borrow-the-frame kit still gets taught in develop-the-faculty mode — the agent narrates the *why*, leaves the blanks, pushes you toward the owned capability. The other way around, the same. *Otherwise the borrows quietly hollow you out by teaching you frames where you owed faculties. The check told us who you are. That does not change when you reach across kits.*
- The lesson's reason for existing: **your situation is not a permanent residence.** The builder bringing software back in-house will, in eighteen months, be the builder growing engineers. The builder hardening her prototype will, six months after launch, be the builder holding verification debt. *The builder who took the check today will not be the same builder a year from now. The kit is built to travel. Borrowing is how it travels with you.*

## Lesson 6 — Where to Start Today, and How the Rest Helps You

*What you get: one thing to do this week, plus a map of everything ahead — and the move that aligns the configured kit with the aim that brought you here.*

- The threshold statement: *we have done all the structural work this unit is going to do.* Before you close it, one thing to do this week and a short map of how everything else helps you.
- **Your first move, one sentence and one piece of work — do not pad it: take the check, name your situation, run the first exercise in your kit.** Three concrete branches that show the exercise is small on purpose:
  - SaaS vendor dependency → build-versus-buy on one line item you've been wondering about.
  - Legacy → write down the *three things you do not understand* about the system you inherited; pick the one a characterization test would protect first.
  - AI gap → write the spec for the next ticket *before* the agent does — and check whether your spec and the agent's would have differed.
  - *Everything else in your kit is a deeper version of it.*
- **The move ahead of the exercise (the aim restored to its proper place):** Lesson 1 opened on the relationship between your aim and your situation; here it cashes into the move. **Write the aim down.** In the kit's origination surface, where the agent's first suggestion cannot quietly overwrite it. *The carrot from the end of the last unit might fit cleanly inside the situation you just claimed; it might not.* Two builder sketches that earn the move: the builder who came in to insource an agency may have meant to free a budget for a project that matters to her, *and the agency move only earns its weight if she actually points the savings at that project;* the builder who came in to harden a prototype may have meant to keep a thing she loves alive, *and the hardening only earns its weight if it is in service of the thing she loves rather than the thing investors are now expecting.* **Writing it down will not change the modules. It will change whether the modules do their job.**
- **The whole unit in one line, the one to keep when the lessons blur:** *there is no single right path — there is the one built for where you actually are, and the freedom to borrow the rest.*
- The forward map, in **kit moments** rather than topics:
  - **Unit 3 — Use.** Your kit becomes the *setup sheet* for how you actually run a build with a crew of agents. The ADLC pipeline is the same for every builder; the kit you just got configures which agents foreground at which gates, which gates harden for your situation, and how each skill is taught. *You'll recognize the modules from this unit, but as moves in a working build rather than entries in a kit.* Unit 1 gave you the stance. Unit 2 located you. Unit 3 puts the stance and the location into a day of work.
  - **Unit 4 — Watch.** Every trade you just made becomes a question you carry across a career. The long-game questions you noticed inside the six situations are not lesson ornaments — they're the readings Unit 4 takes off the work over years. *The trades that look like one-time calls are the things you will be watching trend.*
- **The two clocks return, because every unit ends here for a reason.** *Becoming viable* is this situation, this kit, this week's first exercise. *Remaining viable* is the trade you just signed, watched across every situation you'll move through. **One stance. Two clocks. The same instrument. Now you know where it is pointed.**
- **Kit moment — the handoff.** *Install. Configure. Use. Watch.* You have done two of the four. The kit knows you. **Unit 3 begins where it starts running against work that matters.**
