# Unit 1 — Install: Knowing Who Does What

*Structural map of the polished Unit 1 lesson content at `curriculum/lessons/unit-1.md`. Working titles preserved. Each lesson teaches a fundamental — the opportunity, AI vs OI, the two jobs, condition, observability, upkeep — and hands the builder to a kit moment so the doctrine is taught **and** made operable.*

---

**The promise stated before Lesson 1.**
By the end of this unit, you will know what the AI is for, what *you* are for, and why the line between them is the most valuable thing you own right now. The kit will be running on your machine. You will have taken your first honest readings against it. And you will see — in your own data — what the rest of the kit is teaching you to do.

**What this unit hands the builder to.** Unit 2: a running, instrumented, honest kit waiting for your situation.

## Lesson 1 — What's at Stake

*What you get: you see the opportunity clearly, you understand why the risk is yours alone, and you install the kit before the concepts settle.*

- Scene (first person): *I had reasons for building a life around this kind of work — well before AI.* Name the five values one by one — **a canvas** (anything imaginable, made), **a discipline that travels** (judgement as founder and engineer, rewired by years of breaking and rejoining hard problems), **a life you can shape** (when and where you work), **a living** (lucrative in a way that mattered), **a world** (rooms with people you would never have met; lifelong friendships forged on shared problems), **a way to give some of it back** (teaching, which is also its own test — *with AI, you find out fast whether what you think you know actually holds*).
- The hinge: *every one of those is in flux right now. Each can be accelerated by the right relationship with AI — or annihilated by it. Which way it goes is on you.*
- What just changed, said plainly: you can hand a goal to a machine and get working software back — not a snippet, not a scaffold, but the kind a small team used to build in a quarter. The capability is real and is getting better, not worse.
- Why that's thrilling: one person can now do what used to take a whole team.
- The catch in one sentence, italicized so the builder cannot skim it: ***you bear all the risk; the machine bears none.*** The agent loses nothing if the product fails. You lose time, money, reputation, nights — and, quietly over enough builds, the life you might miss before AI entered it.
- Why mistakes cost more now: building got fast and cheap, so the wrong thing arrives faster than the right one ever did. The cliché *experiment more often with less risk* deserves careful examination — sometimes failing once is enough to put a person in the poor-house.
- What this kit promises, in its load-bearing form: **the faculties that win you the opportunity are the same ones you have to keep in form to hold onto the opportunity.** *Becoming viable* is the short clock (this build, in front of you). *Remaining viable* is the long clock (every build after). They are not two sequenced activities — they are one stance held at two horizons.
- **Kit moment.** Stand the kit up now. It is a source tree on your machine; the agents configure themselves against the coding harness you already use (Claude Code, Codex, both); a local telemetry surface starts listening. The kit will **turn the work you care about into a telemetry surface** that listens and honestly reports on your every interaction with AI. It is not a personality test and not a wellness check. By the time the builder finishes this lesson, an instrument is running that knows nothing about them yet — and they have been told that something vital will be revealed as the unit proceeds. That is the correct starting condition.

## Lesson 2 — Rethinking Intelligence

*What you get: you stop competing with the AI and start directing it — because you finally know what each of you is actually for.*

- Open with the temptation worth abandoning: positioning yourself against the machine by listing what it cannot yet do. The list keeps getting shorter; the line worth drawing is **not** between what each side can do but between **what each is meant to do**.

### Artificial Intelligence — the machine

- What it's great at: breaks big goals into steps, weighs options, writes the code, decides when it's done. No tiredness, no bad morning, no distraction. Same game at 3 a.m. as at 10. Given working infrastructure, it is, for our purposes, a constant.
- Three capabilities to name and hold for later: **Think, Decide, Create.** *Hold on to that. We'll revisit it.* (Forward reference to the Think/Decide/Create framing in Lesson 3.)
- What it can't do, ever: **care.** It doesn't want your product to win and isn't hurt when it loses. It cannot suffer the consequences the way you can.
- The structural limit: it decides *how*, never *why* to *begin* the work. AI is goal-oriented by design — every goal it pursues came from someone else. Even *generate goals* is itself an origination, and the origination is human. **The agent should always be downstream of a *why* that it could not author itself.**

### Organic Intelligence — you

- *Organic* is not a flattering metaphor. It begins with the fact that you are alive — and immediately suggests something perishable.
- What's only yours: finite time, choices that touch real people, answerability in every register that matters — paying customer, team depending on you, budget, law, the bank you owe, family. There is a party for whom the work matters, and that party is you. Humans suffer the consequences. The machine cannot be held to account.
- *Self-actuation*, stripped of marketing, reduces to: someone has to point the work at something, and only the someone with something at stake is in the right position to care which direction that is.
- **The catch 22 acknowledged in the kit.** You have weather the machine doesn't. Bad sleep narrows working memory. An argument from this morning bleeds into important afternoon decisions. Three weeks deep into money you don't have, every call starts looking short-term. None of this shows up as an error code — it shows up as worse judgment that often feels exactly like normal judgment. *That is something that had to be learned the hard way, and the unit will return to it.*

*Takeaway: the geometry is not symmetrical. The smart move was never to compete with the machine. It was to give each side the half it was built for — a tireless, uncaring machine to the production, and a finite, answerable human to the deciding what should be produced. The trouble is that doing so requires a new division of labor, and the preservation of judgement against changing weather.*

- **Kit moment.** Open the source tree before turning the page. It isn't arranged by feature. It's arranged around the distinction this lesson just made — surfaces where the machine works, surfaces where the human works, gates at the boundary. The geometry isn't a metaphor; it's a directory structure the builder can read.

## Lesson 3 — Establishing Division of Labor

*What you get: your worth won't vanish with the next model release, because these two jobs never transfer.*

- Refuse the losing move once more: standing on a shrinking platform of tasks the agent can't yet do is *standing on a shrinking platform, in a cold and stormy sea — not a strategy, suicide.*
- The line that does not move with the next model release, said as the structural claim of the lesson: **You start the work. You answer for the work. The agent takes everything in between.** This is liberating and expands creative horizons when the line is held.

### Job 1, the front — Origination

- Picking the right thing to build to begin with. AI can help figure that out, but again, cannot care if it is or isn't the right thing.
- The operational question: *Did I choose this — or did it choose me?*
- The failure mode is not that the agent *seizes* origination — it cannot. The failure is that the human quietly **abdicates** it, responding only to what the tool, the feed, or the agent's next suggestion happens to surface, until choosing what to aim at is a muscle no longer used. **Origination atrophies from disuse, not from AI's malicious seizure.** Harder to defend against because nobody takes it from you.

### Job 2, the back — Answerability

- Standing behind the result when it ships and something breaks. You answer in registers the machine answers in none of — economic, legal, ethical, environmental, the customer at 2 a.m. when the cutover drops orders.
- A model can produce a decision; it cannot be the party held to its outcome. That party is always a living thing.
- **The parallel failure mode that has to be named here** (and is the most expensive lesson in the unit): *Production parallelizes; answerability does not.* The temptation under AI is to multiply yourself without limits — *whatever I ran before, I could now run ten of in parallel*. The first half is true. The second isn't. The number of streams a human can stand behind is bounded by the number they can hold in mind, read closely, override when wrong, and own when shipped. **In practice, one to three things in parallel is where the quality, the schedule, and the budget actually hold.** Past that, you are still originating in name but have stopped originating in fact, and the work carries your signature on choices you didn't have the mind to make.
- **Two failure modes, one abdication.** Disuse withers the muscle because nothing asks for it. Over-distribution stretches the muscle across more concurrent works than one mind can hold answerable. **AI does not raise this ceiling. It reveals one that was always there and makes it easy to walk past.**

### The two ends split each working moment, three places at once

- **Think** — the agent explores; you own the framing. Caching example: the agent proposes a cache with a clean invalidation argument. The producible half is the analysis and the code. The answerable half is whether latency is the real problem — or whether an over-fetching query is the actual cause and a cache would hide a data-model flaw you'll pay interest on for years. *The clean plan is the thing you have to interrupt to ask whether the problem was the one you named.*
- **Decide** — the agent recommends; you live with it. REST-to-GraphQL example: defensible migration plan; the cost no one wrote on the slide is the part that's yours — to the team at 2 a.m., the customers if the cutover drops orders, the budget that funds the rewrite. *A plausible recommendation is not a held decision until someone holds it.*
- **Create** — the agent generates; you judge whether it is *right*, not just whether it *works*. Auth-module example: it compiles and passes its tests. Working is not the same as right. The answerable half is whether this is the auth your product *should* have — threat model, what users believe about how their data is handled, whether the team can own and extend it. ***Generation is not authorship.***

### Steering — the in-flight motion that connects the two ends

- Naming the two ends doesn't finish the work. What does the human do *while the middle runs*?
- Steering is not a third job. It is what holding the two jobs looks like while the work is running — reading the agent's reasoning as it produces, redirecting when the trajectory drifts off the *why* set at the front, interrupting when the path turns into one you cannot stand behind at the back.
- The team analogy that earns the move: you can hand a human team of subordinates a job to be done, and you will still intervene if you see it going sideways. Going sideways is less likely when the team is aware not just of the task but of the DNA of the company, the skills required, and the unique standards they're held to.
- **Kit moment.** In the running kit, look at where each job lives — the origination surface where work is named and scoped, the answerability gates that fire before anything ships, the agent column between them with producing under way. Press the interrupt key on one of the running agents. The work stops. The partial trajectory captures. The builder is now standing in the middle of the agent's column with the option to redirect. That move is **the steering primitive.** Every more elaborate discipline this playbook teaches about steering is built on it.

## Lesson 4 — Acknowledging Human Limitation

*What you get: you learn to spot when you're the problem — the quiet kind that sinks good work — before it costs you.*

- The opening admission: *there are days I should not be the one making the call, and the worst thing about those days is that I am the one who knows it least. The mind that would tell me to step away is the mind that just lost the ability to tell.*
- The geometry of Lesson 3 was correct but does not save you. What sits between the two ends and the human supposed to hold them is **human condition** — not a personal aside to the engineering, but an amplified input into it.
- Why your bad days matter more under AI — the **dilution → concentration** shift:
  - *Before:* cognition spread across hundreds of small acts of production a day. A bad day degraded all of it a little, most of it was self-correcting, and the system had a hundred chances to catch a slip. Condition was **diluted** across high-volume, low-stakes acts.
  - *After:* the agent took the volume of low-stakes acts. What's left to you is exactly the two ends — *judgment-dense, low-volume*. You make fewer moves, each carries far more weight. Condition is now **concentrated** into the handful of calls that decide whether the whole effort was worth making.
- The scary part: failure used to be loud (broken code). Now it's quiet (a bad call that looks like a fine one). **A degraded you no longer ships visibly worse code — the agent handles the code. A degraded you originates the wrong problem, accepts a recommendation you should have overridden, and skims the diff you were supposed to be answerable for.** The failure moved from the visible to the invisible. Bad judgment does not announce itself.
- Worse — **the drunk-driver problem applied to cognition**. The tired mind reports it is thinking clearly. The frightened mind reports it is being prudent. *The impairment and your read on the impairment fail together*, and the one tool you cannot trust at the moment you would reach for it is the in-the-moment self-assessment.
- The fix in one idea — the engineering analogy that turns a soft topic into infrastructure: *you would not run the agent on a server pegged at 100% memory and trust the output; you would fix the infrastructure first, because the output is only as good as what it runs on.* **You are the infrastructure** for the two halves that cannot be handed off. Your condition is not a personal aside to the engineering. It is an engineering input, upstream of every origination and every override.
- The five dials that move your condition, each tied to a specific way the work goes wrong:
  - **Physical** — sleep is not about stamina; sleep is about *whether the override fires*. A tired you doesn't write worse code anymore — the agent writes the code. A tired you accepts the plausible default, and the agent's entire output is plausible defaults. Rested, you catch that the clean-looking migration silently dropped a uniqueness constraint. Exhausted, you see green tests and ship it.
  - **Cognitive** — focus and working memory are what let you hold a frame, and the front job owns the frame. Scattered, you get pulled into whatever the agent surfaced and optimize inside it, never checking the frame at all. The clean-looking thing the agent built is exactly what you built — to *its* specification, which you forgot was supposed to be yours.
  - **Emotional** — regulation is what lets you sit in the unfinished thing on purpose. Dysregulated, you rubber-stamp because you're sick of the problem, or reject a good recommendation because it dented your ego. The answerable half requires tolerating discomfort on purpose.
  - **Spiritual** — your live connection to what is worth doing. The home of origination. Depleted here, you don't make worse choices; you stop choosing. You drift into whatever the feed, the market, or the agent's next suggestion puts in front of you. **Origination atrophies from disuse, and the disuse is what an unreaching self looks like from outside.**
  - **Financial** — scarcity makes you care; desperation makes you stupid. A frightened person rationalizes the engagement he should not take and the cutover he should not ship — and does it with a clean-feeling conscience, *because fear is good at sounding like prudence.*
- The dimensions don't stay in their lanes: bad sleep wrecks regulation; money fear wrecks sleep. The taxonomy isn't the point. The point is that every one of them lands on the same two ends — the only ones you can really hold anymore that have value.
- **Kit moment.** Open the kit's condition surface. It does **not** ask how you feel. It reads weather from your behavior — the same telemetry that has been running since Lesson 1 — because the gauge inside is the part that lies first. A dashboard that asks how you feel and logs the flattering answer is a mirror with a database behind it. The kit knows the difference. The next lesson is about how it does what self-report cannot.

## Lesson 5 — Instrumenting the Human You

*What you get: an honest read on your own state — using the same trick you already use on every system but yourself.*

- The opening admission: *I will instrument anything — latency, error rates, queue depth, cost-per-request, deploy frequency, vendor uptime, model spend by route. The single system I run from the inside, carrying the only two ends a machine cannot take, is the one I have left dark.*
- Why self-observability is the last thing instrumented — the **structural** reason, not the personal one: a business emits its signals to parties who do not share its impairment; you, reading your own gauge, **do** share it. Observer and observed collapse into one instrument, and it is the impaired one. *Doctors do not diagnose themselves. Pilots fly with two of them in the cockpit.* The instruments we have learned to trust are the ones built to acknowledge that the operator cannot be the gauge. **An honest question is only ever as honest as the narrator answering it, and the narrator lies under load.**
- The reframe: the question isn't whether to ask honest questions about yourself. It is **how to ask one whose answer you cannot fake.**
- Three properties separate an instrument from a mirror:
  - **The answer lives outside your head.** *Am I rested?* routes straight through the broken gauge. *What do the last two weeks of accepts actually say?* does not. The reading must be something you could show another person — a log, a count, a record — not a verdict you issue about yourself.
  - **You ask it off the decision.** On a schedule, away from the work, the way a health check runs whether or not anything is wrong. Never in the moment before the call — the moment before the call is when you most want a flattering answer and least notice you are getting one. *Form is established before it is needed, since the moment of need is the moment of inability to assess.*
  - **It targets your specific failure, not the generic dimension.** *Do I sleep enough?* is the wellness-list version. *What's the exact story I tell myself on the night I decide to skip it — and is it ever true?* is yours and no one else's.
- The five dimensions, run through that filter, become instruments — the dishonest vs. honest pairs:
  - **Physical** — Dishonest: *Am I rested?* Honest: *Over the last stretch, the times I accepted agent output without really reading it — what state was I in?* (Not measuring stamina. Measuring whether the override still fires.)
  - **Cognitive** — Dishonest: *Am I focused?* Honest: *How often this week did the agent's first suggestion quietly become my direction — did I choose the frame, or get handed it?*
  - **Emotional** — Dishonest: *Am I calm?* Honest: *My recent accepts and rejects — were they about the work's merits, or about how sick of it I'd gotten?*
  - **Spiritual** — Dishonest: *Do I feel motivated?* Honest: *This period, did I start anything — or only respond to what showed up?*
  - **Financial** — set the tripwire in advance, while you can still think straight. Name the number below which your horizon collapses; draw the line above it before fear is doing the math. *Would I have said yes to this a year ago, at a different balance?*
- *None of these is exhaustive. Each is the shape of a probe. You fail inside these dimensions in a way that is yours and not mine, and the probe that protects your specific failure is the one worth building.*
- **Kit moment — the first reading.** Pick a small piece of real work — small enough that you are not blocked, large enough that you will actually use the agent. While you work, the kit reads four signals: ***accept-without-read***, ***origination ratio***, ***reframe-vs-accept***, ***override-fired***. Each is taken from behavior, not opinion. The only path to a good number is doing the work the number measures. The kit deliberately does **not** ask how focused you felt or how your morning went — those are mirrors with databases behind them, and the kit will not deliver one in good conscience. At the end of the small piece of work, look at the first reading. It is small, possibly unflattering, entirely yours, and the first signal about yourself you have ever generated *that you cannot have flattered into existence — and you generated it inside the work itself.* **You have not been told you are observable. You have observed yourself.**

## Lesson 6 — Maintaining the System

*What you get: you keep your edge over a whole career, you build an alarm that goes off even on the days you'd rather ignore it, and you understand exactly what you can do to make the alarm lie.*

- The opening admission: *a dashboard with no builder is not a dashboard.* The previous lesson handed you a monitor and quietly assumed you would read it. The faculty that degrades is also the faculty that would remember to check whether it is degrading. *The drowsy driver does not pull over to assess his drowsiness, because deciding to assess it is the first thing the drowsiness takes.*
- Maintenance is not enough. Neither is the dashboard. What you need is the practice of keeping the faculty in form **and** the page you cannot be relied on to send yourself **and** the honest statement of what you can do to make all of it lie. Not three habits — one discipline. Three sub-units follow.

### The faculty you stopped maintaining

- The mirror, exact: you can describe the agent's memory in detail — the context window (how much it holds before early tokens slide out of reach) and the persistent store it retrieves from across sessions. You tune both. You prune the context, you decide what goes in the store. You would never run the agent on a context stuffed with junk and trust the output. *You run yourself that way most days and trust the output anyway.*
- **Your working memory is the context window. Your long-term memory is the store.** Holding the frame **is** working memory under load. Recognizing the pattern **is** retrieval from a store you bothered to build. **Memory is not a faculty beside the judgment. It is what the judgment is made of.**
- *Is offloading making me duller?* Acknowledge the live worry. A faculty never called degrades. But the answer is not to hoard recall you don't need. **The answer is to be deliberate about which faculty you are spending. Hand the trivia to the machine. Keep in use the muscle that matters.**
- No regimen — the floor is known (sleep consolidates memory, the obvious poisons corrode it). What you can offer is the **shape of upkeep**, the same three-property test as the probes, each move earning its place only if it protects one of the two ends:
  - **Externalize the frame before you prompt** — the *why*, written down where working memory doesn't have to hold it and the agent's first suggestion can't quietly overwrite it. A frame on paper is one you can check the output against.
  - **Make the store double as the dashboard** — one artifact, not two. The thing that lets you read your condition over time is the same thing that lets you retrieve the pattern, and neither one routes through the gauge that lies.
  - **Keep the muscle you actually need in use** — reach for the prior decision before you ask the agent to surface it. Hold the architecture in your head long enough to know whether the agent's plan fits before you read it.
  - **Protect the dense calls** — you make far fewer moves now and each carries more. Single-thread the origination and the override the way you'd never bother to single-thread the middle.
- *None of those is the answer. Each is the shape of one. You fail inside memory in a way that is yours, and the practice that protects your specific failure is the one worth building.*

### The watchdog

- Maintenance gets you a faculty in better form. It does not get you the page. On the week that matters, you drift past the line without noticing — *because noticing is the function that goes first*, and a scheduled check you have to initiate depends on the one faculty you cannot depend on.
- The car analogy that earns the move: your car doesn't ask whether you're tired. It watches how you steer — the small corrections, the drift, the hours — and when the pattern crosses a line it puts a coffee cup on the dash and suggests you stop. *The reading comes from your behavior, not your say-so. It arrives whether or not you thought to wonder. It is a nudge, not a seizure.* Engineers who understood that a human's read on his own fatigue is the one reading you cannot trust **built the trust out of the loop.**
- Build the same thing for the only system that never had one. The kit already has the telemetry — every accept, every reframe that did not happen, every diff that went through unread. What was missing was the wire from the signal to the alert. **Wire it.** Set the tripwires when you can still think straight — a run of accepts without an override, an origination ratio falling for two weeks, a streak of diffs merged faster than anyone could have read them — and let the agent page you when they trip. *"You have taken the last nine suggestions without changing one — want to actually look at this one?"* The drowsy-driver alert, ported to the only system that never had one.
- The objection-as-the-point: *You are asking the thing you are tempted to over-trust to tell you that you are over-trusting it?* Yes. And notice what that requires. **A monitor built to keep you engaged would be worse than none** — it would be the corporate dashboard counting throughput, the optimization reflex Lesson 4 warned against, wearing a safety vest. The car does not sell you more driving. It tells you to stop, against its own engagement, because the people who built it were answerable for whether you arrived. Build the watchdog the same way: *pointed at judgment, not output; designed to interrupt the productive trance, not extend it.* The signal it reads has to be one you cannot fake — already known how to build, because the previous lesson was about nothing else.
- **The page is delegable. The heeding is not.** The agent can sound the alarm forever at a driver who keeps both hands on the wheel and drives into the dark anyway. The watchdog restores the page your condition could not page itself. It does not restore the part that was always yours: being the one who, paged, actually stops.

### The honest defeat

- The posture stated up front, not buried in a settings panel: **you can defeat the kit.** Told plainly because the operator most motivated to fudge the reading is the one reading it, and that is true of any instrument turned on the self. A kit that could not be defeated would be a different thing — something policing you in your own house. *That is not what's on offer. It would also not work; the kind of mind that wants to win an argument with its own dashboard will always find a way.*
- What is on offer is a kit whose **defeats are visible.**
  - **Defaults are honest.** Telemetry is on. The gameable settings are off.
  - **Defeats are visible.** Disabling a signal, lowering a tripwire, deleting a record — each is itself a logged event in a record you cannot quietly purge.
  - **The kit does not lock you out.** No DRM, no remote kill, no telemetry shipped off your machine. *You own the data, the keys, and the consequences.*
- **A defeated kit is still a workflow.** It may organize your agents and skills in a way you like. It may make your day cleaner. It is no longer the thing this playbook is for, *and the value of being wrong — the value the rest of the kit turns on — is what a quietly defeated kit removes first, because it removes the trace.*
- The single question the kit cannot decide for you, waiting at the moment the page arrives, is the one the lesson closes on: **When the coffee cup lights up, do I pull over, or do I explain to myself why this time is different?** *I do not know your answer. I am still wrestling with mine.*

- **Kit moment.** The tripwires are set; the watchdog is wired; the defeat posture is named, not hidden. The page the condition unit said you lacked is now restored. The heeding has not been — it was always yours.

## Lesson 7 — Beginning the Practice

*What you get: one thing to do on your very next build, plus a map of everything ahead — and the question the kit cannot ask for you.*

- The lesson's threshold statement: *this is where the kit stops introducing itself and starts being useful.* The kit is installed, the doctrine held, the first reading in. The builder has a small record that did not exist before — not a snapshot of how you felt, but a trace of what you did, taken from inside the work itself.
- The deliberately-small carry-over. Refuse the temptation of a twelve-step regimen — the kit already does most of the counting. What it cannot do is the one move that goes at the front: **Write the *why* down first** in the kit's origination surface, before you prompt. The agent's first suggestion cannot quietly overwrite a frame you have already externalized. *That is the one move to make on the very next build, ahead of any other discipline this kit teaches. The kit counts the rest.*
- The whole kit in one line, returned to as earned: **the skills that win you the opportunity are the same ones you keep sharp to hold on to it.** The lesson on condition and the lesson on the long clock are not different lessons — they are the same lesson at two different time horizons. *Becoming viable and remaining viable are not two activities in sequence. They are one stance, held at two clocks.*
- What's coming, named in **kit moments**, not topics:
  - **Unit 2 — Configure.** Short, and a *routing* rather than a *grading*. The two-ends diagnostic reads — from behavior the kit has already begun to record — which end you hold badly. The operating profile reads your wiring and tunes how the kit will deliver. The audience routing names the situation you recognize as your own, and produces, as output, the configured modules, agents, skills, ADLC gates, and dashboard signals your kit will emphasize from then on. *Same kit. Different mix of founder and engineering judgement. Different default on develop-versus-borrow.*
  - **Unit 3 — Use.** The configured kit becomes the day-to-day harness against real work. The Agent Development Life Cycle fires across discovery, design, implementation, tests, security, infrastructure, deployment, documentation, and observability — staffed by agents, gated by the human, with the steering you began in Lesson 3 happening continuously through the middle.
  - **Unit 4 — Watch.** The same record the kit has been keeping becomes the longitudinal dashboard for the nine dimensions of long-term viability, read against your own baseline, never against a population norm. *Same instrument, longer clock.*
- **Install. Configure. Use. Watch.**

### The question the kit cannot ask for you

- The closing turn, doctrinally load-bearing and previously underweight in the outline: there is one thing the kit cannot ask for you, the configuration will not write for, and the practice will not reveal. **What aim are you pointing all of this at to begin with? Why did it matter to you to? That part has always been yours, and AI has not changed it. What do you want to do?**
- The carrot frame: if you can now move through projects faster than you ever could, ask what it is you want to move *toward* post haste. To what end the speed? What carrot are you putting in front of yourself to stay motivated? *The next paid build that buys you out of debt? Something else?*
- The first-person example (worth keeping because it grounds the abstraction): the things you'd given up trying to integrate — daily language learning, reading, exercise; the things that develop the *me* I want to be while I am still here on this good Earth — get prioritized out and become a daily disappointment, *until you make them the carrot instead.* The way you get the time for them is to move quickly and successfully through the projects that pay for the life you've chosen — a life that often feels like chose me. It comes back to one question: *Where do I want to be, and how is the work I am originating for AI going to get me there as fast as I can responsibly answer for?*
- **The four questions underneath the carrot, that AI has not changed:**
  1. Where do I want to go?
  2. Do I have a plan?
  3. Am I willing to keep putting my next best foot forward to get there, even when that foot hurts like hell?
  4. Will I adjust the plan when the pain turns out to be the consequence of abdication of will?
- *Setting the destination, holding the plan, being willing to change it when the pain reveals you've dropped one of the two ends — none of your humanity gets handed to the agent. The agent pursues a goal because you assign it. What compels you comes from somewhere more mysterious. The kit can keep you honest about whether you are pursuing what you said you wanted in a healthy way. It cannot pursue it for you.* Remember: ***It doesn't care. It risks nothing.***
- **The closing line, earned and felt:** *I want to do what I want to do, damn it. You do too. Unit 2 begins there.*

- **Kit moment — the handoff.** Install is done. The kit is honest, instrumented, and waiting. Unit 2 is about handing it your situation.
