## Lesson 1 — This Kit Doesn't Have One Builder

Everything in the last unit was the same for you and the person next to you. Two jobs at the ends of the work. One stake asymmetry, written across every move: **you bear all the risk; the machine bears none.** A single instrument — you — and the discipline of treating yourself like one. The floor was deliberately the same, because the floor is the same.

This is where it stops being the same.

The two jobs do not change. Origination at the front and answerability at the back will be your work for the rest of your career, whether you write code or buy it. But *which job you tend to drop* is not the same from builder to builder, and so the fix is not the same either. A founder who has never originated a product from market signal drops one end. A founder who originates beautifully but cannot read the code shipping under her name drops the other. A lead growing engineers drops it in a third place. A team standing over a prototype that has to become a product drops it in a fourth. Same two jobs. Different end dropped. Different work to do.

A static book with one builder teaches one move. We are going to teach one *system* of moves with the right configuration for the builder you actually are. The pieces are interchangeable. You will be handed a default kit built for your situation, and you will be told — loudly, more than once — that you are free to borrow from the other kits when your real life calls for it. Most lives do.

What it will not look like, from the inside, is a branching decision tree. You are going to walk a single lesson sequence the way you always do. The configuration happens behind the scenes. Your *situation* picks the module set. Your *persona* — the kind of builder you are inside that situation — sets whether each module develops a faculty in you or just hands you enough frame to stay answerable. Your *wiring* — the way your particular instrument is built — tunes how each module is taught. The same kit, fit to you, without making you build the fitting.

Six common spots. Each with a kit. None of them a cage. That is the offer.

## Lesson 2 — The Check That Points You to Your Spot

Before any kit lands, we have a problem the last unit already taught us how to solve.

You cannot grade yourself. We agreed on this in Lesson 5 of the last unit, and it does not get less true because the question changed. **The broken part is the part doing the checking.** If we ask you to pick your situation by reading your own self-description — *am I weak at origination? am I overestimating my review capacity?* — we have handed the choice to the part of you most motivated to say the flattering thing. The same flatterer that would have lied to a wellness question will lie to a situation question. We are not going to ask.

So the check does not ask how good you are. It does not ask what you wish were true. It reads what you actually did. The last thing you built, beginning to end — what did you originate, what did you accept, what did you defer? The last thing you shipped — could you stand behind it on a stage? The last thing you inherited — did you understand it, or did you decide to be afraid of it? Behavior is the gauge. Self-rating is the mirror. We are using the gauge.

That read does two things in one pass.

The first is the one you already know it has to do. It picks the end you tend to hold badly. The producible/answerable split, ported from Unit 1 Lesson 3 and turned into a sorting key, asks which end of the work is most likely to fall to you and least likely to be there when it does. *Which end do you hold badly?* The answer routes you to one of the six situations and to its default kit. This is the load-bearing read.

The second is the read most checks skip, and it changes how every module that follows is delivered to you. The agent is consistent across builders; you are not. Unit 1 instrumented your *weather* — the day-to-day variance that makes a normal afternoon a worse afternoon. The check now adds your *climate* — the stable wiring underneath the weather. An ADHD creative and a methodical sequential thinker have different climates, and the same bad night degrades each differently. The same module, taught the same way, lands differently on each of them. So the same module will *not* be taught the same way to each of them. The climate read is what does that work.

The line that has to be drawn here, drawn early, and drawn out loud is this: this is **functional, not categorical**. We are not typing you. We are not handing you a personality letter or a learning-style label. The whole pop-psychology industry of personality letters is, on the honest evidence, mostly noise — and even the respectable instruments would do nothing useful here even if they were perfectly valid, because the job is not to *describe* you, it is to *tune delivery* to you. We **instrument the constraint, not the person.** The category you would carry around is a flatter; the constraint your work runs into is a fact. We are reading the constraint.

What comes out of the check is not a label. It is a starting point. A *situation*, which selects the kit that loads. A *persona* tag — first-time founder, repeat founder, career-changer, lead — which decides whether each module is taught to *develop a faculty in you* or just to *hand you the frame.* And a wiring profile, which tunes the pacing and density and how aggressively the frame gets externalized. Re-take the check as you change, because you will change. The output is meant to move.

Three reads. One composition that quietly runs the rest of the kit: **situation picks the module set; persona sets develop-vs-borrow; wiring tunes the delivery.** Hold that sentence — we are going to be honoring it whether you see us doing it or not.

## Lesson 3 — Build It, or Borrow It — Knowing Which

The check has located you. The kit it pointed you at is full of skills you might end up using — directly, or through someone else. So before we build anything, there is one call to make about each of them, and getting it right will save you more wasted effort than anything else in this unit.

The two jobs come back, narrowed. The front and the back, but not for your career — for this skill, in front of you, right now. And the question is: do you need to *own* this, or just *direct* it well enough to stay in charge?

The two honest answers carry weight. One says **develop the faculty** — make this skill a real, owned capability, the kind you can hold both ends with for years. The other says **borrow the frame** — pick up just enough understanding to stay answerable for a call you will never personally author. Both answers are legitimate. Most builders need a mix. Neither answer is the lazy one. The lazy move is not picking — building too much by reflex, or borrowing too little out of fear.

You build for real when the skill is yours to live with. When you will be the one paged at 2 a.m. about it. When the consequences of being wrong about it accrue to your name, your team, your customers. Then you owe it the full faculty. You can direct work in a craft you do not own, but you cannot stand behind work in one — and standing behind it is what we are here for.

You borrow just enough when you will always hand it off. When the skill is real and important but it is *not yours to author* — when a specialist, an agency, an agent, or a vendor will do the producing, and your job is to direct credibly and review honestly. Then you owe the work *enough frame to stay answerable for a call you will never author.* Not more. Building a full faculty in a skill you will never personally exercise is a different kind of mistake than borrowing too little; it costs you weeks and ends in atrophy from disuse.

Both traps are real, and they fail in different ways. Borrow when you should have built, and you go soft — you cannot catch a problem you do not understand, and "trust the expert" eventually becomes a way of saying *I no longer hold this end.* Build when you should have borrowed, and you sink weeks into something you could have rented for the cost of a subscription, while the actual scarce thing — your judgment on what is worth doing in the first place — sits unused.

There is another wrinkle, and the check has already pre-empted it. The same situation can hand two builders different calls on the same skill, because the *persona* tag changes the default. A first-time founder in the SaaS Vendor Dependency situation may be building the founder faculty — origination, market sense, the why-now — for the first time, and needs to develop it. A repeat founder in the same situation already holds that faculty and just needs to borrow whatever engineering frame they do not have. Same situation, opposite calls. The persona overlay is what makes the same kit land correctly on both builders.

A move worth holding onto: borrowing now can turn into building later. The decision is reversible by intent, not just by accident. You can start with just enough frame to be answerable for what a contractor builds, and a year later — if the skill turns out to be central to your work — start developing the full faculty. The kit is built for that; it does not punish you for changing your mind in the direction of more capability.

One last note, foreshadow only, because it is going to matter once you cross into Unit 3. The same call you are making here also configures the AI's teaching register at every gate of the build. When the agent works on a skill you are developing, it is told to *narrate the why* — to leave deliberate blanks for you to fill, to teach. When the agent works on a skill you are borrowing, it is told to *produce* — to deliver the artifact and the minimum frame you need to field a question about it. Same Skill, two modes. **Write each Skill once, with both registers as switchable modes; select the mode by persona tag at intake — never maintain two Skill libraries.** That is an authoring rule; you will never see it. But it is yours that sets the switch.

## Lesson 4 — The Moves Everyone Makes

Before we go to the six situations and the kit that comes with each, we have to put five moves on the table that go in every kit. Whoever you are, however the check sorted you, these five travel with you. Situation configures the middle; these hold the ends. Skip them and the kit cannot do its job, because the kit is built to extend these, not to replace them.

The first is the question we already raised in Unit 1, pointed now at the work in front of you, not at your career arc. Before the kit, before the modules, before the agents — *Did I choose this, or did it choose me?* In Unit 1 we asked it about a build. Here we ask it about the modules you are about to load, the borrows you are about to make, the kit you are about to run. Origination at this layer is the same job as origination at any other layer. If the modules you pick up are the ones the situation reflex hands you, you have abdicated at the level of the kit while doing all the right things inside it. The first move is to make the kit yours.

The second is the same sentence we ended Lesson 4 of Unit 1 with, and you will hear it again because it is now the operating condition of your entire kit: **You are the infrastructure.** Every module you run, every borrow you make, every call between develop and frame — it routes through the one piece of the system you are most likely to forget to maintain. The check captured your wiring; the dashboard from Unit 1 reads your weather. This lesson only restates that none of the moves below work on a degraded instrument. The kit assumes you. Stay you.

The third is the move that names what every situation in this kit is going to ask of you, and we are putting it here so you see it once before the six show it to you six times: **every fix is a trade.** No move makes a problem vanish; it swaps it for a new one. Insource the agency you've been paying, and you trade relationship-and-contract risk for model-capability and lock-in risk; the risk did not leave you, because Unit 1 said it cannot. Bring a SaaS line item in-house, and you trade a subscription for a maintenance burden — discovered a year later, in the ops-staffing line you did not know to budget. Modernize the legacy system you inherited, and you trade a system you couldn't see for one where a model now makes the change decisions on your behalf. The skill is not escaping problems. It is picking the one worth having. We are going to bloom this fully in Lesson 5; here it just gets named, so the kit is honest about what it is.

The fourth is short and ungenerous. The AI writes faster than anyone can read. The one thing in the loop that does not speed up is your ability to actually review what came back, and that bottleneck is now load-bearing in a way it never was when you were writing the code yourself. Guard the one thing that does not speed up: your ability to actually review work you did not write. We will instrument this in Unit 3, formally, as a gate that cannot be handed off. Here it is just a discipline you carry into the kit: do not accept a pace your review cannot honestly keep.

The fifth is its mirror, on the front end. Say what *right* means before you build, not after. Write the target down where you can compare against it — what would make this the right answer, in plain language, before the agent has a chance to hand you something that *looks finished* and quietly redefines right for you. A frame written in advance is one you can check the output against. A frame held in your head on a tired afternoon is a frame the agent reframes for you without either of you noticing. Both ends — origination and answerability — get teeth from this one move.

Five moves. *Did I choose this. You are the infrastructure. Every fix is a trade. Stay able to check work you didn't write. Say what right means before you build.* These are the floor. Your situation adds the rest on top.

## Lesson 5 — Find Your Situation

Now the kits. Six common spots, the trade each one asks you to make, and the long-game question the trade hands you to carry. But before any of them, the reframe that runs through all six, because without it the six look like a menu of escapes and they are not.

The instinct everyone arrives with is the wrong one, and we should get it on the table before any kit lands. You walked in here looking for the fix that *gets rid of* the dependency you are worried about. The contractor, the vendor, the framework, the legacy code. Find the move that makes it go away. We are not going to give you that move, because that move does not exist.

**Every escape is a trade.** No move sheds risk; it only changes risk's shape. This follows straight from the spine we wrote into the first sentence of Unit 1: if you bear all the risk and the machine bears none, then bringing the work in-house, replacing the vendor, modernizing the system, hardening the prototype — none of these *remove* a dependency. They *relocate* it. You shed one dependency and pick up a different one. The fix is real; the escape is a story.

The reframe that does the actual teaching work is the one you have to feel before you read the six. **Escaping dependency was never the goal; choosing which dependency is worth holding is.** This is the first question of the kit — *Did I choose this, or did it choose me?* — pointed at the dependency rather than the product. Origination applied to the trade itself. And the tone matters here, because this reframe can land the wrong way and ruin the unit. It is not "it's all dependencies, why bother." It is clear-eyed choosing. You are not stuck with the dependency you have. You are **trading up to a dependency worth holding.**

The way you make that trade real, instead of letting it stay a story you tell yourself, is mechanical. For each situation you fall into, you keep a small ledger — two columns, *Shed* and *Adopted.* Most people fill in *Shed* without trouble; that is the thing they came in wanting to get rid of. The discipline is to **force at least three entries into the *Adopted* column** and then **sign one residual-risk statement** — one sentence that names what you are choosing to hold, why it is acceptable, and what you instrument or do to make it acceptable. That signature is the whole move. An empty Adopted column does not mean the trade was easy. It means the trade was not decided. It was made, by default, with the new dependency hidden until it showed up as a surprise in year two. We are not letting that happen. The kits include this ledger; the rest of this lesson walks the six.

Read them as one long beat each, and read carefully *only the one the check pointed you at.* The others are useful, but they are useful as borrowable scaffolding (the next lesson) — not as reading you owe right now.

**You've got people worth growing.** You have seen something in one of your people — or in two or three of them — that you cannot un-see. Maybe one is in customer support and keeps proposing better workflows. Maybe one is in operations and built an internal tool with AI that actually works. Maybe it is a self-taught generalist who keeps finding ways to be useful in roles that did not exist before. Sometimes it is a single standout; sometimes it is a small team of change-makers — the handful who would shift how your company actually builds, if they were developed. You do not want to lose them to a bigger company that develops talent faster, and you do not want to keep paying outside vendors when the right rawness is already on your payroll. The work you are committing to is *transferring the engineering and product-ownership faculty into specific developable people* — not training the whole org, not hiring, not sending someone to a bootcamp. The kit is apprenticeship-shaped on purpose: a teaching protocol, an inquiry-vs-delegation diagnostic to spot the learner who is going soft, a manager's module on sponsoring without smothering. The trade you are making is plain when you write it down. You shed the dependence on external hiring. You adopt three things: the risk of developing *delegators who atrophy* instead of *inquirers who build real capability*; the risk that the capability walks out the door once it is real; and an ongoing sponsorship load — inquiry over delegation has to be protected, not assumed. The long-game question your trade will hand you, the one Unit 4 will measure, is the one you have to learn to ask honestly: *is my bench actually getting stronger, or quietly going dull?* The dimensions this lights — Identity and Cognitive — are Unit 4's harvest, not ours; we are just naming them as you go past.

**You're renting what you could own.** You pay every month for a tool that does something close to your core, and you wonder whether you should build your own. Both ends of that tool currently sit with the vendor — they originate its direction, they answer for its uptime, and you live downstream of both. The kit here is *borrow the frame, minimally* — enough engineering literacy to own and run what you used to rent, plus a build-vs-buy decision module sharp enough to tell apart what is genuinely your differentiator from what is a commodity you should keep paying for. The trade is one founders walk into all the time and only see the wrong column of. You shed perpetual rent and the vendor's control of a slice of operations. You adopt the maintenance burden — the answerable half of *build it yourself* — plus capability and model risk if the new tool is agent-built (deprecation, drift), plus the upkeep that can quietly eat the savings, discovered a year later in the ops-staffing line you did not know to budget. The long-game question: *did the time and money I saved actually show up — or get eaten by the upkeep I took on?* Dimensions: Temporal and Economic.

**Someone else builds your software.** An agency or contractor writes your code, and you direct it but do not own it. Origination is partial — they translate your vision into product calls you do not see — and answerability is largely external. The kit moves both back in-house. You build enough literacy to direct the work credibly without being an engineer; if there is internal talent to receive it, you also develop the engineering faculty in them; you set up the audit and handover discipline that keeps tribal knowledge from walking out with the contract. The trade is the one that gets cleanest as agents commoditize the work. You shed the agency margin and the *don't own what you can't change or see* trap. You adopt origination and answerability you must now actually hold — review capacity, directing literacy — plus a differentiation question that lands harder than founders expect: once the same agents are available to everyone, what distinguishes you from the next founder with the identical stack? Plus the real risk of transition stall and lost knowledge during handover. The long-game question: *what makes me different from the next founder with the exact same stack — and does it still pay?* Dimensions: Differential and Economic.

**You inherited a mess you're scared to touch.** A codebase you didn't write, can't fully see, and answer for. This one is unusual in the unit because you hold the answerable end *but cannot hold the frame* — and not because you abdicated. The frame is missing. The previous authors never wrote it down or are no longer there. The kit develops the faculty, because borrowing isn't enough when the system is yours to maintain. Code comprehension and legacy archaeology run first. Characterization tests — the safety net you put under code whose intent is undocumented — run *before* you change anything. A modernize-vs-replace decision module sits at the end. The trade is the one that flatters AI the most and is the most dangerous to take cheap. You shed the system you couldn't see, couldn't safely touch, and feared. You adopt: a modernization in which a model now makes change decisions on your behalf over code you still do not fully frame; the brownfield risk that AI can actually *slow* you on mature code you know well; and the slow, unborrowable work of building the situation model before any safe change. The long-game question: *how much of the judgment about this code is still mine?* Dimension: Judgment.

**Your team hasn't really moved on AI — or it has, and you can't answer for what it ships.** This one shows up in two shapes, and sometimes the same organization has both. The first shape is the team that hasn't moved. You have developers. Some bought Copilot seats. Some tried Cursor. Some have not seriously evaluated any of it. None of you are operating AI-native — none of you are working meaningfully differently than you did three years ago. Every week brings another LinkedIn post from a competitor claiming 10x gains, and you cannot tell if those posts are real, exaggerated, or true and yours is the problem. You feel behind and do not know how far. The second shape is the opposite-looking team that turns out to have the same gap. AI is producing plenty of working code. PRs land fast. The team looks like it has adopted. But you suspect — and you are right to — that nobody is fully verifying or explaining what is shipping. Velocity is up. Standing behind what shipped is down. Either way, AI is not yet an *owned capability* — either you have not taken it up, or you have taken it up without owning the half that matters. The kit develops the faculty because *closing the gap is building owned capability,* and it reaches both shapes through the same destination. Spec-driven development — the spec as the executable contract — runs first. A "can you verify what you shipped?" diagnostic, sharpened by which shape you are in. For the stalled team, a credible first-move pathway that does not just buy seats; for the blind-velocity team, the vibe-to-engineering ladder that walks the team from prompt-and-pray to structured, gated work. The trade looks different by shape and ends in the same place. The pre-adoption team sheds the drag of staying structurally behind, and adopts change-management load plus the tool-evaluation cost plus the real risk of crossing into the blind shape if they chase velocity before the answerable end. The post-adoption-blind team sheds the bottleneck of producing by hand, and adopts verification debt — shippable output nobody can fully verify or explain — plus review-capacity load that does not scale by hiring, plus automation complacency that keeps the gap invisible, plus the specific substitution called the *self-spec*: the agent writing the spec it then implements, hiding origination inside a producible artifact. The long-game question is the same either way, because both kits aim at the same target: *can I still make that call as the volume keeps climbing?* Dimensions: Epistemic and Cognitive.

**Your demo has to become a real product.** You went into this thinking the new tools had removed the engineering time and cost from the equation. And in a way, they had. You built it — on Lovable, on Base44, on Replit, on Bubble, on a Cursor-vibe-coded stack — often in days or weeks, not months. It looks amazing. Real users showed up. Maybe revenue. Maybe enough revenue to quit your day job. And now you are staring at the gap between what you built and what it would actually take to serve 10x the users, integrate with real systems, pass a security review, or be acquired. *Built it. Looks amazing. Scaling is not happening.* The prototype was the easy part. The thing underneath was not built to hold. You feel both proud and exposed. Origination already happened — the thing exists — but the answerable back end was never required by a prototype: security, reliability, real data, observability, the non-functional registers that decide whether something is a product or a parlor trick. The kit develops the faculty on the non-functional surface (a production-readiness hardening module is its flagship; a security module specifically for AI-generated code sits next to it) and borrows the frame where the founder will rely on agents or specialists. The trade has the longest moral tail of any in the unit. You shed the freedom of a prototype that never had to hold the answerable half. You adopt: accountability in registers the prototype never owed — real user data, security, reliability, the consequences that land on people once the thing is live; an expanded threat surface from AI-generated code that the literature already documents; and observability, SLA, and compliance obligations as actual release requirements rather than nice-to-haves. The long-game question: *am I ready to be accountable in ways a prototype never asked of me?* Dimension: Ethical.

Six spots, six trades. The kit is what runs through them. But the part you are going to take past this unit, into the rest of your career, is the part that does not fit on any kit's bullet list. **Each spot drops a different job and makes a different trade — and the trade is the part you'll be tracking for the rest of your career.** That tracking is what Unit 4 is. We are seeding it now so the kit you walk away with is honest about the inheritance it leaves you.

## Lesson 6 — Your Kit Isn't a Cage

The kit you just got is real. Use it. But it cannot be the only thing you use, because real life almost never fits one box. You will be the founder bringing the work back in-house — and the codebase you are inheriting is a mess. You will be the team closing the AI gap — and you will hit the prototype-to-production wall in the same quarter. You will be the lead growing engineers — and you will need a vendor-economics call to stay ranked on what's worth building yourselves. The six situations are not fixed addresses. They are common shapes, and most lives put you across more than one of them.

So borrow on purpose. When your reality calls for a move from another situation's kit, pull it in — deliberately, not at random. Borrow the inheritance protocol from Legacy if you just absorbed someone else's code, even if the check pointed you at the AI Gap. Borrow the build-vs-buy module from SaaS Vendor Dependency if the agency you are insourcing leaves a tool decision behind. Borrow the production-readiness hardening from the prototype kit the day a feature crosses from internal to external. The borrow grid exists for this. Use it the way a working chef uses someone else's knife — because it's the right one for the cut, not because you wanted a new knife.

Two borrows go in almost every kit, even when the situation does not call for them on paper. Checking work you didn't write. Saying what *right* means first. We named them as Moves 4 and 5 last lesson, and we are naming them again here because they are the two universals builders most often drop when their situation does not foreground them. If you take nothing else from outside your default kit, take those two.

A line worth holding onto when you borrow. Borrow the *module* — the unit of guidance and the exercise. Do not borrow the *register*. If you are a develop-the-faculty builder, a borrow you pick up from a borrow-the-frame kit still gets taught to *you* in develop-the-faculty mode — the agents narrate the why, leave you the blanks, push you toward the owned capability. The other way around, the same. Otherwise the borrows quietly hollow you out by teaching you frames where you owed faculties. The check told us who you are; that does not change when you reach across kits.

And the last thing, which is also the reason this lesson is in the unit at all. Your situation is not a permanent residence. The builder bringing software back in-house will, in eighteen months, be the builder growing engineers. The builder hardening their prototype will, six months after launch, be the builder holding verification debt. The builder who took the check today will not be the same builder a year from now. The kit is built to travel. Borrowing is how it travels with you.

## Lesson 7 — Where to Start Today, and How the Rest Helps You

We have done all the structural work this unit is going to do. Before you close it, here is the one thing to do this week, and a short map of how everything else helps you.

Your first move. Take the check, name your situation, and run the first exercise in your kit. That is one sentence and one piece of work; do not pad it. If the check says SaaS Vendor Dependency, run the build-vs-buy on one line item you have been wondering about. If it says Legacy, write down the *three things you do not understand* about the system you inherited and pick the one a characterization test would protect first. If it says AI Gap, write the spec for the next ticket *before* the agent does — and check whether your spec and the agent's would have differed. The exercise is small on purpose; everything else in your kit is a deeper version of it.

Here is the unit in one line, and it is the one to keep when the lessons blur: there is no single right path — there is the one built for where you actually are, and the freedom to borrow the rest.

What's coming, and what it does for you.

In the next unit, your kit becomes the *setup sheet* for how you actually run a build with a crew of agents. The ADLC — the day-to-day loop — is the same pipeline for every builder; the kit you just got configures which agents foreground at which gates, which gate is hardened for you, and how each Skill is taught. You will recognize the modules from this unit, but as moves in a working build rather than entries in a kit. Unit 1 gave you the stance. Unit 2 located you. Unit 3 puts the stance and the location into a day of work.

In the unit after that, every trade you just made becomes a question you carry across a career. The eight long-game questions you noticed inside the six situations — *is my bench going dull, did the savings actually show up, what makes me different, how much of the judgment is still mine, can I still make the call, am I ready to be accountable in registers I didn't previously owe* — these are not lesson ornaments. They are the readings Unit 4 takes off the work over years. Sit with that. The trades that look like one-time calls are actually the things you will be watching trend.

One small thing, surfaced rather than buried. The six situations collectively light *eight* of Unit 4's nine dimensions. One — *Vocational* — has no Unit 2 seed. We will not paper over that here. It is a real call worth holding: does Vocational genuinely belong only to the long clock, or does Unit 2 need a situation pointer to it? Unit 4 will pick that up.

The two clocks return, because every unit ends here for a reason. *Becoming* is this situation, this kit, this week's first exercise. *Remaining* is the trade you just signed, watched across every situation you will move through. One stance, two clocks, same instrument. Now you know where it is pointed.

## Source assumptions

A few notes on what this draft is doing under the hood, since the playbook-editor skill asks for them.

- **Doctrine and supporting artifacts pulled from, mostly verbatim where bolded:** [unit-2-outline.md](curriculum/outlines/unit-2-outline.md) (lesson promises, the five moves, the situation beat shape), [audience-research-plan.md](curriculum/modules/audience-research-plan.md) (the load-bearing diagnostic, the operating-profile streams, the three-layer composition), [relocation-ledger.md](curriculum/modules/relocation-ledger.md) (every-escape-is-a-trade, the Adopted-column discipline, the residual-risk signature, the long-game questions and dimension pointers per situation), [module-skill-crosswalk.md](curriculum/modules/module-skill-crosswalk.md) (the governing one-liner; the register-switch standard quoted in Lesson 3), [organic-intelligence.md](doctrine/organic-intelligence.md) (the two ends, the producible/answerable split), [human-condition.md](doctrine/human-condition.md) (you-are-the-infrastructure, ported into Lesson 4 as one of the five moves), [observability.md](doctrine/observability.md) (instrument-don't-introspect, ported into Lesson 2).
- **Voice register held to:** plain language with weight; lecturer addressing a capable builder; the sorting move stated first and returned to; structural posture rather than memoir; named situations carried as scenes, not as codes.
- **Felt-need rhythm.** Each lesson opens on a pressure the builder can feel (the floor splits; the mirror returns; every skill carries the same question; the reframe before the menu of escapes; real life doesn't fit one box) before any abstraction lands, runs the distinction, hands a usable move where one exists, and returns. The whole unit's return is Lesson 7's *one stance, two clocks, same instrument — now you know where it is pointed.*
- **A drift carried forward, not silently resolved.** Unit 2 *blooms* two concepts — the *relocation lens* and the *operating profile* — that Unit 1 does not yet seed. The research plan calls for a one-line seed of each in Unit 1's stance; neither exists in the current Unit 1 outline or granular. This draft introduces both at full bloom (Lesson 5 for the relocation lens, Lesson 2 for the operating profile) and explains nothing about the missing seed. If Unit 1 picks up the seeds, Lesson 2 and Lesson 5 here get to land as bloom rather than introduction; if it doesn't, the load lands here.
- **Held back deliberately.** The teaching-agent transfer-vs-substitution distinction is foreshadowed at the end of Lesson 3 (the register switch) but not bloomed — that is Unit 3 Lesson 5's job. The module-to-agent crosswalk is referenced only as "setup sheet" in Lesson 7; the join itself is internal authoring structure for now. The five-vs-five universals tension (intake universals in Lesson 2 vs. ongoing universals in Lesson 4) is implicit in the prose but not named as such — see structural observation #2 in [unit-2-granular.md](curriculum/outlines/unit-2-granular.md).
- **Held open for you to fill.** No named Clay scenes. The strongest slots, if you want to ground specific passages, are Lesson 3 (a build/borrow call you got wrong both ways and what it taught you — develop-when-you-should-have-borrowed and the reverse), Lesson 5/SVD (a build-vs-buy call you made and what showed up in year two), Lesson 5/EDD (the agency relationship that taught you which end is yours), Lesson 5/LSD (a legacy system you untangled and the method you used), and Lesson 5/PPW (a prototype you carried across the wall and what broke first). These are the per-experience anchors the audience research plan asks for as "Clay-experience anchors to capture." I left them as posture rather than write them as memoir.
- **The six situations were written as scenes, not as codes.** The granular ToC carries the ITW/SVD/EDD/LSD/AID/PPW shorthand because the matrix and crosswalk depend on it. The narrative deliberately does not — builders meet the situations by their lived shape, not their initialism. If this draft moves toward lesson content, that distinction should hold: the codes stay backstage.
