# Lesson 4 — The Floor

Your situation configures the middle of your kit. Some things hold the ends regardless of which situation claimed you. Five moves go in every kit. Three of them you already met in Unit 1; they earn their place here as the operating conditions of any configured kit. Two are new, because the kit you are about to run does something the bare practice of Unit 1 didn't yet require.

The three from Unit 1, restated in the form they take here.

***Did I choose this, or did it choose me?*** In Unit 1 I asked it about a build. Here you ask it about the modules you load, the borrows you make, the situation you claim. If the modules you pick up are the ones the situation reflex hands you, you have abdicated at the level of the kit while doing all the right things inside it. The first move is to make the kit yours.

***You are the infrastructure.*** Every module you run, every borrow you make, every call between develop and frame — each routes through the piece of the system you are most likely to forget to maintain. The check captured your wiring; the dashboard from Unit 1 reads your weather. None of the moves below works on a degraded instrument. The kit assumes you. Stay you.

***Every fix is a trade.*** You met your trade by name in Lesson 1. Naming it here, after the six rather than before them, lands it differently than naming it before would. The discipline is to keep meeting it. Each time you cross between situations — and you will — the move is the same: book the *Shed*, force the *Adopted*, sign the residual-risk statement. Three sentences in a notebook. The whole move.

The two that are new, because Unit 1 didn't yet need them to be their own discipline.

**Stay able to check work you didn't write.** The AI writes faster than anyone can read. The one thing in the loop that does not speed up is your ability to actually review what came back, and that bottleneck is now load-bearing in a way it never was when you were writing the code yourself. Unit 3 will formalize this as a gate that cannot be handed off. Here it is the discipline you carry into the kit: do not accept a pace your review cannot honestly keep. Treat your review capacity as a constraint to plan around, not a function of effort.

**Say what *right* means before you build, not after.** Write the target down where you can compare against it — what would make this the right answer, in plain language, before the agent has a chance to hand you something that *looks finished* and quietly redefines right for you. A frame written in advance is one you can check the output against. A frame held in your head on a tired afternoon is one the agent reframes for you without either of you noticing. The Unit 1 move on this — *externalize the frame before you prompt* — was the first-build version of this discipline. Here it grows up: every build, every situation, every borrowed module gets a written target.

Five moves. *Did I choose this. You are the infrastructure. Every fix is a trade. Stay able to check work you didn't write. Say what right means before you build.* The first three hold the ends. The last two protect the speed at which you are now able to work. **These are the floor. Your situation adds the rest on top.**
