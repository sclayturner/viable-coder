# Lesson 1 — Find Your Situation

The last unit closed on the line *I want to do what I want to do, damn it. You do too.* I meant it as a handoff. This unit is where the wanting meets the world it has to move through.

Whatever you came in here to get — a budget freed for a project that matters to you, a system you can stand behind, a team you can grow, a thing you have built that you want to keep alive — your aim runs through the place you are actually standing. Not the place you wish you were standing. Not the place a different builder is standing. The one you are in. The aim does not move you by itself; the work that gets you there does, and the work that gets you there is shaped by where you are starting from.

There are six common spots that builders are stuck in right now, and there is a high chance you are in one of them — sometimes in more than one. I am going to walk you through the six. Read the one you recognize carefully; let the others pass for now. The kit has been quietly recording your behavior since Lesson 1 of the last unit, and it can confirm what you recognized from another direction — we will let it do that in the next lesson. But you and I both already know roughly where you are. Let me draw the spots.

Before I do, there is a reframe that has to run through all six, because without it the six look like a menu of escapes — and they are not.

The instinct everyone arrives with is the wrong one, and we should get it out on the table. You walked in here looking for the fix that *gets rid of* the dependency you are worried about. The contractor. The vendor. The framework. The legacy code. The brittle prototype. Find the move that makes the thing go away. I am not going to give you that move, because that move does not exist.

**Every escape is a trade.** No move sheds risk; it only changes its shape. This follows straight from the line at the front of Unit 1: if you bear all the risk and the machine bears none, then bringing the work in-house, replacing the vendor, modernizing the system, hardening the prototype — none of these *remove* a dependency. They *relocate* it. You shed one dependency and pick up a different one. The fix is real; the escape is a story.

The reframe that does the actual teaching work is one you have to feel before you read the six. **Escaping dependency was never the goal. Choosing which dependency is worth holding is.** This is the first question of the kit — *Did I choose this, or did it choose me?* — pointed at the dependency rather than the product. Origination applied to the trade itself. The tone matters here, because the reframe can land the wrong way and ruin the lesson. It is not *it's all dependencies, why bother.* It is clear-eyed choosing. You are not stuck with the dependency you have. You are **trading up to a dependency worth holding.**

The way you make that trade real, instead of letting it stay a story you tell yourself, is mechanical. For each situation you fall into, you keep a small ledger — two columns, *Shed* and *Adopted*. Most people fill in *Shed* without trouble; that is the thing they came in wanting to get rid of. The discipline is to force at least three entries into the *Adopted* column, and then to sign one **residual-risk statement** — one sentence that names what you are choosing to hold, why it is acceptable, and what you instrument or do to make it acceptable. That signature is the whole move. An empty Adopted column does not mean the trade was easy. It means the trade was not decided. It was made, by default, with the new dependency hidden until it showed up as a surprise in year two. We are not letting that happen.

What follows are the six spots. **Read the one you recognize.** The other five are useful, but they are useful as borrowable scaffolding two lessons from now, not as reading you owe right now.

### You've got people worth growing

You have seen something in one of your people — or in two or three of them — that you cannot un-see. Maybe one is in customer support and keeps proposing better workflows. Maybe one is in operations and built an internal tool with AI that actually works. Maybe it is a self-taught generalist who keeps finding ways to be useful in roles that did not exist before. Sometimes it is a single standout; sometimes it is a small team of change-makers — the handful who would shift how your company actually builds, if they were developed. You do not want to lose them to a bigger company that develops talent faster, and you do not want to keep paying outside vendors when the right rawness is already on your payroll.

The work you are committing to is transferring real engineering and product-ownership faculty into specific developable people — not training the whole org, not hiring outside, not sending someone to a bootcamp. The kit is apprenticeship-shaped on purpose: a teaching protocol, an inquiry-versus-delegation diagnostic to spot the learner who is going soft, a manager's module on sponsoring without smothering.

I have done this work and lived its hardest part — the version where the sponsorship pays off, and the version where it produces a polished delegator of the agent instead of an engineer. The difference does not announce itself. It surfaces in the work the developer is asked to stand behind two years in, and by then the bet you made is the bet you have.

The trade is plain when you write it down. You shed the dependence on external hiring. You adopt three things: the risk of developing *delegators who atrophy* instead of *inquirers who build real capability*; the risk that the capability walks out the door once it is real; and an ongoing sponsorship load — inquiry over delegation has to be protected, not assumed. The long-game question you will carry into Unit 4, the one you will have to learn to ask honestly: *is my bench actually getting stronger, or quietly going dull?*

### You're renting what you could own

You pay every month for a tool that does something close to your core, and you wonder whether you should build your own. Both ends of that tool currently sit with the vendor — they originate its direction, they answer for its uptime, and you live downstream of both.

The kit here is to borrow the frame, minimally — enough engineering literacy to own and run what you used to rent, plus a build-versus-buy decision module sharp enough to tell apart what is genuinely your differentiator from what is a commodity you should keep paying for.

I have made this call in both directions. The wrong call always announces itself in a budget line nobody thought to draw — the upkeep on what I built when I should not have, or the year-three rent on what I rented when I could have owned by year two for less.

The trade is one founders walk into all the time and only see the wrong column of. You shed perpetual rent and the vendor's control of a slice of your operations. You adopt the maintenance burden — the answerable half of *build it yourself* — plus capability and model risk if the new tool is agent-built (deprecation, drift), plus the upkeep that can quietly eat the savings, discovered a year later in the ops-staffing line you did not know to budget. The long-game question: *did the time and money I saved actually show up — or get eaten by the upkeep I took on?*

### Someone else builds your software

An agency or contractor writes your code, and you direct it but do not own it. Origination is partial — they translate your vision into product calls you do not see — and answerability is largely external.

The kit moves both back in-house. You build enough literacy to direct the work credibly without becoming an engineer yourself; if there is internal talent to receive it, you also develop the engineering faculty in them; you set up the audit and handover discipline that keeps tribal knowledge from walking out with the contract.

I have been on both sides of this one — the founder directing contractors I could not effectively push back on, and the technical founder bringing that work back in once the cost of *not being able to see it* came due. The agency margin is the part you notice. The directing-without-frame trap is the part that costs you the year.

The trade gets cleaner as agents commoditize the work, and harder in the same breath. You shed the agency margin and the *don't own what you can't change or see* trap. You adopt origination and answerability you must now actually hold — review capacity, directing literacy — plus a differentiation question that lands harder than founders expect: once the same agents are available to everyone, what distinguishes you from the next founder with the identical stack? Plus the real risk of transition stall and lost tribal knowledge during handover. The long-game question: *what makes me different from the next founder with the exact same stack — and does it still pay?*

### You inherited a mess you're scared to touch

A codebase you did not write, cannot fully see, and answer for. This situation is unusual in the unit because you hold the answerable end *but cannot hold the frame* — and not because you abdicated. The frame is missing. The previous authors never wrote it down or are no longer there.

The kit develops the faculty, because borrowing is not enough when the system is yours to maintain. Code comprehension and legacy archaeology run first. Characterization tests — the safety net you put under code whose intent is undocumented — run *before* you change anything. A modernize-versus-replace decision module sits at the end.

I have walked into codebases whose authors had walked out the door before me, and I have learned — slowly, with appropriate damage — that nothing I do to that code is safe until I have written the safety net beneath it. The frame the previous authors did not leave behind is the one I have to build before AI is allowed near the work.

The trade is the one that flatters AI the most and is the most dangerous to take cheap. You shed the system you couldn't see, couldn't safely touch, and feared. You adopt: a modernization in which a model now makes change decisions on your behalf over code you still do not fully frame; the brownfield risk that AI can actually *slow* you on mature code you know well; and the slow, unborrowable work of building the situation model before any safe change. The long-game question: *how much of the judgment about this code is still mine?*

### Your team hasn't really moved on AI — or it has, and you can't answer for what it ships

This one shows up in two shapes, and sometimes the same organization has both.

The first shape is the team that has not moved. You have developers. Some bought Copilot seats. Some tried Cursor. Some have not seriously evaluated any of it. None of you are operating AI-native. None of you are working meaningfully differently than you did three years ago. Every week brings another LinkedIn post from a competitor claiming 10x gains, and you cannot tell whether those posts are real, exaggerated, or true and yours is the problem. You feel behind and do not know how far.

The second shape is the opposite-looking team that turns out to have the same gap. AI is producing plenty of working code. PRs land fast. The team *looks* like it has adopted. But you suspect — and you are right to — that nobody is fully verifying or explaining what is shipping. Velocity is up. Standing behind what shipped is down.

Either way, AI is not yet an *owned capability* — either you have not taken it up, or you have taken it up without owning the half that matters. The kit develops the faculty in both directions through one destination. Spec-driven development runs first — the spec as the executable contract. A *can-you-verify-what-you-shipped* diagnostic is sharpened by which shape you are in. For the stalled team, a credible first-move pathway that does not just buy seats. For the blind-velocity team, the vibe-to-engineering ladder that walks the team from prompt-and-pray to structured, gated work.

I have lived both shapes. I have watched teams I respected stay structurally behind for reasons that felt sensible at the time, and I have been the person — in the last eighteen months — producing more in a week than a quarter used to hold, and asking, honestly, whether I could still stand behind every piece of what came out. The gap shows up in different telemetry depending on which shape you are in. It is the same gap.

The trade looks different by shape and ends in the same place. The pre-adoption team sheds the drag of staying structurally behind and adopts change-management load, the tool-evaluation cost, and the risk of crossing into the blind shape if it chases velocity before the answerable end. The post-adoption-blind team sheds the bottleneck of producing by hand and adopts verification debt — shippable output nobody can fully verify or explain — plus review-capacity load that does not scale by hiring, plus automation complacency that keeps the gap invisible, plus the specific substitution called the *self-spec*: the agent writing the spec it then implements, hiding origination inside a producible artifact. The long-game question is the same either way, because both kits aim at the same target: *can I still make that call as the volume keeps climbing?*

### Your demo has to become a real product

You went into this thinking the new tools had removed the engineering time and cost from the equation. And in a way, they had. You built it — on Lovable, on Base44, on Replit, on Bubble, on a Cursor-vibe-coded stack — often in days or weeks, not months. It looks amazing. Real users showed up. Maybe revenue. Maybe enough revenue to quit your day job. And now you are staring at the gap between what you built and what it would take to serve 10x the users, integrate with real systems, pass a security review, or be acquired. *Built it. Looks amazing. Scaling is not happening.* The prototype was the easy part. The thing underneath was not built to hold. You feel both proud and exposed.

Origination already happened — the thing exists — but the answerable back end was never required by a prototype: security, reliability, real data, observability, the non-functional registers that decide whether something is a product or a parlor trick. The kit develops the faculty on the non-functional surface (a production-readiness hardening module is its flagship; a security module specifically for AI-generated code sits next to it) and borrows the frame where the founder will rely on agents or specialists.

I have built things that found users faster than the infrastructure underneath them was built to hold, and I have done the unglamorous work of putting the answerable half in after the fact. It is harder when the prototype is already in front of customers than when it was still on your laptop, and AI both speeds the building and slows the hardening — in proportions you do not see until you are inside it.

The trade has the longest moral tail of any in the unit. You shed the freedom of a prototype that never had to hold the answerable half. You adopt: accountability in registers the prototype never owed — real user data, security, reliability, the consequences that land on people once the thing is live; an expanded threat surface from AI-generated code that the literature already documents; and observability, SLA, and compliance obligations as actual release requirements rather than nice-to-haves. The long-game question: *am I ready to be accountable in ways a prototype never asked of me?*

---

Six spots. Six trades. **Each spot drops a different job and makes a different trade — and the trade is the part you will be tracking for the rest of your career.** That tracking is what Unit 4 is. We are seeding it now so the kit you walk away with is honest about the inheritance it leaves you.

Before the check, a word about how I know what is in those six scenes. I have stood in most of them, sometimes in more than one at the same time, and watched the rest from close enough to see the shape of the trade as it played out. I have grown developers I needed, and lost some I had spent years sponsoring. I have made the build-versus-buy call wrong, and right, and watched both bills come due. I have signed off on architectures I could not push back on, and I have inherited code whose authors had walked out the door before me. I have shipped things that found users before the infrastructure underneath them was built to hold them. And in the last eighteen months I have stopped writing code in the line-by-line sense — agents do that now — which means I have been living the gap I am asking you to look at, from the inside, on my own work first.

None of that is here as a credential. It is here because each of these trades came with a moment I either caught or did not, and I would rather you have those misses available to you than read this unit as if I were standing outside it. **I can advise where I have stood.** That is the contract for the rest of this kit.

If one of those scenes felt unmistakably like your own, that is the situation we will configure your kit against. If two or three of them felt half-true, that is normal — most lives put you across more than one of the six, and the borrowing discipline two lessons from now is for exactly that. The next lesson is the kit confirming what you just recognized, from a direction you cannot fake.
