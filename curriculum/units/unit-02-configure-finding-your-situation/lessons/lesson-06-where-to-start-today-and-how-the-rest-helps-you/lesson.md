# Lesson 6 — Where to Start Today, and How the Rest Helps You

We have done all the structural work this unit is going to do. Before you close it, here is the one thing to do this week, and a short map of how everything else helps you.

Your first move. **Take the check, name your situation, run the first exercise in your kit.** That is one sentence and one piece of work; do not pad it. If the check says SaaS vendor dependency, run the build-versus-buy on one line item you have been wondering about. If it says legacy, write down the *three things you do not understand* about the system you inherited and pick the one a characterization test would protect first. If it says the AI gap, write the spec for the next ticket *before* the agent does — and check whether your spec and the agent's would have differed. The exercise is small on purpose. Everything else in your kit is a deeper version of it.

One move ahead of the exercise, because Lesson 1 of this unit opened on it and the configured kit will be so eager to start that the move can disappear underneath the work. **Write the aim down.** In the kit's origination surface, where the agent's first suggestion cannot quietly overwrite it. The carrot from the end of the last unit might fit cleanly inside the situation you just claimed; it might not. A builder who came in to insource an agency may have meant to free a budget for a project that matters to her, and the agency move only earns its weight if she actually points the savings at that project. A builder who came in to harden a prototype may have meant to keep a thing she loves alive, and the hardening only earns its weight if it is in service of the thing she loves rather than the thing investors are now expecting. Writing it down will not change the modules. It will change whether the modules do their job.

The whole unit in one line, and it is the one to keep when the lessons blur: **there is no single right path — there is the one built for where you actually are, and the freedom to borrow the rest.**

What is coming next, and what it does for you.

In Unit 3, your kit becomes the setup sheet for how you actually run a build with a crew of agents. The Agent Development Life Cycle — the day-to-day loop — is the same pipeline for every builder; the kit you just got configures which agents foreground at which gates, which gates harden for your situation, and how each skill is taught. You will recognize the modules from this unit, but as moves in a working build rather than entries in a kit. Unit 1 gave you the stance. Unit 2 located you. Unit 3 puts the stance and the location into a day of work.

In Unit 4, every trade you just made becomes a question you carry across a career. The long-game questions you noticed inside the six situations — *is my bench going dull, did the savings actually show up, what makes me different, how much of the judgment is still mine, can I still make that call, am I ready to be accountable in registers I did not previously owe* — these are not lesson ornaments. They are the readings Unit 4 takes off the work over years. Sit with that. The trades that look like one-time calls are the things you will be watching trend.

The two clocks return, because every unit ends here for a reason. **Becoming viable** is this situation, this kit, this week's first exercise. **Remaining viable** is the trade you just signed, watched across every situation you will move through. One stance. Two clocks. The same instrument. Now you know where it is pointed.

Install. Configure. Use. Watch.

You have done two of the four. The kit knows you. Unit 3 begins where it starts running against work that matters.
