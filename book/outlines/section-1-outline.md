# Section 1: Knowing Who Does What
 
*(working titles throughout)*
 
**The promise, stated before Chapter 1:** By the end of this section you'll know exactly what the AI is for, what *you* are for, and why that line is the most valuable thing you own right now.
 
---
 
## Chapter 1 — Why This Is the Best Time to Build (and Why It's on You)
 
*What you get: you'll see the opportunity clearly, and understand in plain terms why you're the one who wins it bigger or loses it all.*
 
- Why this work was worth building a life around (told briefly, in first person): a canvas, a discipline that travels, a life you can shape, a living, a world of people, a way to give some of it back. *Every one of those is in play right now.*
- What just changed: you can hand a goal to an AI and it builds real, working software — start to finish.
- Why that's thrilling: one person can now do what used to take a whole team.
- The catch in one sentence: the AI does the work, but you carry the risk — and every one of the things this career gave you can be *accelerated* by AI or *annihilated* by it. Which way it goes is on you.
- Why mistakes cost more now: building got fast and cheap, so aiming at the wrong thing wastes time you can't get back.
- What this book promises: win the opportunity *and* keep it.

## Chapter 2 — Two Kinds of Intelligence at the Table
 
*What you get: you'll stop competing with the AI and start directing it — because you'll finally know what each of you is actually for.*
 
### Artificial Intelligence — the machine
 
- What it's great at: breaks big goals into steps, weighs options, writes the code, decides when it's done.
- What it never does: get tired, have a bad day, lose focus. Same game at 3 a.m. as at 10.
- What it can't do, ever: care. It doesn't want your product to win, and isn't hurt when it loses.
- The limit that matters most: it decides *how*, never *why*. Every goal it chases came from someone else.
### Organic Intelligence — you
 
- What's only yours: you're alive. Your time runs out, your choices touch real people, and you answer for all of it.
- Why being alive is the whole point: there's a person for whom this matters — and that's where every "why" comes from.
- Your catch: you have weather. Bad sleep, money worry, a fight this morning — and none of it shows up as an error message.
*The takeaway: the smart move isn't you-vs-the-machine. It's giving each side the half it's built for.*

## Chapter 3 — The Two Jobs That Stay Yours Forever
 
*What you get: your worth won't vanish with the next model release, because these two jobs never transfer.*
 
- Don't draw the line at "things AI can't do" — that line moves every few months, and your value moves with it.
- Draw it here instead: you *start* the work and you *answer* for the work. The AI takes everything in between.
- Job 1, the front — **Origination:** picking the right thing to build. The first question of the book: *"Did I choose this, or did it choose me?"*
- Job 2, the back — **Accountability:** standing behind the result when it ships and something breaks.
- How both jobs show up while you work — three quick examples:
  - *Thinking:* the AI explores options; you decide what the real question is.
  - *Deciding:* the AI recommends; you're the one who lives with it.
  - *Creating:* the AI generates; you judge whether it's *right*, not just whether it *works*.

## Chapter 4 — The Catch: You're the Weak Link Now (and That's Fixable)
 
*What you get: you'll learn to spot when you're the problem — the quiet kind that sinks good work — before it costs you.*
 
- Why your bad days matter more now: the AI took all the small jobs, so you make fewer calls, and each one counts for far more.
- The scary part: failure used to be loud (broken code). Now it's quiet (a bad call that looks like a fine one).
- Worse: you can't feel it. A tired brain thinks it's sharp; a scared brain thinks it's being careful.
- The fix in one idea: treat yourself like a system you'd never run on broken hardware. **You are the infrastructure.**
- The five dials that move your condition, each tied to a real way work goes wrong: sleep, focus, mood, drive, money.
---
 
## Chapter 5 — How to Check Yourself (Since You Can't Trust the Mirror)
 
*What you get: an honest read on your own state — using the same trick you already use on every system but yourself.*
 
- The problem with "just check in with yourself": the broken part is the part doing the checking.
- The trick you already know: you don't ask a system how it feels — you measure it from the outside.
- What a good self-check looks like: the answer lives *outside your head*, you ask it *on a schedule* (not mid-decision), and it targets *your* specific weak spot.
- Dishonest vs. honest, side by side: *"Am I rested?"* vs. *"How many changes did I wave through last week without really reading them?"*
- Let the AI help: it doesn't share your bad week and won't accept "I'm fine" as data. Two warnings — it's not there to make you grind harder, and it can measure you but can never make you care.
---
 
## Chapter 6 — Staying in Shape and Building Your Own Alarm
 
*What you get: you'll keep your edge over a whole career, and set an alarm that goes off even on the days you'd rather ignore it.*
 
- Keep the muscle that matters: let the AI hold the trivia (lookups, facts); you keep the judgment about what's worth remembering.
- A few habits that protect the two jobs: write the *why* down before you prompt; let your logs double as both your memory and your scoreboard.
- The honest problem with self-checks: you have to remember to run them — and forgetting is the first thing a bad week takes.
- The fix — build a watchdog: like the drowsy-driver alert in your car. Your AI already sees every shortcut you take; wire it to warn you.
- The one part you can't hand off: the AI can sound the alarm; only you can pull over. *"Do I stop, or tell myself this time is different?"*
