# Section 1 — Install: Knowing Who Does What

*Working titles throughout. Drafted fresh against the preface, the kit concept manifest, and the maintenance trilogy. Section 1 still teaches the fundamentals — the opportunity, AI vs OI, the two jobs, condition, observability, upkeep — and **also** hands the reader to a kit moment at the close of each chapter, so the doctrine is taught AND made operable.*

---

**The promise stated before Chapter 1.**
By the end of this section, you will know what the AI is for, what *you* are for, and why the line between them is the most valuable thing you own right now. The kit will be running on your machine. You will have taken your first honest readings against it. And you will see — in your own data — what the rest of the book is teaching you to do.

**What this section hands the reader to.** Section 2: a running, instrumented, honest kit waiting for your situation.

---

## Chapter 1 — What's at Stake

*What you get: you see the opportunity clearly, you understand why the risk is yours alone, and you install the kit before the concepts settle.*

- Scene (first person, briefly): why this work was worth building a life around — a canvas, a discipline that travels, a life you can shape, a living, a world of people, a way to give some of it back. *Every one of those is in play right now.*
- What just changed: you can hand a goal to an AI and it builds real, working software — start to finish.
- Why that's thrilling: one person can now do what used to take a whole team.
- The catch in one sentence: the AI does the work, but **you** carry the risk — and every one of the things this career gave can be *accelerated* by AI or *annihilated* by it. Which way it goes is on you.
- Why mistakes cost more now: building got fast and cheap, so aiming at the wrong thing wastes time you can't get back.
- What this book promises: win the opportunity *and* keep it.
- **Kit moment:** the first build is the kit. Stand it up now, before the rest of this section makes it operable. The source tree downloads; the agents configure against your existing harness (Claude Code, Codex, both); a local telemetry surface starts listening. A 90-second readiness move — structural, not introspective — sets the zero-point so later readings are scored against *your* own normal. By the time you reach Chapter 2 you have an instrument running that knows nothing about you yet. That is the correct starting condition.

## Chapter 2 — Rethinking Intelligence

*What you get: you stop competing with the AI and start directing it — because you finally know what each of you is actually for.*

### Artificial Intelligence — the machine

- What it's great at: breaks big goals into steps, weighs options, writes the code, decides when it's done.
- What it never does: get tired, have a bad day, lose focus. Same game at 3 a.m. as at 10.
- What it can't do, ever: **care.** It doesn't want your product to win, and isn't hurt when it loses.
- The limit that matters most: it decides *how*, never *why*. Every goal it chases came from someone else.

### Organic Intelligence — you

- What's only yours: you're alive. Your time runs out. Your choices touch real people. You answer for all of it.
- Why being alive is the whole point: there's a party for whom this matters — and that's where every *why* comes from.
- Your catch: you have weather. Bad sleep, money worry, a fight this morning — and none of it shows up as an error message.

*Takeaway: the smart move isn't you-vs-the-machine. It's giving each side the half it's built for.*

- **Kit moment:** open the source tree. It isn't arranged by feature. It's arranged around this distinction — surfaces where the machine works, surfaces where you work, gates at the boundary. The geometry isn't a metaphor; it's a directory structure you can read.

## Chapter 3 — Establishing Division of Labor

*What you get: your worth won't vanish with the next model release, because these two jobs never transfer.*

- Don't draw the line at "things AI can't do" — that line moves every few months, and your value moves with it.
- Draw it here instead: you **start** the work and you **answer** for the work. The AI takes everything in between.
- **Job 1, the front — Origination.** Picking the right thing to build. The first question of the book: *"Did I choose this, or did it choose me?"* Origination atrophies from disuse, not from seizure.
- **Job 2, the back — Answerability.** Standing behind the result when it ships and something breaks. You answer in registers the machine answers in none of — economic, legal, ethical, the customer at 2 a.m.
- The two ends split each working moment, three places at once:
  - **Think** — the AI explores; you own the framing. (The caching example: latency may not be the real problem.)
  - **Decide** — the AI recommends; you live with it. (The REST-to-GraphQL example: a defensible plan whose risk is yours.)
  - **Create** — the AI generates; you judge whether it's *right*, not just whether it *works*. (The auth-module example: working is not the same as right.)
- The middle is the agent's column — but **you are not absent from it**. Steering is the in-flight motion that connects the two ends, and the interrupt key on every major coding agent is its most primitive form: a button that turns *wait for the artifact* into *stay in the work*.
- **Kit moment:** in the running kit, look at where each job lives — the origination surface where work is named and scoped; the answerability gates that fire before anything ships; the agent column between them. Then press the interrupt key on one of the running agents. The partial trajectory captures; you are now standing in the middle with the option to redirect. That move is the steering primitive every more elaborate discipline in the playbook is built on.

## Chapter 4 — Acknowledging Human Limitation

*What you get: you learn to spot when you're the problem — the quiet kind that sinks good work — before it costs you.*

- Why your bad days matter more now: the AI took all the small jobs, so you make fewer calls, and each one counts for far more. Variance got *concentrated*, not removed.
- The scary part: failure used to be loud (broken code). Now it's quiet (a bad call that looks like a fine one).
- Worse — **the drunk-driver problem**: you can't feel it. A tired brain reports it's sharp; a frightened brain reports it's being prudent. The impairment and your read on the impairment fail together.
- The fix in one idea: treat yourself like a system you'd never run on broken hardware. **You are the infrastructure.** Your condition is an engineering input, upstream of every origination and every override.
- The five dials that move your condition, each tied to a real way work goes wrong:
  - **Physical** — sleep and energy are the substrate the override runs on. Tired, you accept the plausible default — and the agent's entire output is plausible defaults.
  - **Cognitive** — focus and working memory are what let you hold a frame. Scattered, you get pulled into whatever the agent surfaced and optimize inside it.
  - **Emotional** — regulation is what lets you sit in the unfinished thing on purpose. Dysregulated, you rubber-stamp to be done with it.
  - **Spiritual** — your live connection to what's worth doing. Depleted here, you don't make worse choices; you stop choosing.
  - **Financial** — scarcity makes you care; desperation makes you stupid. Fear sounds like prudence.
- The dimensions don't stay in their lanes: bad sleep wrecks regulation; money fear wrecks sleep. The taxonomy isn't the point. The point is that every one of them lands on the same two ends.
- **Kit moment:** the kit's condition surface doesn't ask how you feel. It reads weather from your behavior — the same telemetry that's been running since Chapter 1 — because the gauge inside is the part that lies first. The mirror with a database behind it is the dashboard you don't build.

## Chapter 5 — Instrumenting the Human You

*What you get: an honest read on your own state — using the same trick you already use on every system but yourself.*

- The problem with "just check in with yourself": the broken part is the part doing the checking.
- The trick you already know: you don't ask a system how it feels — you instrument it. You make it emit signals you can read from outside, on a dashboard, over time, whether or not the system would think to report them itself.
- What a good question looks like, three properties:
  - **Its answer lives outside your head** — not a verdict you issue about yourself, but a log, a count, a record.
  - **You ask it off the decision** — on a schedule, away from the work; never in the moment, because that's when you most want a flattering answer.
  - **It targets your specific failure**, not the generic dimension — *Do I sleep enough?* is the wellness list; *what's the exact story I tell myself on the night I skip it?* is yours and no one else's.
- The five dimensions, as instruments (the dishonest vs. honest pairs):
  - **Physical** — Dishonest: *Am I rested?* Honest: *Over the last stretch, the times I accepted agent output without really reading it — what state was I in?*
  - **Cognitive** — Dishonest: *Am I focused?* Honest: *How often this week did the agent's first suggestion quietly become my direction?*
  - **Emotional** — Dishonest: *Am I calm?* Honest: *My recent accepts and rejects — were they about the work's merits, or about how sick of it I'd gotten?*
  - **Spiritual** — Dishonest: *Do I feel motivated?* Honest: *This period, did I start anything — or only respond to what showed up?*
  - **Financial** — set the tripwire in advance, while you can still think straight: *would I have said yes to this a year ago, at a different balance?*
- **Kit moment — the first reading.** Run a small piece of real work through the kit. While you work, the kit reads four signals — *accept-without-read*, *origination ratio*, *reframe-vs-accept*, *override-fired*. Each is taken from behavior, not opinion. The only path to a good number is doing the work the number measures. At the end, look at the first reading. It is small, possibly unflattering, entirely yours, and the first signal about yourself you have ever generated that you cannot have flattered into existence. You have not been told you are observable. You have observed yourself.

## Chapter 6 — Maintaining the System

*What you get: you keep your edge over a whole career, you build an alarm that goes off even on the days you'd rather ignore it, and you understand exactly what you can do to make the alarm lie.*

### Upkeep — the faculty you stopped maintaining

- You run yourself most days the way you'd never run the agent — context stuffed, no pruning, retrieval untended — and trust the output anyway.
- The mirror: the agent holds the *producible* half of memory (storage, lookup); you hold the *answerable* half (what's worth remembering, and what it means).
- The shape of upkeep (not a regimen — your specific failure is yours, not mine):
  - **Externalize the frame before you prompt** — the *why*, written down where working memory doesn't have to hold it and the agent's first suggestion can't quietly overwrite it.
  - **Make the store double as the dashboard** — one artifact, not two; the same record that lets you read your trend lets you retrieve the pattern.
  - **Keep the muscle you actually need in use** — reach for the prior decision before you ask the agent to surface it; hold the architecture in your head long enough to know whether the agent's plan fits.
  - **Protect the dense calls** — you make far fewer moves now and each carries more; single-thread the origination and the override the way you'd never bother to single-thread the middle.

### The watchdog — the page you can't be relied on to send yourself

- Maintenance gets you a faculty in better form. It doesn't get you the page. On the week that matters, you drift past the line without noticing — because noticing is the function that goes first.
- Your car solved this years ago: it watches how you steer, and when the pattern crosses a line it puts a coffee cup on the dash. The reading comes from behavior, not your say-so; it arrives whether or not you thought to wonder; it's a nudge, not a seizure.
- Build the same thing for the only system that never had one. The kit already has the telemetry. Set the tripwires when you can think straight — a run of accepts without an override, an origination ratio falling for two weeks, diffs merged faster than anyone could have read them — and let the agent page you when they trip. *"You've taken the last nine suggestions without changing one — want to actually look at this one?"*
- **The page is delegable; the heeding is not.** The agent can sound the alarm forever at a driver who keeps both hands on the wheel and drives into the dark anyway.

### The defeat posture — stated up front, not buried in settings

- **Defaults are honest.** Telemetry is on. The gameable settings are off.
- **Defeats are visible.** Disabling a signal, lowering a tripwire, deleting a record — each is itself a logged event in a record you cannot quietly purge.
- **The kit does not lock you out.** No DRM, no remote kill, no telemetry shipped off your machine. You own the data, the keys, and the consequences.
- **A defeated kit is still a workflow.** It may organize your agents and skills in a way you like. It is no longer the thing this playbook is for.
- The single question the kit cannot decide for you, waiting at the moment the page arrives: *do you pull over, or do you explain to yourself why this time is different?*

- **Kit moment:** the tripwires are set; the watchdog is wired; the defeat posture is named, not hidden. The page that the condition section said you lacked is now restored. The heeding has not been — it was always yours.

## Chapter 7 — Beginning the Practice

*What you get: one thing to do on your very next build, plus a map of everything ahead.*

- **Your first move (do it on build #1):** write the *why* down first, in the kit's origination surface. The kit already counts how many AI outputs you accept without really reading. The first move is yours; the rest is reading.
- The whole book in one line: **the skills that win you the opportunity are the same ones you keep sharp to hold onto it.**
- What's coming, named in **kit moments**, not topics:
  - **Section 2 — Configure.** Your situation picks the module set; persona sets develop-vs-borrow; wiring tunes the delivery. The kit outputs the configured modules, agents, skills, ADLC gates, and dashboard signals your kit will emphasize from then on. Same kit. Different mix of founder and engineering judgement.
  - **Section 3 — Use.** The configured kit becomes the day-to-day harness against real work. The ADLC fires across discovery, design, implementation, tests, security, infrastructure, deployment, documentation, and observability — staffed by agents, gated by you, with the steering you began in Chapter 3 happening continuously through the middle.
  - **Section 4 — Watch.** The same record the kit has been keeping becomes the longitudinal dashboard for the nine dimensions of long-term viability, read against your own baseline, never against a population norm. Same instrument, longer clock.
- Install. Configure. Use. Watch.
- **Kit moment — the handoff.** Install is done. The kit is honest, instrumented, and waiting. Section 2 is about handing it your situation.
