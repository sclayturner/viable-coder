- Section 1 was the same for everyone: the same two jobs, the same hard truth about who carries the risk.
- Here it splits. The two jobs don't change — but *which one you tend to drop* does, and so does the fix.
- There are six common spots people get stuck in right now. You're probably standing in one of them.
- So you won't get a one-size-fits-all plan. You'll get the kit built for your spot — plus permission to borrow from the others.

## Chapter 2 — The Check That Points You to Your Spot
 
*What you get: a short, honest read on where you stand — one that works even though you can't be trusted to grade yourself.*
 
- Same problem as the mirror in Section 1: the part of you doing the self-rating is the part most likely to flatter you.
- So this check doesn't ask how good you are. It reads what you actually did: the last thing you built, the last thing you shipped, the last thing you inherited.
- What comes out is a *starting point, not a label*. It points you to a situation and a kit — it does not stamp a grade on you, and you can re-take it as you change.
- It reads a second thing too: *how you work best* — fast or slow, lots of structure or little — so the same lesson can be taught your way.
- One warning: this is about how you *work*, not who you *are*. It's wiring, not a personality type — and it's there to fit the teaching to you, never to box you in.
---
 
## Chapter 3 — Build It, or Borrow It — Knowing Which
 
*What you get: the single call that saves you the most wasted effort — for each skill, whether to own it or just direct it.*
 
- Back to the two jobs: the front, where you *start* the work, and the back, where you *answer* for it.
- For every skill in front of you, one question: do you need to *own* this, or just *direct* it well enough to stay in charge?
- Build it for real when it's yours to live with. Borrow just enough when you'll always hand it off.
- The trap on each side: borrow when you should have built, and you go soft — you can't catch a problem you don't understand. Build when you should have borrowed, and you sink weeks into something you could have rented.
- The good news: borrowing now can turn into building later. Start with just enough, and grow into it as the skill becomes yours.
---
 
## Chapter 4 — The Moves Everyone Makes
 
*What you get: five moves that go in every kit, whoever you are — because they protect the two jobs no matter your situation.*
 
- **Did I choose this, or did it choose me?** — the first question of the book, now pointed at the work in front of you, before you build a thing.
- **You are the infrastructure.** — from Section 1, and it still holds: your condition shapes every call you make here.
- **Every fix is a trade.** — no move makes a problem vanish; it swaps it for a new one. The skill isn't escaping problems, it's picking the one worth having.
- **Stay able to check work you didn't write.** — the AI writes faster than anyone can read, so guard the one thing that doesn't speed up: your ability to actually review it.
- **Say what "right" means before you build.** — write the target down first, so you can tell whether the AI hit it or just gave you something that looks finished.
*The takeaway: these five are the floor. Your situation adds the rest on top.*

## Chapter 5 — Find Your Situation
 
*What you get: the six most common spots people are stuck in — find yours, and meet the kit built for it.*
 
### You've got people worth growing
 
- This is you if: you lead a team and want to grow your own builders instead of renting them.
- The risk: you hold both jobs today — the trick is handing the building skill to real people without it leaking away.
- What you do: teach so people *build* the skill, not so they lean on the AI and go soft — and spot the leaner early.
- The trade: you stop renting talent and start growing it — but now your team's sharpness is yours to keep up.
- The question you'll carry into the long game: *is my bench actually getting stronger, or quietly going dull?*
### You're renting what you could own
 
- This is you if: you pay every month for a tool that does something close to your core, and you wonder if you should build your own.
- The risk: someone else holds both jobs — you don't really control the thing you lean on.
- What you do: learn just enough to own and run it, and tell apart what's worth owning from what's worth renting.
- The trade: you swap a rent check for a thing you now have to maintain.
- The question you'll carry into the long game: *did the time and money I saved actually show up — or get eaten by the upkeep I took on?*
### Someone else builds your software
 
- This is you if: an agency or contractor writes your code, and you direct it but don't own it.
- The risk: the people who start your software, and answer for it, work for someone else.
- What you do: build enough know-how to direct the work credibly, then bring it back in-house without stalling out.
- The trade: you gain control — but once everyone has the same AI tools, you have to know what's still uniquely yours.
- The question you'll carry into the long game: *what makes me different from the next founder with the exact same stack — and does it still pay?*
### You inherited a mess you're scared to touch
 
- This is you if: you own a codebase you didn't write, can't fully see, and are afraid to change.
- The risk: you answer for it, but you can't hold a picture of how it works — because the picture was never there.
- What you do: build real understanding first (with the AI's help, and knowing where the AI actually slows you down), and put a safety net under it before you touch anything.
- The trade: you swap "a system I can't see" for "a system the AI helps me change" — but now some of the calls are the AI's, not yours.
- The question you'll carry into the long game: *how much of the judgment about this code is still mine?*
### Your team ships what it can't explain
 
- This is you if: AI is producing plenty of working code, but nobody can fully verify or explain it.
- The risk: the work flows, but the back job — standing behind it — isn't being held.
- What you do: close the gap by building real ownership — say what "right" means up front, and prove you can check what you shipped.
- The trade: you swap raw speed for the standing job of telling true from just-looks-true.
- The question you'll carry into the long game: *can I still make that call as the volume keeps climbing?*
### Your demo has to become a real product
 
- This is you if: you have a working prototype that now has to survive real users.
- The risk: you started it and it exists — but a demo never made you handle the hard, unglamorous half.
- What you do: harden the parts a prototype skips — security, reliability, real data — and know which to own and which to hand to specialists.
- The trade: you swap "it works on my machine" for answering to real people the moment it's live.
- The question you'll carry into the long game: *am I ready to be accountable in ways a prototype never asked of me?*
*The takeaway: each spot drops a different job and makes a different trade — but the trade is the part you'll be tracking for the rest of your career.*

## Chapter 6 — Your Kit Isn't a Cage
 
*What you get: the freedom to borrow — so the playbook fits your real life, not just the box you landed in.*
 
- Your situation hands you a starter kit: the moves built for your spot, ready to go.
- But real life rarely fits one box. The founder bringing work back in-house also inherits a mess; the team closing the AI gap also hits the demo-to-product wall.
- So borrow on purpose: when your reality calls for a move from another situation, pull it in — deliberately, not at random.
- And two moves belong to almost everyone: *checking work you didn't write*, and *saying what "right" means first*. If you take nothing else from outside your kit, take those.
