## Ch 1 — What the Day-to-Day Becomes (When a Crew of Agents Does the Middle)

**Promise:** You'll see how one person can now run the whole build — and why that doesn't change who's in charge.
**Doctrine drawn on:** [conceptual-playbook-outline.md](book/outlines/conceptual-playbook-outline.md) §Part 3.1; [doctrine/playbook-lexicon.md](doctrine/playbook-lexicon.md) (ADLC term claim); [doctrine/organic-intelligence.md](doctrine/organic-intelligence.md) (the two ends, ported from Section 1 as the constant across the new pipeline).
**Felt-need rhythm:** Scene (the thing being transformed has a name most people never learned) → Tension (each step existed for a reason — and most scrappy teams skipped most of it and paid later) → Distinction (the path stays; the team in the middle becomes a crew of agents — the **ADLC**) → Tool *(none yet — the kit lands in Ch 2 and runs in Ch 3)* → Return (the one line that carries the section).

- **1.1 The thing being transformed — most people were never taught its name.** Building software has always followed a set path: figure out what's needed, design it, build it, test it, ship it, keep it alive. That path is the **Software Development Life Cycle (SDLC)**, and whether or not you've heard the term, every app on your phone was built on some version of it. The one place in the book we assume zero prior knowledge and define a term in full.
- **1.2 Why each step existed.** Each step was there to kill a specific risk at the right moment — *so you didn't build the wrong thing, design yourself into a corner, or ship something broken to real people.* The path's shape is not arbitrary; it is a sequence of risk-killers.
- **1.3 The catch that shaped everything for forty years.** Doing those steps *well* took a whole team and took real time — which is exactly why scrappy startups skipped most of it and paid the interest later. The pipeline was a luxury good. The pre-2020 startup compromise — *cut the middle, ship fast, pay later* — was a forced choice, not a virtue.
- **1.4 What just changed.** AI agents can now do the work *inside* every one of those steps. The path stays; the team in the middle becomes a crew of agents. *Key phrase (lifted from outline Ch 1):* "**the same old SDLC, re-staffed by agents, producing everything a full human team once produced.**" We claim the name: the **Agent Development Life Cycle — the ADLC.** *(Naming aside on vendors using "Agentic Development Life Cycle" to mean *building agentic systems* stays out of body text; goes in an endnote.)*
- **1.5 The one line that carries the whole section.** *Key phrase (lifted, load-bearing):* "**the agents do the middle; you keep the two jobs from Section 1 — you *start* the work, and you *answer* for it.**" Origination at the front (G1) and answerability at the back (G7), now living at specific spots on a board. *Seed forward (Ch 4):* the two ends are jobs the machine can't take; what holds them through the middle is **steering** — the in-flight motion that reads the agent's reasoning as it runs and redirects when the trajectory drifts. Named here once; bloomed in Ch 4.

**Narrative anchor activated:** *The two gates* (the two ends from Section 1, now relocated to named spots on a board); *The prototype-to-production wall* (named as the constraint that *used* to be load-bearing); *Steering through the middle* (seeded at 1.5 — bloomed in Ch 4).
**Seeds forward:** Ch 2 (the kit becomes the setup sheet for the shop; the same substrate is what makes the middle readable); Ch 3 (the spine walked through nine gates; the human reads each column as it runs); Ch 4 (steering as doctrine — observability of the middle); Section 4 (the path runs; the long clock reads it, including steering depth).
**Editorial note:** Ch 1 is the one place in the book that defines an industry term in full. Recommend a small plain-language diagram of the SDLC beside the ADLC — same path, human team vs. agent crew (open decision #2 from the outline).

## Ch 2 — Your Shop: Where the Work Lives, the Team, and the House Rules

**Promise:** You'll know the handful of pieces that make up an AI-run build — and see that the kit you got in Section 2 is how yours gets set up.
**Doctrine drawn on:** [conceptual-playbook-outline.md](book/outlines/conceptual-playbook-outline.md) §Part 3.2; [module-skill-crosswalk.md](book/modules/module-skill-crosswalk.md) §The governing idea (one line); [doctrine/observability.md](doctrine/observability.md) (one place the work lives = the instrument).
**Felt-need rhythm:** Scene (the kit from Section 2 hasn't run yet — it has to land somewhere) → Tension (a "shop" sounds vague; what are the actual pieces?) → Distinction (four pieces, in plain terms — and Section 2's kit configures all four) → Tool (the four-piece shop) → Return (you're not coding anymore; you're running a shop).

- **2.1 One place the work lives.** The code, its full history, the to-do list, and the plan all sit in one place you can audit. *The to-do list **is** the plan — nothing important lives only in someone's head.* The single audit surface is what makes the rest of the shop honest; without it, the board can't read itself. *Doctrine carried (seed for Ch 4):* the same substrate is what makes the agent's work **readable as it runs**, not only when it lands — every reasoning step, every choice, every edit recorded in flight. This is the structural precondition for steering; one substrate, three readings off it (the human reads themselves, the human reads the agent in flight, the board reads the human after the fact — per `doctrine/steering.md`).
- **2.2 The team — the "subagents."** Instead of hiring a product manager, an architect, a tester, a security reviewer, you direct a set of specialized agents — each with one narrow job, each handing you back clean work to check. Plain term first; real word ("subagents") in parentheses once, mirroring how Section 1 handled "origination."
- **2.3 The house rules — the "Skills."** A short, reusable file that teaches an agent *how your shop does a thing* — how you write a spec, what "done" means to you, your security checklist — so every agent produces it *your* way, every time, instead of some generic way. Same plain-term-first treatment.
- **2.4 The thing that lets the AI see everything you know — the "MCP."** A universal adapter that lets your agents read across the scattered places your truth actually lives — support tickets, meeting notes, design files, chat threads — so the work is shaped by everything the company knows, not just what you happen to remember on a given afternoon. The MCP is what closes the gap between *what the agent can see* and *what the company knows.*
- **2.5 And here's where Section 2 plugs in.** Your situation already decided which of these agents step forward and which house rules they run. *Key phrase (lifted from outline Ch 2):* "**the kit you got wasn't advice to read and forget — it was the setup sheet for this shop.**" The crosswalk does the join — quietly, behind the scenes.
- **2.6 The governing one-liner that runs everything underneath.** *Key phrase (lifted from [module-skill-crosswalk.md](book/modules/module-skill-crosswalk.md)):* "**the ADLC pipeline is invariant; Section 2 configures only the interaction layer.**" The board is the same for everyone; the audience kit tunes the narrow band where AI meets the human — three knobs (which subagents foreground, which gate hardens, which register the Skill runs in).
- **2.7 The takeaway.** *Key phrase (lifted from outline Ch 2):* "**you're not really coding anymore — you're running a shop. A team, a rulebook, and one place it all lives.**"

**Narrative anchor activated:** *The dashboard / watchdog* (seeded — one place the work lives is the substrate the board will read in Ch 7); *Steering through the middle* (seeded at 2.1 — the substrate that enables it is named here, the move itself blooms in Ch 4); *Right-sized routing* (seeded indirectly at 2.2/2.3 — the team and the rulebook are the seam where the routing policy will be configured; the doctrine itself blooms in Ch 5).
**Seeds forward:** Ch 3 (the same four pieces run through nine gates); Ch 4 (the substrate at 2.1 is what makes observability-of-the-middle possible — steering doctrine lands there); Ch 5 (the team and rulebook from 2.2/2.3 are where routing policy is configured — right-sized routing doctrine lands there); Ch 6 (the house rules are the knob the knife-edge sits under — and the primary steering instrument); Ch 7 (one place the work lives is also what makes the board possible at three scales).
**Open structural question:** The outline introduces *subagents*, *Skills*, and *MCP* in plain terms with the real word in parentheses once, mirroring "origination" from Section 1. Outline open decision #3 asks whether to keep them named, hide them entirely (cleaner, less capable), or teach them as named tools (more capable, less plain). The granular treats them as the Section 1 origination pattern; confirm at draft time.

## Ch 3 — One Job, Start to Finish

**Promise:** You'll watch a single piece of work move through every step — and see exactly where your hands are and where the agents' are.
**Doctrine drawn on:** [section-3-chapter-3-matrix.md](book/outlines/section-3-chapter-3-matrix.md) (the spine and gate-by-gate detail); [doctrine/organic-intelligence.md](doctrine/organic-intelligence.md) (the two ends as the gates at the ends); [doctrine/reader-orientation-pattern.md](doctrine/reader-orientation-pattern.md) (Scene→Tension→Distinction→Tool→Return at chapter level and at gate level).
**Felt-need rhythm:** Scene (one ticket moves left to right across a board with ten columns; every column has an agent in it; the human stands between the columns) → Tension (if every column is staffed, what is the human still doing here?) → Distinction (the agent fills the column; the human owns the *gate between* the columns — two of those gates are the two jobs from Section 1) → Tool (the gate-by-gate rhythm — see the matrix) → Return (the ticket is shipped and observed; the reader has felt the rhythm).

This chapter's sub-beat granular *is* the chapter-3 matrix. The matrix carries one card per gate — universal beat, scene anchor, distinction, producing subagent, register, configurable column (per-audience changes), trailing signal, substitution risk, exercise slot. Sub-beats below are at chapter-rhythm level only; see the matrix for the per-gate granular.

- **3.1 The opening scene.** *Key phrase (proposed, from matrix):* "**one piece of work moves left to right across a board with ten columns. Every column has an agent in it. You're standing between the columns.**" The image the rest of the chapter walks through.
- **3.2 The chapter's question.** If every column is staffed, what is the human still doing here? The tension that the gate-by-gate walk exists to answer.
- **3.3 The chapter's answer in one sentence.** *Key phrase (lifted from outline Ch 3):* "**the agent fills the column; you own the gate.**" Stated up front; demonstrated nine times; returned to in 3.6. *Held implicitly through the chapter (named explicitly in Ch 4):* you don't only own the gate at the column's *boundary* — you hold it *across the column* by reading the agent's reasoning in flight. The gate is the boundary; steering is what fills the space between the boundaries. The chapter walks the rhythm; Ch 4 names the in-flight motion.
- **3.4 The walk — nine gates.** Each gate is one universal beat in the body, configured by the matrix's per-audience column. *(Detail and per-gate cards live in the matrix; do not duplicate here.)*
  - G1 **Start it** *(originate; the front of the two jobs)*
  - G2 **Spell it out** *(PRD / specify; **self-spec** is the load-bearing substitution to police)*
  - G3 **Break it down** *(`Ready`; the second face of Can-vs-Should)*
  - G4 **Design it** *(architecture; producible options, answerable choice)*
  - G5 **Build it** *(implement; **genuinely the agent's column** — the chapter deliberately doesn't pull back from this)*
  - G6 **Test it, secure it, wire it up** *(the Iron Triangle dissolves — luxuries become the floor; LSD inverts the gate, characterization tests run before implement)*
  - G7 **Review it** *(answerable; the back of the two jobs; rubber-stamp is the most dangerous gate on the board for everyone)*
  - G8 **Write it down** *(documentation; **free isn't the same as honest**)*
  - G9 **Ship it, and watch it** *(deploy & observe; the build talks back, and quietly observes you too — bridge to Ch 7)*
- **3.5 The loop.** The board doesn't end; it cycles. What you learn out in the world feeds the next *start it.* The same board has been quietly emitting the signals Section 4 will read on the long clock. *(Bridge to Ch 7.)*
- **3.6 The takeaway.** *Key phrase (lifted from outline Ch 3):* "**the agent fills the column; you own the gate. That rhythm is the whole job.**"

**Narrative anchor activated:** *The two gates* (full bloom — the two ends from Section 1 now live as G1 and G7 on a named board); *The prototype-to-production wall* (returns at G6 and G9 — the formerly-skipped work, now standard from day one).
**Seeds forward:** Ch 4 (G1 and G7 named as the two that *never* transfer — the doctrinal claim the matrix already enforces structurally); Ch 5 (G5 specifically — *which model runs this column* is the routing-doctrine question, and the matrix's per-gate column is also where the per-gate routing policy will land); Ch 6 (G5/G6/G7 are where register switches; the configurable column already books it); Ch 7 (the trailing signals named in 3.4 are what the board reads about *you*).

## Ch 4 — What You Can't Hand Off (the Gates, and the Steering That Makes Them Holdable)

*Pairs with Ch 5 ("What You Can't Leave to Default Settings"). Both chapters refuse one of the agent's defaults: Ch 4 refuses the default of opacity in the middle; Ch 5 refuses the default of frontier-on-every-task. Same structural discipline at two axes.*

**Promise:** You'll know the exact moments the work is yours alone — *and* the in-flight move that keeps those moments from collapsing into ceremony — and why skipping either is the quiet way good builds go bad.
**Doctrine drawn on:** [doctrine/organic-intelligence.md](doctrine/organic-intelligence.md) (the two ends as doctrine, not just chapter geometry; the in-flight motion of holding them); [doctrine/steering.md](doctrine/steering.md) (steering and observability of the middle — **canonical** for this chapter's load-bearing addition); [doctrine/observability.md](doctrine/observability.md) (instrument-don't-introspect — the principle the work-observability sibling ports to a second scale); [doctrine/human-condition.md](doctrine/human-condition.md) (the answerable end as condition-load, not procedure); [doctrine/voice/lived-answerability.md](doctrine/voice/lived-answerability.md) (the 2 a.m. test); [conceptual-playbook-outline.md](book/outlines/conceptual-playbook-outline.md) §Part 3.4.
**Felt-need rhythm:** Scene (two of the nine gates aren't like the others) → Tension (every other gate had an agent in it — these two don't, and the chapter has to say why honestly *and* what makes them holdable when the agent's column runs at machine pace) → Distinction (these are the two jobs from Section 1, now living at specific spots on the board — and the machine does neither of the things they require; what makes them holdable is the in-flight motion of **steering**, with **observability of the middle** as its structural precondition) → Tool (the rubber-stamp diagnostic — the two ways each gate goes bad when steering is absent; the produce-and-explain configuration that makes the middle observable) → Return (skipping the gates *or* skipping the steering is the quiet way good builds go bad).

- **4.1 Two of those steps aren't like the others.** G1 (originate — deciding it's worth building, saying it's ready) and G7 (answer — reviewing what shipped, approving the release). *Key phrase (lifted from outline Ch 4):* "**these are the two jobs from Section 1, now living at specific spots on the board.**"
- **4.2 Why these two can't transfer.** Everything between them, the agents can do. These two, they can't — *because they're about wanting the thing to be right and being the one who gets the call at 2 a.m., and the machine does neither.* The honest version of the limit, not a procedural one: the gates demand a *stake in the outcome* that the machine cannot supply.
- **4.3 The good news (the Iron Triangle dissolves).** Because the agents absorb the *time* the middle used to cost, the things only big, late-stage companies could once afford — real tests, proper security, solid infrastructure, docs that stay true — are now standard from day one. *Key phrase (proposed, from outline Ch 4):* "**the luxuries became the floor.**" The pre-2020 startup compromise (cut the middle, pay later) is structurally over for *new* work. *Carry forward to 4.5:* the same compression of the middle that delivers the luxuries-as-floor also makes the middle opaque at machine pace; the next beat names the structural answer.
- **4.4 The honest catch (where the headline doesn't hold).** *"Time is no longer the constraint"* holds best for *new* work built from scratch. For the tangled system you inherited and can't fully see — your Section 2 **Legacy Software Debt** situation, if that's you — the agent can speed the typing but not the *understanding*, and the understanding was always the slow part. The chapter deliberately punctures its own headline inside the section that most celebrates it. *(Open decision #4 from the outline: confirm we want the qualification here.)*
- **4.5 What makes the two gates holdable — observability of the middle and steering.** The two ends are jobs the machine can't take; that is doctrine. What the chapter has to also say honestly is *what makes those two jobs holdable*, because no human can stand behind a fluent 200-line PR they didn't watch get written at the pace an agent writes it. The fix isn't *be more careful at G7*; the fix is structural. **The agent's reasoning has to be readable while the work runs, not summarized after.** That readability is what [doctrine/steering.md](doctrine/steering.md) calls **observability of the middle** — the same instrument-don't-introspect principle from Section 1 Ch 5 ([doctrine/observability.md](doctrine/observability.md)), now ported to a second scale: the human reads the *agent* in flight, not only the *self*. With it, the human can **steer** — read the agent's reasoning as it produces, redirect when the trajectory drifts off the *why-now*, press the interrupt key when the path turns into one they can't stand behind. *Key phrases (proposed; canonical in [doctrine/steering.md](doctrine/steering.md)):* "**steering is the in-flight form of holding the two jobs — not a third job, but what holding them looks like while the work is running**"; "**opacity in the middle is now a configuration choice; rubber-stamp at the back is what the wrong choice produces.**" *Where it lands in the chapter's geometry:* between the chapter's celebratory beats (4.3–4.4) and the failure-mode beats (4.6–4.7). The honest version of the *good news* is that the middle is now observable in flight in ways no human team's middle ever was — *if* the practitioner configures it that way.
- **4.6 The trap at G1 — origination by drift, recast as steering's absence at the front.** Waving a piece of work to *ready* because the agent suggested it. *Key phrase (proposed, from matrix G1 substitution risk):* "**you didn't choose it — it chose you.**" The first question of the book, failed at a gate. Feels like progress; is the agent's hand on your end. *What 4.5 adds:* this is what origination looks like without steering. The agent surfaces the next ticket; nothing forces the human to read the agent's reasoning for *why this one*; the ticket enters the board. The structural fix is the steering one: the *why-now* is human-authored, on the record, before the gate opens.
- **4.7 The trap at G7 — review by rubber-stamp, recast as steering's absence at the back.** Waving a finished build through review without really reading it. Automation complacency, structurally instrumented in Ch 7, named here as the quiet failure mode. *Key phrase (proposed, from matrix G7 substitution risk):* "**the single most dangerous gate on the board for everyone.**" *What 4.5 adds:* rubber-stamp is not a character failure; it is the inevitable outcome of an *opaque middle* running faster than human review capacity. The only honest review at G7 is one informed by readings taken *across the middle*. The structural fix is the steering one: the produce-and-explain register configured at Ch 6 makes the reasoning legible in flight, so the gate at the back is informed by what happened across the column, not by the artifact alone.
- **4.8 Why both traps feel like progress.** Both feel like progress. Both are you quietly handing off a job that was yours to keep — and, more specifically, handing off the *in-flight motion* of steering that holds the two jobs through the middle. The emotional center of the chapter — the failure mode does not announce itself, because steering's absence does not produce an artifact you can point at; it produces a long, fluent, plausible artifact that you signed without reading. The chapter ends honest about that.

**Narrative anchor activated:** *The two gates* (named as doctrine — the two from Section 1 are now permanent fixtures of the board, not just a section's geometry); *Steering through the middle* (full bloom — named as doctrine; canonical at `doctrine/steering.md`; recast both failure modes as steering's absence); *The prototype-to-production wall* (named, then qualified — the wall did fall, except where it didn't); *The dashboard / watchdog* (recast — the dashboard is now operating at three scales, with steering as the in-flight scale).
**Seeds forward:** Ch 5 (the *sibling discipline* — what you can't leave to default settings; the same explicit-structural-discipline shape applied to the cost axis); Ch 6 (the register knob is what *protects* G1 and G7 — produce-and-explain is the primary steering instrument that makes the middle observable in flight); Ch 7 (rubber-stamp gets structurally instrumented — *the work-system is the instrument* — and the signals it reads are mostly steering signals, with the Ch 5 routing signals also landing here); Section 4 (Vocational and Identity dimensions both bloom from G1 and G7 held over years; declining reframe-vs-accept ratio is a long-clock reading of *steering depth*).
**Open structural question:** Outline open decision #4 asks whether to qualify the big claim *here*, in the section that most celebrates it, rather than only in back-matter. The granular places the puncture at 4.4 deliberately — the same place the audience research plan flags the LSD exception. Confirm at draft time. *Additional question raised by 4.5:* the chapter now has eight beats rather than seven; confirm the addition is load-bearing enough to justify the chapter expansion. Recommendation from the granular: yes — without 4.5 the chapter says *these gates can never transfer* and *here are two ways they go bad* but doesn't say *what makes them holdable at all.* The chapter that names the limit owes the chapter that names the answer; not splitting them across chapters is the cleaner spine.
**Clay-experience anchor slot:** *A build where you waved a review through and paid for it.* The chapter's load-bearing scene at G7 — now structurally a *steering-absence* scene, which sharpens it. Left as a named slot, not written. *(Outline open decision #6.)*

## Ch 5 — What You Can't Leave to Default Settings

**Promise:** You'll know the second default the agent has — frontier-on-every-task — and the explicit structural discipline (classify, route, right-size) that meets it, so your token bill, your rate-limit exposure, and your provider dependency reflect choices you actually made instead of defaults you never noticed.
**Doctrine drawn on:** [doctrine/right-sized-models.md](doctrine/right-sized-models.md) (**canonical** for this chapter); [doctrine/steering.md](doctrine/steering.md) (sibling — same shape, different axis); [doctrine/organic-intelligence.md](doctrine/organic-intelligence.md) (the human bears the cost, the machine bears none); [doctrine/playbook-lexicon.md](doctrine/playbook-lexicon.md) §*relocation lens* / *every escape is a trade* / *trade up to a dependency worth holding* (the routing posture is the dependency stance, explicit); [conceptual-playbook-outline.md](book/outlines/conceptual-playbook-outline.md) §Part 3 (new beat, since this chapter did not exist in the prior conceptual outline).
**Felt-need rhythm:** Scene (Ch 4 named one silent default — opacity in the middle; the chapter that follows it owes the second one) → Tension (the harness reaches for the frontier model on every task by default, and the bill, the rate-limit exposure, and the provider dependency are all silently climbing while the artifacts read clean) → Distinction (the discipline that meets the default is two primitives — classifier and router — plus the open-source / self-hosted posture as a first-class option) → Tool (the classify-and-route configuration; the policy-override-rule pattern; the cost-line-as-its-own-dashboard discipline; the locally-served-model setup pattern) → Return (the agent will pick the most expensive option on your behalf, every time, unless you configured otherwise).

- **5.1 The bridge from Ch 4 — there's a second silent default.** *Key phrase (proposed; lifted from [doctrine/right-sized-models.md](doctrine/right-sized-models.md)):* "**the agent has two defaults that, left running, hollow the practice; Ch 4 named the first; this chapter names the second.**" Steering refuses opacity; right-sized routing refuses frontier-on-every-task. Both feel like progress while they fail. Both are mitigated by explicit structural policy, not exhortation.
- **5.2 The default the discipline refuses.** Most AI coding harnesses today do not classify and route by default. They let one model — usually the frontier model the harness ships with — run every subagent, in every column, on every task, regardless of complexity. *Key phrase (proposed):* "**a boilerplate refactor that needed a 70B open-source model got handed to a frontier reasoning model; the harness produced fluent results; nobody noticed until the bill arrived.**" The lived-economics version of the doctrinal claim — the chapter has to make the default *feel* present before naming its mitigation.
- **5.3 The first primitive — the classifier.** A small, fast model whose only job is to *read the complexity of an incoming task* and emit a routing decision. Plain term first; technical name in parentheses once, mirroring how Ch 2 handled *subagents / Skills / MCP. Reader-facing:* "the thing that decides which model gets this one." *Key insight (from doctrine):* the classifier runs cheap because reading complexity is not what frontier capability is for.
- **5.4 The second primitive — the router.** The component that takes the classifier's recommendation and assigns the work to a model in your configured fleet — *and* the seam where your explicit *policy* lives. The router is where the practitioner's overrides bind: "for this kind of work, even if the classifier says mid-tier is fine, escalate to frontier" (stakes) or "for this kind of work, even if the classifier says frontier is warranted, run mid-tier" (cost). *Reader-facing:* "the thing that hands the task to the right-sized model — under rules you wrote."
- **5.5 The third piece — open-source / self-hosted as a first-class option.** The classifier-router architecture is hollow if the router can only choose between the same vendor's tiers. Right-sizing means a wider stack — open-source code models for boilerplate and deterministic transforms, mid-size open-source for summarization and extraction, self-hosted models for internal-only tooling, locally-served models for high-volume background work, and the frontier model for the work that genuinely needs it. *Key phrase (lifted from doctrine):* "**the open-source / self-hosted option is doing two jobs at once: cost control AND the *concentration-of-power* register from the Section 4 Ethical dimension. Same primitive, two registers.**" Treating it as cost-only undersells the doctrine.
- **5.6 The honest catch (parallel to Ch 4's Iron-Triangle qualification).** Right-sizing only works where the classifier can read complexity reliably. For genuinely judgment-dense, open-ended, or first-of-its-kind work, the classifier will under-read the task; frontier capability is still the right call. *Key phrase (proposed):* "**the discipline isn't 'always go cheap'; it is 'stop defaulting upward.'**" The chapter punctures its own headline the same way Ch 4 punctures the Iron-Triangle headline at 4.4 — in the section that most exposes the qualification. *Substitution risk mirror:* a classifier that quietly down-routes work it didn't understand has stepped onto the practitioner's side of the line, the same way the *self-spec* agent does at G2.
- **5.7 The trap — default escalation, with a face.** Waving a piece of work onto the frontier model because the harness ships that way. The economic mirror of *origination by drift* (Ch 4.6) and *review by rubber-stamp* (Ch 4.7). *Key phrase (lifted from doctrine):* "**you didn't choose the policy; the policy chose you.**" The same shape as the previous chapter's traps. Feels like progress. Hollows the practice the same way.
- **5.8 Tips and tricks the harness doesn't ship with.** *(Chapter-depth detail; lives in body text, not at this outline altitude.)* Concrete moves for practitioners whose harness has no classifier-router built in: manual routing rules per Skill, model-fleet configuration patterns, the policy-override rule pattern, the locally-served-model setup pattern, the cost-line-as-its-own-dashboard discipline. *(Open structural question: how much of this should ship in body text vs. a [book/exercises/](book/exercises/) or [book/modules/](book/modules/) supplement — the chapter has the highest *operational* density of any in the section, and the granular surfaces this but does not resolve it. ## Ch 6 — Teaching the AI Your Way (and the Knife-Edge Under It)
